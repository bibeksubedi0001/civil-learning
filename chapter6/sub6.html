Section 6.6

Backpropagation Demystified

The algorithm that lets a network distribute blame for its mistakes backwards through every weight, exactly like tracing failure responsibility back through a structural load path.

~22 min read Intermediate 2 interactive demos Chain rule heavy
Scroll to begin

The Blame Assignment Problem

In Section 6.5 we discovered that learning means rolling downhill on a loss surface. Gradient descent tells us which direction to nudge each weight — but a deep network has thousands or millions of weights, organised in stacked layers. The question that stalled neural networks for two decades was deceptively simple:

When the output is wrong, how much of the blame belongs to a weight buried four layers deep, on the third neuron from the left?

For a single-layer perceptron the answer is obvious — each weight directly touches the output. But once you stack hidden layers, the contribution of an early weight is mediated by every layer that follows it. The error you observe at the top is the compound result of every transformation in between.

CE Analogy — Forensic Load Path Analysis

Picture a steel truss bridge that just deflected past its serviceability limit under a load test. The midspan dropped 40 mm instead of the predicted 25 mm. Which member is most responsible?

You don't blame every member equally. You trace the load path backwards: the bottom chord carried tension proportional to its influence coefficient, the diagonals carried shear proportional to theirs, and so on. A member with a high sensitivity coefficient — one whose stiffness change would noticeably shift the deflection — deserves more of the blame than a member that barely participates.

Backpropagation is exactly this trace. The "sensitivity coefficient" of each weight is its partial derivative with respect to the loss — how much the loss would change if that one weight wiggled. The chain rule of calculus gives us a recursive way to compute every one of them in a single sweep from output to input.

Historical context. The credit-assignment problem is so deep that Marvin Minsky's 1969 book Perceptrons effectively froze neural-network research for over a decade. The thaw came when Paul Werbos (1974 PhD thesis) and later Rumelhart, Hinton & Williams (1986 Nature paper) showed that the chain rule, applied systematically, solves blame assignment in time linear in the number of edges. That paper is arguably the single most consequential algorithm in modern AI.

Computational Graphs

Before we touch the chain rule, we need a clean mental model of what a neural network is. Forget "layers of neurons" for a moment. Internally, a network is a computational graph — a directed acyclic graph where every node is a single mathematical operation, and every edge carries a number forward.

Consider the absurdly tiny "network" $$L = (w_2 \cdot \sigma(w_1 \cdot x + b_1) + b_2 - y)^2$$ We can rewrite it as a graph of primitive operations:

  • z1 = w1 · x
  • a1 = z1 + b1
  • h = σ(a1)
  • z2 = w2 · h
  • ŷ = z2 + b2
  • e = ŷ − y
  • L = e2

Each line is an atomic operation whose derivative we already know from first-year calculus. The trick of backprop is that derivatives compose along edges — if you know the derivative of every local operation, you can stitch them together to get the derivative of the whole graph.

Two passes, one graph. The same graph is traversed twice. Forward pass: compute every node's value left-to-right. Backward pass: compute every node's gradient right-to-left. Forward gives you the prediction; backward gives you the recipe for improvement.

The Chain Rule — Backbone of Backprop

From calculus, if $y = f(u)$ and $u = g(x)$, then $$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx}$$ Read it aloud: "the sensitivity of y to x equals the sensitivity of y to u, multiplied by the sensitivity of u to x." That single identity, applied recursively across a graph, is backpropagation. There is no other secret ingredient.

Stack three operations $y = f(g(h(x)))$. The chain rule extends: $$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{df} \cdot \frac{df}{dg} \cdot \frac{dg}{dh} \cdot \frac{dh}{dx}$$ Each factor is the local derivative of one operation. To compute the gradient at the bottom, multiply local derivatives along the path. In a general DAG with branching, you sum the contributions over all paths — a fact that falls out of the multivariable chain rule.

CE Analogy — Gear Train Sensitivity

A gear train of three wheels with ratios 2:1, 3:1, 4:1 produces an overall ratio of $2\times3\times4 = 24$. If you wiggle the input by 1°, the output rotates 24°. The chain rule is exactly the same idea: each "gear" is a layer's local derivative, and the total sensitivity is the product as the signal traces through.

The danger: if any local derivative is much smaller than 1, the chain product collapses towards zero (vanishing gradient). If any is much greater than 1, it blows up (exploding gradient). We will see this in Demo 2.

chain_rule_demo.py — one-line backprop on a tiny scalar net 
import math
x, y_true = 2.0, 1.0
w1, b1, w2, b2 = 0.5, 0.1, -0.8, 0.2

# forward pass
z1 = w1 * x + b1
h  = 1 / (1 + math.exp(-z1))     # sigmoid
z2 = w2 * h + b2
y  = z2                          # linear output
L  = (y - y_true) ** 2

# backward pass — pure chain rule
dL_dy   = 2 * (y - y_true)
dL_dz2  = dL_dy * 1
dL_dw2  = dL_dz2 * h
dL_db2  = dL_dz2
dL_dh   = dL_dz2 * w2
dL_dz1  = dL_dh  * h * (1 - h)   # sigmoid'
dL_dw1  = dL_dz1 * x
dL_db1  = dL_dz1

Backprop Step-by-Step on a 2–2–1 Network

Let us anchor the algebra in a concrete network with two inputs, two hidden neurons (sigmoid), and one linear output. We will walk through one full forward + backward pass with real numbers, then formalise the update rule.

Setup

  • Input $\mathbf{x} = (1.0,\ 0.5)$, target $y = 1.0$
  • Hidden weights $W^{(1)} = \begin{pmatrix}0.4 & -0.2\\ 0.3 & 0.7\end{pmatrix}$, biases $\mathbf{b}^{(1)} = (0.1,\ -0.1)$
  • Output weights $\mathbf{w}^{(2)} = (0.6,\ -0.5)$, bias $b^{(2)} = 0.2$
  • Hidden activation: sigmoid $\sigma(z)$. Output: linear. Loss: $\tfrac{1}{2}(\hat{y}-y)^2$.

Forward pass

Hidden pre-activations: $z^{(1)}_1 = 0.4(1.0) + (-0.2)(0.5) + 0.1 = 0.40$, $z^{(1)}_2 = 0.3(1.0) + 0.7(0.5) - 0.1 = 0.55$.

Hidden activations: $h_1 = \sigma(0.40) \approx 0.599$, $h_2 = \sigma(0.55) \approx 0.634$.

Output: $\hat{y} = 0.6(0.599) + (-0.5)(0.634) + 0.2 \approx 0.242$. Loss: $L = \tfrac{1}{2}(0.242 - 1)^2 \approx 0.287$.

Backward pass

Output error signal (output is linear, so $\sigma'(z^{(2)}) = 1$):

$$\delta^{(2)} = \hat{y} - y = -0.758$$

Hidden error signal — propagate through $\mathbf{w}^{(2)}$ and multiply by $\sigma'(z^{(1)}_j) = h_j(1-h_j)$:

$$\delta^{(1)}_j = w^{(2)}_j \cdot \delta^{(2)} \cdot h_j(1-h_j)$$

$\delta^{(1)}_1 = 0.6 \cdot (-0.758) \cdot 0.599 \cdot 0.401 \approx -0.109$
$\delta^{(1)}_2 = -0.5 \cdot (-0.758) \cdot 0.634 \cdot 0.366 \approx 0.088$

Weight gradients

For every weight, $\dfrac{\partial L}{\partial W_{ij}} = a_i \cdot \delta_j$ where $a_i$ is the activation feeding into the weight:

  • $\partial L/\partial w^{(2)}_1 = h_1 \cdot \delta^{(2)} \approx 0.599 \cdot (-0.758) = -0.454$
  • $\partial L/\partial w^{(2)}_2 = h_2 \cdot \delta^{(2)} \approx 0.634 \cdot (-0.758) = -0.481$
  • $\partial L/\partial W^{(1)}_{11} = x_1 \cdot \delta^{(1)}_1 = 1.0 \cdot (-0.109) = -0.109$
  • $\partial L/\partial W^{(1)}_{12} = x_2 \cdot \delta^{(1)}_1 = 0.5 \cdot (-0.109) = -0.055$
  • $\partial L/\partial W^{(1)}_{21} = x_1 \cdot \delta^{(1)}_2 = 1.0 \cdot 0.088 = 0.088$
  • $\partial L/\partial W^{(1)}_{22} = x_2 \cdot \delta^{(1)}_2 = 0.5 \cdot 0.088 = 0.044$

Update rule

With learning rate $\eta = 0.1$, every weight steps opposite to its gradient:

$$\Delta W = -\eta \cdot \frac{\partial L}{\partial W}, \qquad W \leftarrow W + \Delta W$$

Example: $w^{(2)}_1$ moves from $0.6$ to $0.6 - 0.1(-0.454) = 0.645$. The output weight grows because the network under-predicted and $h_1$ contributed positively. This is blame assignment in action.

Vanishing & Exploding Gradients

Recall the gear-train analogy: chain-rule gradients are products of local derivatives. In a deep network with $L$ layers, the gradient at the bottom is roughly $$\frac{\partial L}{\partial W^{(1)}} \;\sim\; \prod_{\ell=2}^{L} W^{(\ell)} \cdot \sigma'(z^{(\ell)})$$ Two pathologies follow immediately.

Vanishing gradients

The sigmoid derivative $\sigma'(z) = \sigma(z)(1-\sigma(z))$ has a maximum of $0.25$ at $z = 0$. Multiply twenty of those together and you get $0.25^{20} \approx 10^{-12}$. The gradient that reaches the first layer is essentially noise — the early layers stop learning. This is precisely what crippled deep sigmoid networks before 2010.

Exploding gradients

The opposite: if the weight matrices have spectral norm $> 1$, the product blows up exponentially. Updates become huge, training diverges, and you see NaN in your loss. Recurrent networks unrolled over long sequences are especially vulnerable.

Modern remedies (preview)

  • ReLU activations — derivative is exactly $1$ on the active half, breaking the shrinkage.
  • Careful initialisation — He / Xavier scaling keeps activations and gradients at unit variance through the depth.
  • Batch normalisation — rescales pre-activations every layer so $\sigma'$ stays in its useful regime.
  • Residual connections — ResNets add a "skip" that lets gradient bypass deep stacks entirely. The gradient highway.
  • Gradient clipping — for exploding gradients, just cap the norm.

You Don't Write Backprop — The Framework Does

Here is the practical good news: in PyTorch, TensorFlow, JAX or any modern framework, you never code the backward pass. You write only the forward computation. The framework records every operation you perform on a tracked tensor into a computational graph, then traverses it in reverse using known local derivatives. This is called automatic differentiation (autograd, autodiff).

The contrast with finite-difference numerical gradients is enormous. For $N$ parameters, finite differences need $O(N)$ forward passes; reverse-mode autograd needs one forward and one backward, independent of $N$. That is why training a billion-parameter model is even computationally conceivable.

autograd_demo.py — PyTorch does the chain rule for you 
import torch

# Tensors that track gradients
w1 = torch.tensor(0.5, requires_grad=True)
w2 = torch.tensor(-0.8, requires_grad=True)
x  = torch.tensor(2.0)
y_true = torch.tensor(1.0)

# Forward — just write the math.
h = torch.sigmoid(w1 * x + 0.1)
y_hat = w2 * h + 0.2
loss = (y_hat - y_true) ** 2

# One line. Whole graph differentiated.
loss.backward()

print(w1.grad, w2.grad)  # gradients filled in automatically
Why learn the manual derivation then? Because every bug you will ever hit — exploding loss, dead neurons, frozen layers — is diagnosed by reasoning about gradient flow. The framework computes; you must still understand.

A Brief History of an Algorithm That Changed Everything

  • 1960s — Kelley, Bryson, Dreyfus develop reverse-mode automatic differentiation in the optimal-control community. Nobody connects it to neural networks.
  • 1974 — Paul Werbos's Harvard PhD thesis describes applying reverse-mode AD to multi-layer neural networks. It is largely overlooked.
  • 1982 — Werbos publishes the result more broadly. Still no critical mass.
  • 1986 — Rumelhart, Hinton & Williams publish Learning representations by back-propagating errors in Nature. This is the moment the field re-ignites.
  • 1989 — LeCun applies backprop to convolutional networks for handwritten-digit recognition. Backprop graduates from theory to product.
  • 2006–2012 — Layer-wise pre-training, ReLU, GPUs, and large datasets together unlock deep networks. Backprop scales.
  • 2015–present — Autograd frameworks make backprop invisible. Every transformer, diffusion model, and protein folder you read about is, under the hood, the same chain-rule sweep.

Check Your Understanding

Q1. Backpropagation is, mathematically, an application of which calculus identity?
  • The product rule.
  • The fundamental theorem of calculus.
  • The chain rule.
  • Taylor's theorem.
Correct — backprop is the chain rule applied recursively along the computational graph.
Q2. Why does a deep sigmoid network suffer vanishing gradients?
  • Because sigmoid outputs are always positive.
  • Because $\sigma'(z) \le 0.25$, so the chain product shrinks geometrically with depth.
  • Because sigmoid is non-monotonic.
  • Because sigmoid is computationally expensive.
Yes — multiplying many factors $\le 0.25$ collapses the early-layer gradient towards zero.
Q3. For a hidden weight $W^{(1)}_{ij}$ in a fully-connected layer, the gradient is the product of which two quantities?
  • The output prediction times the target.
  • The input activation $x_i$ feeding the weight, times the downstream error signal $\delta_j$.
  • The learning rate times the loss.
  • The weight itself times the bias.
Exactly — $\partial L/\partial W_{ij} = x_i \cdot \delta_j$. Input on one side, error on the other.
Q4. Why is reverse-mode autodiff vastly preferred over numerical (finite-difference) gradients in deep learning?
  • Numerical gradients are less accurate but faster.
  • Reverse-mode computes all $N$ parameter gradients in one backward pass; finite differences need $O(N)$ forward passes.
  • Numerical gradients cannot handle ReLU.
  • Numerical gradients require a GPU.
Right — this scaling is the entire reason training million-parameter models is feasible.

Key Takeaways

  • Backpropagation solves the credit-assignment problem by recursively applying the chain rule along a computational graph.
  • Forward pass computes activations left-to-right; backward pass computes gradients right-to-left, using local derivatives stored along the way.
  • For any weight, $\partial L/\partial W_{ij} = a_i \cdot \delta_j$, and the update is $\Delta W = -\eta \cdot a_i \cdot \delta_j$.
  • Vanishing/exploding gradients are a direct consequence of multiplying many local derivatives — deep networks need ReLU, good init, BatchNorm, or skip connections to stay healthy.
  • Modern frameworks compute backprop automatically (autograd). You write the forward; the chain rule is free.
  • Just like tracing failure responsibility through a load path, backprop assigns blame proportional to each weight's sensitivity coefficient — its partial derivative.
