Sub-chapter 6.4

Loss Functions

The scalar score that tells a neural network how wrong it is — and how to get less wrong tomorrow.

22 min read Intermediate 2 interactive demos

What a Loss Function Does

A neural network is just a parameterized function. Until we tell it what “good” means, it has no direction. The loss function — sometimes called the cost or objective — collapses the model's predictions and the ground-truth labels into a single scalar number that says how wrong the model is right now.

The job description

  • Differentiable: we need to take its gradient with respect to weights so backprop can flow.
  • Aligned with the goal: minimizing the loss must actually correspond to better real-world performance.
  • Well-behaved: smooth enough to optimize, bounded enough to avoid numerical blow-ups.
  • Scalar: optimization needs a single number to descend on, not a vector.

Civil-engineering analogy: the acceptance criterion

Every construction project ships with a quality-control specification. A concrete cylinder breaks at 28 MPa instead of the specified 30 MPa — is that a 2 MPa demerit, a 4 MPa demerit (squared), or a hard rejection? The acceptance rule you choose completely determines what the contractor optimizes for. A loss function is exactly that: the formal scoring rule the optimizer uses to decide which predictions are “in spec” and which are not.

Key idea. Picking a loss function is a modelling decision, not a mechanical one. MSE assumes Gaussian noise. Cross-entropy assumes a categorical distribution. Huber assumes occasional outliers. Choose the loss that matches your data.

Regression Losses

When the target is a continuous number — concrete compressive strength, beam deflection, settlement in millimetres — we need a loss that compares two real numbers. Four classics dominate practice.

Mean Squared Error (MSE / L2)

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{MSE}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(y_i - \hat{y}_i\right)^2 $$

  • Pros: smooth everywhere, easy gradient ($2(\hat{y}-y)$), convex for linear models.
  • Cons: quadratic penalty makes it extremely sensitive to outliers — one bad sample dominates the gradient.
  • Use when: errors are roughly Gaussian and large mistakes really are much worse than small ones.

Mean Absolute Error (MAE / L1)

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{MAE}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\left|y_i - \hat{y}_i\right| $$

  • Pros: robust to outliers, errors in the original units, intuitive.
  • Cons: non-differentiable at zero (kink), constant gradient magnitude makes fine-tuning slow.
  • Use when: data has heavy tails or a few mislabelled samples you don't want to dominate training.

Huber Loss — the hybrid

Quadratic near zero, linear far away. Combines MSE's smoothness with MAE's robustness.

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\delta}(r) = \begin{cases} \tfrac{1}{2}r^2 & |r| \leq \delta \\ \delta\left(|r| - \tfrac{1}{2}\delta\right) & |r| > \delta \end{cases} $$

The threshold $\delta$ controls the “quadratic zone”. Tune it to roughly the standard deviation of clean residuals. Huber is the default choice when you suspect a small fraction of outliers but want smooth gradients in the bulk.

RMSE — MSE in original units

$$ \text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\mathcal{L}_{\text{MSE}}} $$

Mathematically a monotone transform of MSE, so optimizing one optimizes the other. We report RMSE because its units match the target (MPa, mm, kN), making it legible to engineers. We typically train on MSE because the square root adds nothing to the optimization but can complicate gradients.

Penalty schedule on a construction job

Imagine three contracts for delivering concrete with target strength 30 MPa:

  • MSE contract: penalty $= \$10 \times (\Delta\text{MPa})^2$. A 1-MPa miss costs $10; a 5-MPa miss costs $250.
  • MAE contract: penalty $= \$50 \times |\Delta\text{MPa}|$. A 1-MPa miss costs $50; a 5-MPa miss costs $250.
  • Huber contract: quadratic up to 2 MPa, linear beyond. Distinguishes “within tolerance noise” from “serious non-conformance” without unbounded liability for one rogue truck.

The contractor's optimization strategy depends entirely on which contract they signed. So does your network's.

Classification Losses

When the target is a category — crack type, soil class, defect / no-defect — we don't compare numbers, we compare probability distributions. The dominant tool is cross-entropy.

Binary Cross-Entropy (BCE / log loss)

For a single sample with true label $y \in \{0, 1\}$ and predicted probability $\hat{y} \in (0, 1)$:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{BCE}} = -\bigl[\, y\,\log(\hat{y}) + (1-y)\log(1-\hat{y})\,\bigr] $$

When the truth is 1 and we predict 0.99 the loss is tiny ($-\log 0.99 \approx 0.01$). Predict 0.01 instead and the loss explodes to $-\log 0.01 \approx 4.6$. Predict 0.0 exactly and the loss is $+\infty$ — the model gets infinite punishment for confident wrong answers. That's a feature, not a bug.

Categorical Cross-Entropy

For $K$ mutually exclusive classes, with one-hot truth $\mathbf{y}$ and softmax output $\hat{\mathbf{y}}$:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{CCE}} = -\sum_{k=1}^{K} y_k \log(\hat{y}_k) $$

Because $\mathbf{y}$ is one-hot, only the term for the correct class survives: we are simply maximizing the log-probability assigned to the right answer. This is exactly the negative log-likelihood of the data.

Why CE pairs with sigmoid / softmax

The combination is no accident. Take BCE with a sigmoid output $\hat{y} = \sigma(z)$:

$$ \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_{\text{BCE}}}{\partial z} = \hat{y} - y $$

The gradient is just the prediction error. No saturating $\sigma'(z)$ term to vanish, no chain-rule explosion. The same magic happens for softmax + categorical cross-entropy. This pairing is the reason classification networks train at all.

By contrast, if you tried to train a sigmoid classifier with MSE, the gradient picks up a $\sigma'(z) = \sigma(z)(1-\sigma(z))$ factor that vanishes when the model is very confident and very wrong — exactly when you most need a strong gradient signal. Demo 2 below makes this concrete.

Hinge loss (SVM-style)

For binary labels $y \in \{-1, +1\}$ and raw score $s$:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{hinge}} = \max(0,\, 1 - y\cdot s) $$

Hinge says: “once the score is on the right side by a margin of 1, stop caring.” It produces sparse gradients (zero on already-correct points) and the maximum-margin decision boundaries that made classical SVMs famous. Some modern detectors still use hinge variants for their margin behavior.

As $\hat{p} \to 0$ (wrong class with high confidence), BCE diverges and its gradient grows without bound — the optimizer feels a massive corrective force. MSE meanwhile flattens out near 0 and 1, providing almost no gradient when you most need one. That asymmetry is why classification networks use cross-entropy, not MSE.

Specialty Losses

Beyond the workhorses, several losses solve specific problems you'll encounter in infrastructure-AI projects.

Focal loss — for imbalanced data

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{focal}} = -(1-\hat{y})^{\gamma} \log(\hat{y}) $$

A modulation of cross-entropy that down-weights easy, well-classified examples (where $\hat{y} \to 1$) so the optimizer focuses on hard cases. With $\gamma=2$ a confidently correct prediction contributes about 100× less loss than under plain CE. Essential when 99% of your bridge images contain no defect and you don't want the network to win by saying “no defect” every time.

IoU and Dice — for segmentation

For predicted mask $P$ and ground truth mask $G$:

$$ \text{IoU} = \frac{|P \cap G|}{|P \cup G|}, \quad \text{Dice} = \frac{2\,|P \cap G|}{|P| + |G|} $$

Used as $1 - \text{Dice}$ or $1 - \text{IoU}$ to make a loss to minimize. They directly optimize the overlap metric reported in segmentation papers, and unlike per-pixel cross-entropy they handle class imbalance gracefully — a 5-pixel hairline crack still matters even when the background is 200 000 pixels of clean concrete.

Contrastive & triplet losses

For similarity learning: pull embeddings of the same object close together and push embeddings of different objects apart. Triplet loss uses an anchor, a positive and a negative example:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{triplet}} = \max\!\bigl(0,\; \|a-p\|^2 - \|a-n\|^2 + m\bigr) $$

Used for face recognition, structural-similarity search (“find bridges that look like this one”), and any retrieval task.

Composite losses. Real systems often sum several losses with weights: total = α·BCE + β·Dice + γ·L2-on-weights. The weighting coefficients become hyperparameters — be ready to tune them.

Choosing a Loss in Civil-Engineering AI

Predicting concrete compressive strength

Continuous target in MPa. Errors are roughly Gaussian once we control batch and curing time. Use MSE for training, report RMSE for stakeholders. If the dataset includes a few mis-recorded specimens that test at implausible values, switch to Huber with $\delta \approx 1\sigma$ of the clean residuals.

Classifying crack type from photos

Multi-class problem (longitudinal, transverse, alligator, map, none). Use categorical cross-entropy with a softmax head. If your “none” class dominates, switch to focal loss to refocus training on the rarer crack types.

Bridge defect segmentation

Pixel-level mask of corrosion or spalling on bridge deck imagery. The defect occupies maybe 1–3% of pixels. Pure pixel-wise BCE will collapse. Use Dice loss, optionally combined with BCE ($\mathcal{L} = 0.5\,\text{BCE} + 0.5\,(1-\text{Dice})$) for the best of both worlds.

PyTorch loss snippets 
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F

# Regression: concrete strength (MPa)
mse  = nn.MSELoss()
mae  = nn.L1Loss()
huber = nn.HuberLoss(delta=1.0)

# Multi-class: crack type (5 classes)
ce = nn.CrossEntropyLoss()      # expects raw logits + integer labels
# logits: (B, 5)    labels: (B,)
loss = ce(logits, labels)

# Binary segmentation: corrosion mask
bce_logits = nn.BCEWithLogitsLoss()  # numerically stable

def dice_loss(pred_logits, target, eps=1e-6):
    pred = torch.sigmoid(pred_logits)
    inter = (pred * target).sum(dim=(1,2,3))
    union = pred.sum(dim=(1,2,3)) + target.sum(dim=(1,2,3))
    return 1.0 - (2*inter + eps) / (union + eps)

# Combined for severe imbalance
def combo(pred, target):
    return 0.5 * bce_logits(pred, target) + 0.5 * dice_loss(pred, target).mean()

Quick Check

1. Your dataset contains a small fraction of mis-recorded outliers in concrete strength. Which regression loss is most appropriate?

  • a) MSE — outliers will average out
  • b) Huber loss with $\delta$ near one residual standard deviation
  • c) Cross-entropy
  • d) Hinge loss

Huber is quadratic in the bulk (smooth gradient on clean data) and linear far away (bounded influence per outlier). MSE would let one rogue datapoint dominate training.

2. Why does cross-entropy pair so well with sigmoid / softmax outputs?

  • a) The two functions cancel out, removing the need for backprop
  • b) It's a historical convention with no mathematical reason
  • c) The chain-rule derivative simplifies to (prediction − truth), avoiding vanishing gradients from $\sigma'(z)$
  • d) It guarantees convex loss surfaces for deep networks

The $\sigma'(z) = \sigma(z)(1-\sigma(z))$ term in the gradient cancels exactly with the $1/\hat{y}$ from log loss, leaving the clean gradient $\hat{y} - y$.

3. You're segmenting hairline cracks that occupy ~1% of pixels in bridge-deck imagery. Pixel-wise BCE trains a model that predicts “no crack” everywhere. Best fix?

  • a) Increase learning rate
  • b) Switch to MSE
  • c) Add more layers
  • d) Use Dice loss (or BCE + Dice combination)

Dice optimizes overlap directly and is scale-invariant to class size, so a 5-pixel crack contributes meaningfully to the loss.

4. Compared to MSE, what does MAE's gradient look like as a function of the residual $r = \hat{y} - y$?

  • a) Constant magnitude $\pm 1$ with a discontinuity at $r=0$
  • b) Linearly proportional to $r$
  • c) Quadratic in $r$
  • d) Always exactly zero

$\frac{d}{dr}|r| = \text{sign}(r)$, which is $\pm 1$ depending on direction and undefined at zero. This makes MAE robust to outliers but slow to converge near the minimum.

Wrap-up

  • A loss function is the scalar acceptance criterion that defines what “wrong” means.
  • Regression: MSE (default), MAE (robust), Huber (best of both), RMSE for reporting.
  • Classification: cross-entropy — pairs with sigmoid/softmax for clean gradients.
  • Specialty: focal for imbalance, Dice/IoU for segmentation, triplet for similarity.
  • The loss you choose is a modelling assumption. Match it to your data and your goal.
