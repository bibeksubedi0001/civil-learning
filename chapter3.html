Fundamentals

What is Unsupervised Learning?

In Supervised Learning (Ch 2), we had labeled data - every input came with a known answer. Unsupervised Learning has no labels. The algorithm must discover patterns, structure, and groupings entirely on its own.

Think of the difference between a guided tour and solo exploration. Supervised = a guide tells you "this is a temple, this is a market." Unsupervised = you're dropped into an unknown city with no map, no guide, no signs - and you must figure out the neighborhoods, the busy areas, and the quiet zones by yourself.

The GIS Surveyor's Challenge

You receive a satellite image covering 500 km² of mixed terrain. No land-use map exists for this area. You have spectral data (reflectance values in multiple wavelengths) for every pixel, but no labels - nobody has classified what's forest, urban, water, or agriculture. Unsupervised learning examines the spectral patterns and automatically groups similar pixels together, effectively creating the land-use map from scratch.

The Four Types of Unsupervised Learning

Most Common

Clustering

Group similar data points together. Like grouping soil samples by their properties into natural categories that nobody predefined.

Simplification

Dimensionality Reduction

Reduce the number of variables while preserving the essential information. Like summarizing a 50-variable soil report into 3 key factors.

Safety-Critical

Anomaly Detection

Find data points that don't belong to any group. Like detecting a sensor reading that's wildly different from all others - potential structural failure.

Relationships

Association Rules

Discover which events co-occur. Like finding that projects with material X AND contractor Y tend to experience delays.

Core Concept

Clustering - Discovering Natural Groups

Clustering is the heart of unsupervised learning. Given a set of data points, the algorithm identifies natural groupings - clusters of points that are similar to each other and different from points in other clusters.

CE Analogy: Land-Use Zoning from Satellite Data

Each pixel in a satellite image has values for Red, Green, Blue, Near-Infrared, and other bands. Water pixels have similar spectral signatures (low Red, high Blue). Forest pixels have high Near-Infrared. Urban pixels have mixed moderate values. The clustering algorithm discovers these groups without being told what "water" or "forest" looks like. It just sees that certain pixels have similar numbers and groups them together.

Algorithm

K-Means Clustering - The Workhorse

K-Means is the most widely used clustering algorithm. It's simple, fast, and effective. You specify K (the number of clusters), and the algorithm iteratively assigns each point to the nearest cluster center and recalculates the centers.

How K-Means Works (Step by Step)

1

Choose K Random Starting Points (Centroids)

Place K "pins" randomly on the map. These are the initial guesses for cluster centers.

2

Assign Each Point to the Nearest Centroid

Every data point looks at the K centroids and joins the closest one. Like people gravitating to the nearest exit.

3

Recalculate Centroids

Move each centroid to the average position of all points assigned to it.

4

Repeat Until Stable

Keep reassigning and recalculating until the centroids stop moving. Convergence usually happens in 10-20 iterations.

The "K" problem: K-Means requires you to choose K in advance. But how do you know how many clusters exist in your data? Methods like the "Elbow Method" and "Silhouette Score" help, but it often requires domain expertise. A geologist might know there are 4 major soil types in a region, so K=4 makes sense.
Algorithm

Hierarchical Clustering - The Dendrogram Builder

Instead of requiring K upfront, hierarchical clustering builds a tree (dendrogram) that shows how clusters merge at different levels. You can then "cut" the tree at any level to get the desired number of clusters.

CE Analogy: Project Grouping

Imagine 50 construction projects with attributes (budget, duration, team size, complexity). Hierarchical clustering first pairs the two most similar projects, then merges similar pairs, building up from individual projects to categories. Cut at the top: 2 groups (large vs small). Cut in the middle: 5 groups (residential, commercial, infrastructure, renovation, demolition).

DBSCAN - Density-Based Clustering

DBSCAN (Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise) is special because it can find clusters of any shape and automatically detects outliers.

CE Analogy: Settlement Mapping

When mapping human settlements from satellite data, villages and cities come in all shapes - not just circular blobs. A riverine settlement might follow the river for kilometers. DBSCAN can detect these elongated, irregular clusters that K-Means (which assumes circular clusters) would split incorrectly. It also automatically identifies isolated buildings as "noise" - not belonging to any settlement.

Advantages

No need to specify K. Finds arbitrary shapes. Handles noise/outliers. Works well for spatial data like GIS coordinates.

Challenges

Needs tuning of two parameters: epsilon (neighborhood size) and minPoints (minimum density). Struggles when clusters have very different densities.

Knowledge Check #1

You want to identify land-use zones from satellite data, but you don't know how many zones exist and some pixels are noise (clouds, shadows). Which algorithm is best?
A K-Means - it's the most popular clustering method
B DBSCAN - doesn't need K and handles noise automatically
C Linear Regression - to predict land-use values
Correct! DBSCAN discovers the number of clusters automatically and labels noise points separately - perfect for messy satellite data.
K-Means requires knowing K in advance and can't handle noise. Regression is supervised and predicts numbers, not clusters. DBSCAN is the right fit here.
Technique

Dimensionality Reduction - Simplifying Complexity

Real-world data has many features (dimensions). A soil sample might have 20+ measured properties. A satellite image has 10+ spectral bands. Dimensionality reduction compresses this into fewer dimensions while keeping the important patterns.

CE Analogy: The Cross-Section Simplification

A 3D terrain model has millions of data points. But for a road design, you often only need a 2D cross-section at key chainages. Dimensionality reduction is like extracting the most informative cross-sections - you lose some detail but retain the essential shape that drives design decisions.

PCA - Principal Component Analysis

PCA is the most common dimensionality reduction technique. It finds the directions (principal components) along which the data varies the most, and projects the data onto those directions.

PCA Visualizer - 2D → 1D Reduction See how PCA finds the best axis

t-SNE - Visualization of High-Dimensional Data

While PCA preserves global structure, t-SNE preserves local structure - it's excellent for visualizing clusters in high-dimensional data by "unfolding" complex relationships into a 2D plot that humans can interpret.

When to use which: PCA for data preprocessing (reducing features before training another model). t-SNE for visualization (seeing if clusters exist in your data). PCA is fast and deterministic; t-SNE is slower but reveals nonlinear relationships.
Safety-Critical

Anomaly Detection - Finding What Doesn't Belong

Anomaly detection identifies data points that are significantly different from the majority. In civil engineering, anomalies are often the most important things to find - a bridge sensor reading that suddenly deviates could indicate structural failure.

CE Analogy: Structural Health Monitoring

A bridge has 200 strain gauges recording data every second. 99.9% of readings are normal (the bridge behaving as expected). An anomaly detection model learns what "normal" looks like from millions of readings. When a sensor suddenly shows a pattern it's never seen before - that's an anomaly. It triggers an alert before anyone visually notices the crack.

Knowledge Check #2

A bridge monitoring system uses ML to detect unusual strain patterns. Is this supervised or unsupervised learning?
A Supervised - it learns from labeled examples of failures
B Unsupervised - it learns "normal" patterns and flags anything unusual
Correct! In practice, we rarely have labeled examples of failures (thankfully, bridges don't fail often). So the system learns what "normal" looks like from routine data, making it unsupervised anomaly detection.
Think about it: do we have labeled examples of bridge failures to train on? Usually not. The system learns "normal" and flags deviations - classic unsupervised anomaly detection.

Clustering demographic, economic, and infrastructure data to identify distinct urban zones for planning purposes.

Code Peek

Unsupervised Learning in Code

K-Means: Land-Use Classification

land_use_clustering.py 
# Unsupervised: Classify satellite pixels into land-use zones
# NO LABELS - the algorithm discovers groups on its own

from sklearn.cluster import KMeans
import numpy as np

# Load satellite data: 50,000 pixels × 6 spectral bands
pixels = load_satellite_data("landsat_scene.tif")
# Shape: (50000, 6) - R, G, B, NIR, SWIR1, SWIR2 per pixel

# Apply K-Means with 5 clusters (we estimate 5 land-use types)
kmeans = KMeans(n_clusters=5, random_state=42)
labels = kmeans.fit_predict(pixels)
# labels = [2, 0, 0, 3, 1, 4, 2, ...] - cluster ID for each pixel

# Visualize: reshape labels back to image dimensions
classified_map = labels.reshape(image_height, image_width)
# Each cluster represents a land-use type:
# Cluster 0 → Water (low reflectance in all bands)
# Cluster 1 → Forest (high NIR, low visible)
# Cluster 2 → Urban (moderate, mixed reflectance)
# Cluster 3 → Agriculture (seasonal green patterns)
# Cluster 4 → Barren land (high SWIR, low NIR)

# The algorithm didn't know these names - WE interpret the clusters
# by examining each cluster's centroid spectral signature
Notice: fit_predict() instead of fit(X, y). There's no y (target labels) - the algorithm discovers structure from the input alone. The human's job is to interpret what each cluster represents.

Anomaly Detection: Structural Monitoring

bridge_anomaly.py 
# Anomaly Detection: Flag unusual sensor readings on a bridge

from sklearn.ensemble import IsolationForest

# 6 months of normal sensor data (strain, temp, vibration, tilt)
normal_data = load_data("bridge_sensors_2024.csv")
# Shape: (260000, 4) - readings every minute for 6 months

# Train the anomaly detector on "normal" behavior
detector = IsolationForest(contamination=0.01) # expect ~1% anomalies
detector.fit(normal_data)

# Check new incoming readings
new_reading = [[245, 18.3, 0.42, 0.08]] # strain, temp, vibration, tilt
prediction = detector.predict(new_reading)
# → -1 (ANOMALY!) or +1 (normal)

if prediction[0] == -1:
    send_alert(" Anomalous reading detected - inspect sensor/structure")
Isolation Forest works by randomly partitioning data. Anomalies are easier to isolate (fewer partitions needed), so the algorithm naturally separates them. No labels required - it just learns the "normal" distribution.
Assessment

Chapter Summary & Final Quiz

No Labels Required

Unsupervised learning discovers structure in unlabeled data. The algorithm finds patterns humans didn't specify.

Clustering Algorithms

K-Means (fast, needs K), Hierarchical (tree-based), DBSCAN (density-based, handles noise, finds any shape).

Dimensionality Reduction

PCA simplifies data while preserving variance. t-SNE visualizes high-dimensional clusters. Both reduce complexity.

Anomaly Detection

Finds unusual patterns in data. Critical for structural health monitoring, sensor validation, and quality control.

Final Challenge

You have CPT (Cone Penetration Test) data from 100 boreholes. You want to automatically identify distinct soil layers across the site. You don't know in advance how many layers exist, and some CPT readings might be erroneous. Which approach is most appropriate?
A Supervised classification using labeled soil types
B K-Means with K=3 clusters
C DBSCAN - discovers number of layers automatically and handles noise
D Linear regression to predict soil type
Excellent! DBSCAN is ideal because: (1) you don't know K, (2) erroneous readings are treated as noise, and (3) soil layers may have complex shapes in feature space. K-Means with K=3 assumes you know the answer. Supervised requires labels you don't have.
Consider: you don't have labels (ruling out supervised), you don't know K (ruling out K-Means with a fixed K), and you have noise. DBSCAN handles all three requirements.
