Chapter 2 — Sub-chapter 2.9

Overfitting, Underfitting & Regularization

A model that memorises every crack in one specific bridge deck is useless on the next bridge. A model so simple it predicts the same strength for every concrete mix is equally useless. Finding the sweet spot between too specific and too generic is one of the most important skills in applied machine learning — and it mirrors a challenge every civil engineer already understands: designing for the particular without losing the general.

40 min read 2 interactive demos 4 quiz questions

The Goldilocks Problem of Machine Learning

A geotechnical engineer collects soil samples from a construction site: 12 boreholes, each with unique moisture content, grain-size distribution, and Atterberg limits. She could design a foundation tailored exactly to each borehole — but that foundation would fail at the 13th borehole. Alternatively, she could use the same standard foundation everywhere — safe, but wasteful and sometimes inadequate.

Machine learning models face the same dilemma. An overfit model memorises the training data (including its noise and quirks) and fails on new data. An underfit model is too simple to capture real patterns and performs poorly everywhere. The goal is the sweet spot: a model complex enough to learn the true signal, but constrained enough to generalise.

This sub-chapter dissects the bias-variance tradeoff, the formal framework behind the overfitting/underfitting problem. We will then explore regularization — a family of techniques that deliberately constrain models to improve generalisation. These techniques include L1 (Lasso), L2 (Ridge), Elastic Net, dropout, and early stopping.

In this sub-chapter, we will cover:

  • The bias-variance tradeoff and why every model navigates it
  • Overfitting: when your model memorises noise instead of learning signal
  • Underfitting: when your model is too simple to capture the real relationship
  • The sweet spot: how to find the right model complexity
  • Regularization: L1 (Lasso), L2 (Ridge), and Elastic Net
  • Dropout and early stopping for neural networks
  • Civil engineering analogies for every concept
  • Two interactive demos to visualise these ideas in action

The Bias-Variance Tradeoff

Every prediction error can be decomposed into three components:

Error Decomposition 
Total Error = Bias^2 + Variance + Irreducible Noise

Bias      = Error from wrong assumptions (too simple model)
Variance  = Error from sensitivity to training data fluctuations
Noise     = Random error inherent in the data (cannot be eliminated)

Bias measures how far off your model's average prediction is from the true value. A high-bias model makes strong assumptions — for example, assuming the relationship between concrete strength and water-cement ratio is perfectly linear, when in reality it follows a curve. No matter how much data you give it, the model will systematically miss the true pattern.

Variance measures how much your model's predictions change when trained on different subsets of data. A high-variance model is extremely sensitive to the specific training samples. Train it on Monday's data and it predicts one thing; train it on Tuesday's data and it predicts something completely different. It has effectively memorised the noise in each dataset.

Civil Engineering Analogy: The Surveyor

Imagine a surveyor measuring the elevation of a benchmark point. Bias is like a systematic error in the instrument: every reading is 5 cm too high, no matter how many measurements are taken. Variance is like random wobble in the readings: each measurement scatters around the true value. A good instrument has both low bias (accurate) and low variance (precise). In machine learning, we seek models that are both accurate (low bias) and stable (low variance).

The tradeoff arises because reducing one tends to increase the other:

  • Simple models (few parameters) have high bias but low variance. They under-learn the data but are stable across different training sets.
  • Complex models (many parameters) have low bias but high variance. They can capture intricate patterns but are overly sensitive to the specific training data.

The goal is to find the sweet spot where the total error (bias squared plus variance) is minimised. This is the fundamental challenge of model selection.

The Classic U-Shaped Curve 
   Error
    |
    |  \                             /
    |   \   Test Error             /
    |    \                       /
    |     \                    /       High Variance
    |      \       Sweet     /         (Overfitting)

As complexity increases:
  - Training error decreases monotonically (model fits data better)
  - Test error first decreases (less bias), then increases (more variance)
  - The sweet spot is where test error is minimised

Overfitting: Memorising the Noise

Overfitting occurs when a model learns the training data too well, capturing not just the underlying pattern but also the random noise, outliers, and quirks specific to that particular dataset. The result: excellent performance on training data, terrible performance on new, unseen data.

Symptoms of Overfitting

  • Training error is very low (near zero)
  • Validation/test error is much higher than training error
  • The gap between training and test error grows as model complexity increases
  • The model's predictions are erratic and unstable on new data
  • Small changes in input produce wildly different outputs

Civil Engineering Analogy: The Over-Customised Foundation

Imagine designing a foundation system based on a single borehole at one corner of the site. You optimise the pile length, diameter, and reinforcement for the exact soil profile at that one location. The design works perfectly there — but move 20 metres to where the clay layer is thicker and the sand lens is absent, and your "perfect" design fails. You have overfit your design to one specific sample of the ground conditions.

A seasoned geotechnical engineer uses multiple boreholes, incorporates uncertainty, and designs for a range of plausible conditions — just as a well-regularised model learns from the overall trend, not from individual data points.

Common Causes

Insufficient Training Data

When training data is scarce, the model has limited examples to learn from. With only 30 concrete cylinder test results, a model with 20 parameters can essentially memorise each sample. More data dilutes the influence of individual noise points and helps the model find the true underlying relationship.

Excessive Model Complexity

A 15th-degree polynomial fitted to 20 data points will pass through (or near) every point, producing wild oscillations between them. The model has enough "knobs" to contort itself into any shape, matching noise rather than signal. This is the most common cause of overfitting: the model has more capacity than the data warrants.

Training for Too Long

In iterative training (gradient descent, neural networks), each epoch refines the model's fit. Early epochs capture the big patterns; later epochs start fitting the noise. If you train for 10,000 epochs when 500 would suffice, you are effectively polishing noise into your model.

Noisy or Unclean Data

Data with measurement errors, mislabelled examples, or outliers provides the model with "fake patterns" to learn. If a few concrete strength tests have typos (280 MPa instead of 28 MPa), a complex model will bend itself to accommodate those points, corrupting its predictions for legitimate data.

Underfitting: Too Simple to Learn

Underfitting is the opposite problem: the model is too simple to capture the underlying relationship in the data. It performs poorly on both training and test data because it has not learned the pattern at all.

Symptoms of Underfitting

  • Both training and test errors are high
  • Training and test errors are similar (both bad)
  • The model's predictions are flat or overly simplistic
  • Adding more training data does not improve performance
  • The learning curve plateaus early at a high error

Civil Engineering Analogy: The One-Size-Fits-All Foundation

Using the same 600mm-diameter, 12-metre-long driven pile for every project — a 3-storey office building on clay, a 30-storey tower on rock, a waterfront warehouse on fill — is the structural equivalent of underfitting. The design is so generic that it is grossly over-conservative for simple cases, grossly under-designed for complex ones, and optimal for none. It ignores the actual ground conditions, building loads, and site constraints.

Common Causes

  • Model too simple: Fitting a straight line to data that follows a quadratic or exponential curve.
  • Important features missing: Predicting concrete strength without including water-cement ratio, curing time, or aggregate type.
  • Too much regularization: Overly aggressive regularization can strangle the model, preventing it from learning even legitimate patterns. (We will see this in Demo 2.)
  • Insufficient training: Stopping gradient descent after just a few epochs, before the model has had a chance to converge.

Diagnosing the Problem: Training vs. Test Error

Diagnostic Table 
Condition            | Train Error | Test Error | Diagnosis
---------------------+-------------+------------+-------------------
Both high, similar   |    HIGH     |    HIGH    | UNDERFITTING
Train low, test high |    LOW      |    HIGH    | OVERFITTING
Both low, similar    |    LOW      |    LOW     | GOOD FIT (sweet spot)

Key Insight:
  - If train error is already high, the model cannot even learn the
    training data. Adding more data or regularization will NOT help.
    You need a more complex model or better features.
  - If train error is low but test error is high, the model has
    learned the training data but cannot generalise. Regularization,
    more data, or a simpler model can help.

Finding the Sweet Spot

The sweet spot is the model complexity at which the test error is minimised. To the left, underfitting dominates (high bias). To the right, overfitting dominates (high variance). The sweet spot balances both.

In practice, we find the sweet spot through:

  • Cross-validation: Split the data into k folds, train on k-1 folds, and validate on the remaining fold. Repeat k times and average the validation error. This gives a robust estimate of generalisation performance. (Covered in detail in Sub-chapter 2.8.)
  • Learning curves: Plot training and validation error as a function of training set size or model complexity. The gap between the two curves tells you whether you are overfitting or underfitting.
  • Regularization: Instead of manually selecting model complexity, add a penalty term that automatically constrains the model. This is the focus of the next sections.

Civil Engineering Analogy: Design Codes

Structural design codes (Eurocode, ACI, IS codes) are essentially regularization for civil engineering. They prevent engineers from overfitting to a specific scenario by imposing minimum requirements, safety factors, and design constraints. A design code says: "Even if your specific analysis says you can use 10mm bars at 300mm spacing, you must use at least 12mm bars at 200mm spacing." This regularization sacrifices a tiny amount of optimality for a large gain in robustness across variable real-world conditions.

Regularization: Taming Model Complexity

Regularization is the deliberate introduction of constraints that prevent a model from fitting the training data too closely. Instead of choosing a simpler model architecture, we keep the complex model but add a penalty term to the loss function that discourages large or unnecessary parameter values.

Regularized Loss Function 
Regularized Loss = Original Loss + lambda * Penalty(weights)

Where:
  - Original Loss = e.g. MSE = (1/n) * SUM(y_i - y_hat_i)^2
  - lambda (lambda) = regularization strength (hyperparameter)
  - Penalty = a function of the model weights

lambda = 0   -->  No regularization (original loss only)
lambda = inf -->  Maximum regularization (weights forced to zero)

The hyperparameter lambda controls the tradeoff between fitting the data and keeping the weights small. A small lambda applies gentle regularization; a large lambda aggressively penalises complexity, potentially causing underfitting. The optimal lambda is found through cross-validation.

L2 Regularization (Ridge Regression)

L2 regularization adds the sum of squared weights to the loss function. This is called Ridge regression when applied to linear models.

Ridge (L2) Loss 
L2 Penalty = lambda * SUM(w_j^2)

Ridge Loss = MSE + lambda * SUM(w_j^2)

Effect:
  - Shrinks all weights toward zero (but never exactly to zero)
  - Large weights are penalised quadratically (a weight of 10 incurs
    100x the penalty of a weight of 1)
  - Distributes the model's capacity across all features
  - Effective when many features contribute small amounts

Geometrically, L2 regularization constrains the weight vector to lie within a hypersphere. The loss contours intersect this sphere at a point where all weights are small but non-zero.

Civil Engineering Analogy

L2 regularization is like a building code requirement for ductility. The code does not eliminate any structural member, but it limits how much load any single member carries. If one column wants to take 80% of the lateral load (a very "large weight"), the code imposes capacity limits that distribute the load more evenly across all columns. Every column participates, but none dominates excessively.

L1 Regularization (Lasso)

L1 regularization adds the sum of absolute weights to the loss function. This is called Lasso (Least Absolute Shrinkage and Selection Operator) when applied to linear models.

Lasso (L1) Loss 
L1 Penalty = lambda * SUM(|w_j|)

Lasso Loss = MSE + lambda * SUM(|w_j|)

Effect:
  - Drives some weights exactly to zero (feature selection!)
  - Produces sparse models with fewer active features
  - Useful when you suspect only a few features truly matter
  - Less effective when features are correlated (picks one, drops others)

The key difference from L2: L1 can produce sparse models where many coefficients are exactly zero. This makes Lasso a powerful tool for automatic feature selection. In a model with 50 input features, Lasso might discover that only 8 truly matter and set the remaining 42 weights to zero.

Geometrically, L1 constrains weights to a hypercube (diamond shape in 2D). The loss contours tend to intersect the corners of this diamond, which correspond to axis-aligned solutions where some weights are exactly zero.

Civil Engineering Analogy

L1 regularization is like a cost-optimisation exercise that eliminates unnecessary members from a truss. If a truss has 30 members but only 18 carry significant load, an optimisation algorithm with an L1-style penalty will zero out the 12 redundant members entirely, producing a lighter, simpler structure. This is exactly what Lasso does to model features: it does not just shrink unimportant weights — it removes them completely.

Elastic Net: Best of Both Worlds

Elastic Net combines L1 and L2 penalties, blending the feature-selection ability of Lasso with the stability of Ridge.

Elastic Net Loss 
Elastic Net = MSE + lambda * [ alpha * SUM(|w_j|) + (1-alpha) * SUM(w_j^2) ]

Where:
  alpha = 1.0  -->  Pure Lasso (L1)
  alpha = 0.0  -->  Pure Ridge (L2)
  alpha = 0.5  -->  Equal blend

Advantages:
  - Handles correlated features better than pure L1
  - Can select groups of correlated features together
  - Provides sparsity (from L1) + stability (from L2)
  - Ideal when feature count >> sample count

Elastic Net is particularly useful in civil engineering applications where input features are often correlated. For example, in a concrete mix design model, water content and water-cement ratio are strongly correlated. Pure Lasso might arbitrarily pick one and drop the other. Elastic Net handles this gracefully by grouping correlated features together.

Comparison Summary

Method       | Penalty       | Sparsity | Correlated Features
-------------+---------------+----------+--------------------
Ridge (L2)   | SUM(w_j^2)    | No       | Keeps all, shrinks
Lasso (L1)   | SUM(|w_j|)    | Yes      | Picks one, drops rest
Elastic Net  | L1 + L2 blend | Partial  | Groups together

Dropout: Regularization for Neural Networks

Dropout is a regularization technique designed specifically for neural networks. During each training iteration, dropout randomly "turns off" a fraction of neurons (typically 20-50%), forcing the network to learn redundant representations. At test time, all neurons are active, but their outputs are scaled down to account for the dropout rate.

Dropout Mechanism 
Training Phase (dropout_rate = 0.5):
  For each training batch:
    - Randomly select 50% of neurons in each layer
    - Set their outputs to zero (temporarily "dead")
    - Forward/backward pass uses only surviving neurons
    - Next batch: different random neurons are dropped

Testing Phase:
    - ALL neurons are active (no dropping)
    - Multiply each neuron's output by (1 - dropout_rate)
    - This approximates the average of all possible sub-networks

Effect:
  - Prevents co-adaptation (neurons cannot rely on specific partners)
  - Each neuron must learn independently useful features
  - Equivalent to training an ensemble of many smaller networks
  - Dramatically reduces overfitting in deep networks

Civil Engineering Analogy

Dropout is like robustness testing through redundancy. Imagine designing a bridge where, during the design verification phase, you randomly remove 30% of the structural members and check if the bridge still stands. If any single member's removal causes collapse, your design lacks redundancy. By repeatedly testing with random member removals, you force the design to distribute loads across multiple paths — exactly what dropout does to neural network weights.

Early Stopping: Knowing When to Quit

Early stopping is perhaps the simplest regularization technique: monitor the validation error during training, and stop when it starts increasing. The training error will continue decreasing, but the validation error will begin to rise as the model starts memorising noise.

Early Stopping Strategy 
Epoch:    1    50   100  200  500  1000  2000  5000
Train:  0.95  0.42  0.21  0.08  0.02  0.005  0.001  0.0002
Valid:  0.98  0.45  0.24  0.15  0.13  0.18   0.25   0.42
                                        ^
                                        |
                              STOP HERE (patience = 100 epochs)

Early Stopping Algorithm:
  1. Train model, recording validation loss after each epoch
  2. Track the best validation loss seen so far
  3. If validation loss has not improved for "patience" epochs, STOP
  4. Restore the model weights from the best epoch
  5. The "patience" hyperparameter controls how long you wait

Early stopping is an example of implicit regularization. It does not change the loss function but limits the model's effective complexity by restricting how long it trains. The number of training epochs effectively controls model complexity: more epochs = more complex model = higher risk of overfitting.

Civil Engineering Analogy

Early stopping is like knowing when to stop optimising a structural design. The first few iterations of optimisation yield dramatic improvements — reducing member sizes, saving material costs. But after a point, further "optimisation" starts shaving safety margins, removing redundancy, and making the design brittle against unexpected loads. A wise engineer stops optimising before the design becomes too finely tuned to the assumed loading conditions.

Regularization Techniques at a Glance

Summary Comparison 
Technique        | How It Works                  | When to Use
-----------------+-------------------------------+---------------------------
L2 (Ridge)       | Penalises sum of w^2          | Many features, all relevant
L1 (Lasso)       | Penalises sum of |w|          | Feature selection needed
Elastic Net      | Blend of L1 + L2              | Correlated features
Dropout          | Randomly deactivate neurons   | Deep neural networks
Early Stopping   | Stop training at best val err | Any iterative model

Key Hyperparameters:
  - lambda: regularization strength (L1, L2, Elastic Net)
  - alpha: L1/L2 mixing ratio (Elastic Net)
  - dropout_rate: fraction of neurons dropped (Dropout)
  - patience: epochs to wait before stopping (Early Stopping)

Practical Guidelines for Civil Engineers

Start Simple, Add Complexity as Needed

Begin with a simple model (linear regression, low-degree polynomial). If training and validation errors are both high (underfitting), gradually increase complexity. If the training error is low but validation error is high (overfitting), add regularization or reduce complexity. This mirrors good engineering practice: start with a simple analysis, add sophistication only when the simple model is inadequate.

Always Use a Validation Set

Never evaluate your model only on training data. Always hold out a validation set (or use cross-validation) to get an honest estimate of generalisation performance. In civil engineering terms: do not claim your structural design is adequate just because it passes the load case you designed for. Test it against a range of realistic load combinations.

Use Regularization by Default

When using linear models, prefer Ridge or Lasso over plain least-squares regression. The computational cost is negligible, and the generalisation benefit is almost always positive. When training neural networks, always include dropout and early stopping. Think of regularization as a safety factor: it costs almost nothing but provides substantial protection against overfitting.

Tune Hyperparameters with Cross-Validation

The regularization strength lambda should be tuned using k-fold cross-validation (Sub-chapter 2.8). Try a range of values on a logarithmic scale (e.g., 0.001, 0.01, 0.1, 1, 10, 100) and select the value that minimises the average validation error. Do not pick lambda based on training error alone — that defeats the purpose of regularization.

Collect More Data When Possible

The most robust cure for overfitting is more data. More training samples reduce variance without increasing bias. If you can run additional lab tests, collect more sensor readings, or obtain more inspection reports, do so. Regularization is a substitute for data when data is expensive or limited — not a replacement for good data collection practices.

Quick Check 1

A neural network predicting concrete compressive strength achieves a training RMSE of 0.5 MPa and a test RMSE of 12.3 MPa. What is the most likely diagnosis?

Correct! The enormous gap between training error (0.5 MPa) and test error (12.3 MPa) is the hallmark of overfitting. The model has memorised the training data (achieving near-zero error) but fails to generalise to new data. Regularization, more training data, or a simpler architecture would help.
Not quite. The key diagnostic clue is the large gap between training and test error. A training RMSE of 0.5 MPa shows the model has learned the training data almost perfectly, but the test RMSE of 12.3 MPa shows it cannot generalise. This is classic overfitting.

Quick Check 2

You are building a model to predict traffic flow using 40 features (temperature, time of day, road width, lane count, etc.), but you suspect that only 8-10 features actually matter. Which regularization technique is most appropriate?

Correct! L1 (Lasso) regularization is the ideal choice when you need feature selection. It drives the coefficients of irrelevant features to exactly zero, producing a sparse model with only the truly important features. Ridge would shrink all 40 features but keep them all active.
Not quite. When you need to identify which features matter (feature selection), L1 (Lasso) is the best choice because it drives unimportant feature weights to exactly zero. Ridge keeps all features active (just smaller), and dropout is for neural networks, not feature selection.

Quick Check 3

An engineer increases the regularization strength (lambda) in a Ridge regression model for predicting beam deflection. What happens?

Correct! As lambda increases, the model is increasingly constrained, so training error rises (the model can no longer fit the training data as closely). Test error may initially decrease (as overfitting is reduced) but eventually increases too (as the model becomes too constrained — underfitting). This is the U-shaped curve for test error as a function of lambda.
Not quite. Increasing lambda constrains the model more, which always increases training error (the model fits training data less closely). Test error follows a U-shape: it decreases as overfitting is corrected, then increases as the model becomes too simple (underfitting).

Quick Check 4

Correct! An overfit model is like designing for one specific soil sample — it performs perfectly at that one location but fails elsewhere. Option A describes underfitting (too generic), option B describes excessive regularization, and option D describes cross-validation.

Key Takeaways

Summary

  1. Bias-Variance Tradeoff: Every model navigates a tradeoff between underfitting (high bias, low variance) and overfitting (low bias, high variance). The goal is to minimise the total error.
  2. Overfitting occurs when the model memorises noise and fails to generalise. Symptoms: low training error, high test error.
  3. Underfitting occurs when the model is too simple. Symptoms: high training error, high test error.
  4. L2 (Ridge) shrinks all weights toward zero but never eliminates features. Best for many small contributions.
  5. L1 (Lasso) drives some weights to exactly zero, performing automatic feature selection. Best when few features matter.
  6. Elastic Net blends L1 and L2, handling correlated features gracefully.
  7. Dropout randomly deactivates neurons during training, forcing the network to learn redundant representations.
  8. Early Stopping halts training when validation error stops improving, implicitly limiting model complexity.
  9. Regularization is like a design code for ML: it sacrifices a small amount of training performance for a large gain in real-world robustness.
