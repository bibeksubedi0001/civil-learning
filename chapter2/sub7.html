Chapter 2 — Sub-chapter 2.7

Support Vector Machines

Imagine you must draw a fence between two groups of soil samples on a scatter plot. There are many possible fences, but only one sits as far as possible from both groups — maximising the safety margin. Support Vector Machines find that optimal fence, even when the data curves, spirals, or lives in dimensions no human can visualise. From classifying earthquake damage levels to predicting soil liquefaction, SVMs give civil engineers a mathematically rigorous boundary with built-in robustness guarantees.

45 min read 2 interactive demos 4 quiz questions

Finding the Widest Road Between Classes

In the previous sub-chapters, we learned how logistic regression draws a decision boundary by modelling the probability of class membership, and how decision trees partition the space into axis-aligned rectangles. Support Vector Machines take a fundamentally different approach: they search for the maximum margin hyperplane — the boundary that is as far away as possible from the nearest data points of both classes.

Think of it as road design. When you plan a highway dividing two neighbourhoods, you do not place the road right next to the houses on either side. You centre it so there is a generous buffer zone — a margin — between the road and the closest buildings. The houses that sit right at the edge of the buffer are the support vectors, and together they define where the road must go. Every other house is irrelevant to the road's position.

SVMs were developed in the 1990s by Vladimir Vapnik and colleagues, and they remain one of the most elegant and theoretically grounded algorithms in machine learning. They come with strong generalisation guarantees derived from statistical learning theory, and they perform remarkably well on small-to-medium datasets — exactly the scenario many civil engineering projects face, where data is expensive to collect.

In this sub-chapter, we will cover:

  • The maximum margin hyperplane and why maximising the margin leads to better generalisation
  • Support vectors — the critical data points that define the boundary
  • The soft margin formulation and the C parameter for handling noisy data
  • The kernel trick: linear, polynomial, and RBF kernels for non-linear boundaries
  • Support Vector Regression (SVR) for continuous prediction tasks
  • Civil engineering applications: earthquake damage classification and soil liquefaction prediction

A Quick Review: Linear Decision Boundaries

Before diving into SVMs, let us recall what a linear decision boundary looks like. In two dimensions, a linear classifier separates the feature space with a straight line. In three dimensions, it uses a plane. In d dimensions, it uses a hyperplane — a flat (d-1)-dimensional subspace.

Linear Decision Boundary 
Hyperplane equation:   w . x + b = 0

Where:
  w = weight vector (normal to the hyperplane)
  x = input feature vector
  b = bias (offset from origin)
  .  = dot product

Decision rule:
  If  w . x + b > 0  -->  class +1
  If  w . x + b < 0  -->  class -1

In 2D (features x1, x2):
  w1*x1 + w2*x2 + b = 0   (a straight line)

Logistic regression finds a hyperplane that separates the classes, but it does not care how far the boundary sits from the nearest data points. If the data is linearly separable, there are infinitely many hyperplanes that achieve perfect training accuracy. Logistic regression picks one based on maximising the likelihood — but is that the best one?

SVMs answer this question definitively: the best hyperplane is the one with the largest margin.

The Maximum Margin Hyperplane

The margin of a hyperplane is the distance between the hyperplane and the nearest data point from either class. The SVM formulation seeks the hyperplane that maximises this margin. Intuitively, a wider margin means the classifier is more confident — there is more room for new, unseen data to fall on the correct side.

Margin Geometry 
Distance from a point x_i to the hyperplane  w . x + b = 0:

  distance = |w . x_i + b| / ||w||

Where ||w|| is the Euclidean norm of w.

For correctly classified points with labels y_i in {-1, +1}:

  y_i * (w . x_i + b) >= 0   (correct side)

The margin (distance to the nearest point from either class):

  margin = 2 / ||w||   (after canonical scaling)

SVM objective:
  Maximise  2 / ||w||
  Subject to  y_i * (w . x_i + b) >= 1   for all i

Equivalent (easier to optimise):
  Minimise  (1/2) * ||w||^2
  Subject to  y_i * (w . x_i + b) >= 1   for all i

The constraint y_i * (w . x_i + b) >= 1 ensures that every training point lies on the correct side of the margin. The points for which this constraint is exactly tight — those sitting on the margin boundary — are called support vectors.

Engineering Analogy: Factor of Safety

The margin is analogous to a factor of safety in structural design. When you design a beam, you do not size it for the exact expected load — you add a safety factor. The wider the margin (higher the safety factor), the more robust the design is against unexpected variations. SVMs build in the maximum possible safety factor for the given data, which is why they tend to generalise well even with limited training samples.

Support Vectors: The Critical Few

A remarkable property of SVMs is that the optimal hyperplane depends only on the support vectors — the data points that lie exactly on the margin boundaries. All other points could be moved or even removed without changing the solution at all.

This is fundamentally different from logistic regression, where every data point contributes to the cost function and influences the boundary. In an SVM, if you have 1,000 training samples but only 15 of them are support vectors, the classifier is fully determined by those 15 points. This sparsity has practical advantages:

  • Memory efficiency — At prediction time, only the support vectors need to be stored, not the entire training set.
  • Interpretability — You can examine the support vectors to understand which samples the model considers borderline or difficult. In engineering terms, these are the edge cases.
  • Robustness — Adding more data that is far from the boundary does not change the model. Only data near the margin can affect it.
1

Positive Margin Boundary

The hyperplane w . x + b = +1 passes through the nearest positive-class support vectors. All positive-class points lie on or beyond this boundary.

2

Decision Boundary

The hyperplane w . x + b = 0 sits exactly halfway between the two margin boundaries. This is the actual decision surface used for classification.

3

Negative Margin Boundary

The hyperplane w . x + b = -1 passes through the nearest negative-class support vectors. All negative-class points lie on or beyond this boundary (on the opposite side).

The width of the "street" between the two margin boundaries is 2 / ||w||. Minimising ||w|| widens this street — hence the convex optimisation problem above.

Soft Margin: Handling Noisy Data

Real-world engineering data is rarely perfectly separable. Measurement errors, mislabelled samples, and genuine outliers mean that no hyperplane can correctly classify every training point. The hard margin SVM described above would simply fail — the optimisation problem becomes infeasible.

The soft margin formulation introduces slack variables (ξ_i) that allow some points to violate the margin or even sit on the wrong side of the boundary, at a penalty cost:

Soft Margin SVM 
Minimise:  (1/2) * ||w||^2  +  C * SUM(xi_i)

Subject to:
  y_i * (w . x_i + b) >= 1 - xi_i   for all i
  xi_i >= 0                           for all i

Where:
  xi_i = slack variable for sample i
       = 0 if sample is on or beyond the correct margin boundary
       = between 0 and 1 if inside the margin but correctly classified
       = > 1 if misclassified

  C = regularisation parameter that controls the trade-off:
      Large C  -->  low tolerance for violations (narrow margin, less bias)
      Small C  -->  high tolerance for violations (wide margin, more bias)

The C Parameter: Rigidity vs. Flexibility

The C parameter is the single most important hyperparameter in a linear SVM. It controls the trade-off between two competing objectives:

  • Large C (e.g., 1000): The model strongly penalises violations. It will try to classify every training point correctly, even if it means a very narrow margin. This is analogous to designing a structure with zero tolerance for deflection — precise but potentially brittle if your measurements have noise.
  • Small C (e.g., 0.01): The model is lenient about violations and prefers a wide margin, even if some training points are misclassified. This is like allowing generous deflection tolerances — more forgiving and robust, but potentially too loose for critical applications.

Bias-Variance Trade-off in SVMs

Large C leads to low bias, high variance — the model fits training data tightly but may not generalise. Small C leads to high bias, low variance — the model is smoother and more robust but may underfit. In civil engineering terms, you are choosing between a tight specification (high C) and a robust specification (low C). Cross-validation helps you find the right balance for your specific dataset.

In practice, C is typically chosen by cross-validation, often searching over a logarithmic grid such as [0.001, 0.01, 0.1, 1, 10, 100, 1000]. The optimal C depends on the noise level in the data and the degree to which the classes overlap.

Practical Tip

Always standardise your features (zero mean, unit variance) before training an SVM. Because the margin depends on the Euclidean norm of w, features with large numerical ranges will dominate the optimisation. In civil engineering data, compressive strength (in MPa) and length (in mm) can differ by orders of magnitude. Standardisation ensures every feature contributes proportionally.

The Kernel Trick: Non-Linear Boundaries

Many real-world problems are not linearly separable. Consider predicting whether a soil will liquefy during an earthquake: the safe and unsafe zones in a plot of shear stress vs. relative density are separated by a curved boundary, not a straight line. A linear SVM would fail here.

The kernel trick is the mathematical insight that makes SVMs extraordinarily powerful. The idea is:

1

Map to a Higher Dimension

Transform the original features into a much higher-dimensional space using a mapping function φ(x). In this new space, the data that was not linearly separable may become linearly separable. For example, 2D data with a circular boundary can be separated by a hyperplane in 3D.

2

Use a Kernel Function Instead

Computing φ(x) explicitly can be prohibitively expensive (the mapped space might be infinite-dimensional). The kernel trick avoids this by noting that the SVM optimisation only needs dot products between data points: φ(x_i) . φ(x_j). A kernel function K(x_i, x_j) computes this dot product directly in the original space, without ever constructing the high-dimensional representation.

3

Find a Linear Boundary in the Mapped Space

The SVM finds a maximum-margin hyperplane in the high-dimensional kernel space. When projected back to the original feature space, this hyperplane appears as a non-linear decision boundary — a curve, a closed region, or an arbitrarily complex shape.

Kernel Functions 
The SVM dual formulation depends only on dot products K(x_i, x_j):

1. Linear Kernel:
   K(x_i, x_j) = x_i . x_j
   Equivalent to no transformation. Good when data is already linearly separable.

2. Polynomial Kernel:
   K(x_i, x_j) = (gamma * x_i . x_j + r)^d
   Maps to polynomial feature space of degree d.
   Captures interactions between features up to order d.

3. Radial Basis Function (RBF / Gaussian) Kernel:
   K(x_i, x_j) = exp(-gamma * ||x_i - x_j||^2)
   Maps to infinite-dimensional space.
   Creates smooth, localised decision boundaries.
   gamma controls the radius of influence of each support vector.

4. Sigmoid Kernel:
   K(x_i, x_j) = tanh(gamma * x_i . x_j + r)
   Related to neural networks (two-layer perceptron).
   Less commonly used in practice.

The RBF Kernel: The Swiss Army Knife

The Radial Basis Function (RBF) kernel is the most widely used kernel in practice. It implicitly maps data to an infinite-dimensional space and creates smooth, flexible decision boundaries. The key parameter is gamma:

  • Large gamma: Each support vector has a small radius of influence. The boundary tightly wraps around individual points, potentially overfitting. In engineering terms, this is like fitting a spline through every data point.
  • Small gamma: Each support vector has a broad radius of influence. The boundary is smooth and generalises well, but may underfit. This is like fitting a simple curve through scattered data.

Intuition: Gaussian Mountains

Imagine placing a Gaussian "mountain" (bell curve) on top of each support vector. Gamma controls how steep these mountains are. The decision boundary runs along the "valleys" between the positive and negative mountains. With large gamma, you get sharp, narrow peaks and the boundary weaves between individual points. With small gamma, the mountains are broad and gentle, and the boundary is a smooth contour separating the two groups of peaks.

In practice, the two hyperparameters C and gamma are tuned simultaneously, typically using a grid search with cross-validation. A common strategy is to search over C ∈ {0.1, 1, 10, 100, 1000} and gamma ∈ {0.001, 0.01, 0.1, 1, 10}.

Support Vector Regression (SVR)

SVMs are not limited to classification. Support Vector Regression (SVR) adapts the maximum-margin idea to predict continuous values. Instead of finding a hyperplane that separates classes, SVR finds a hyperplane (or curved surface with kernels) that fits the data within a specified tolerance band.

SVR Formulation 
SVR Objective:
  Minimise  (1/2) * ||w||^2  +  C * SUM(xi_i + xi_i*)

Subject to:
  y_i - (w . x_i + b) <= epsilon + xi_i
  (w . x_i + b) - y_i <= epsilon + xi_i*
  xi_i, xi_i* >= 0

Where:
  epsilon = the width of the tolerance tube (epsilon-insensitive zone)
            Errors within epsilon are ignored (zero loss)
  xi_i    = slack for points above the tube
  xi_i*   = slack for points below the tube
  C       = penalty for points outside the tube

Key idea:
  - Points inside the epsilon-tube contribute zero loss
  - Only points outside the tube are penalised
  - Support vectors are points on or outside the tube boundary

The epsilon parameter defines a "tube" around the regression function. Any prediction within epsilon of the true value is considered perfect (zero error). Only data points outside this tube become support vectors and influence the model. This makes SVR robust to small measurement noise — a valuable property when working with civil engineering test data that inherently contains experimental scatter.

SVR for Concrete Strength Prediction

Consider predicting the 28-day compressive strength of concrete from mix design parameters (water-cement ratio, aggregate content, admixture dosage, curing temperature). SVR with an RBF kernel can capture the non-linear relationships between these variables while ignoring small experimental variations (within the epsilon tube). The support vectors correspond to the critical mix designs that define the model's prediction surface — the boundary cases where small changes in the mix significantly affect strength.

SVR shares the same kernel options as SVM classification (linear, polynomial, RBF). The choice of kernel depends on the expected relationship between features and the target variable. For most civil engineering regression tasks, the RBF kernel is a strong default choice because it can approximate any continuous function given enough support vectors.

CE Application: Earthquake Damage Classification

After a major earthquake, rapid damage assessment of buildings is critical for public safety and recovery planning. Engineers must inspect thousands of structures and classify each into damage grades (e.g., the European Macroseismic Scale: D0 through D5). SVMs can automate this classification using features extracted from sensor data, structural characteristics, and ground motion parameters.

Feature Set for Damage Classification

A typical SVM model for earthquake damage classification might use the following features:

  • Structural: Number of storeys, construction material (encoded), age, plan irregularity index, soft-storey indicator
  • Ground motion: Peak ground acceleration (PGA), spectral acceleration at the building's fundamental period, Arias intensity, duration
  • Geotechnical: Site class (soil type), distance to fault rupture, liquefaction susceptibility index

For multi-class problems (D0 through D5), SVMs use a one-vs-one or one-vs-rest strategy. One-vs-one trains a binary SVM for every pair of classes (15 classifiers for 6 classes) and uses majority voting. One-vs-rest trains one SVM per class (6 classifiers) and assigns the class with the highest decision function value.

Research has shown that SVMs with RBF kernels achieve classification accuracies of 75-85% on earthquake damage data, outperforming logistic regression and performing comparably to random forests. The key advantage of SVMs in this context is their robustness to class imbalance (most buildings suffer low damage, few suffer high damage) when combined with class-weighted penalties.

Class Imbalance Handling

When damage classes are imbalanced (e.g., 1000 D0 samples but only 30 D5 samples), use class-weighted SVM: set class_weight='balanced' in scikit-learn. This automatically adjusts the C parameter for each class inversely proportional to its frequency, preventing the model from simply predicting the majority class for everything.

CE Application: Soil Liquefaction Prediction

Soil liquefaction — the phenomenon where saturated, loose, granular soil temporarily loses its strength and behaves like a fluid during seismic shaking — is one of the most dangerous geotechnical hazards. It can cause buildings to sink, embankments to slide, and underground structures to float. Predicting whether liquefaction will occur at a site given specific seismic loading is a classic binary classification problem.

Traditional vs. SVM Approach

The traditional approach uses empirical charts that plot the Cyclic Stress Ratio (CSR) against corrected SPT blow count (N1)60 or CPT tip resistance (qc1N). A manually drawn boundary curve separates "liquefy" and "no liquefy" cases based on historical earthquake case studies. SVMs can learn this boundary from data, potentially capturing non-linear interactions that the hand-drawn curve misses, such as the effect of fines content, overburden stress, and earthquake magnitude.

The features for an SVM liquefaction model typically include:

  • CSR — Cyclic stress ratio (seismic demand)
  • (N1)60 — Corrected SPT blow count (soil resistance)
  • Fines content (FC) — Percentage of particles finer than 0.075 mm
  • Effective overburden stress (σ'v)
  • Earthquake magnitude (Mw)
  • Depth to groundwater table

The RBF kernel is particularly well-suited here because the boundary between liquefiable and non-liquefiable conditions is smooth but non-linear. Studies have shown that SVM models achieve classification accuracies exceeding 90% on liquefaction databases, with the decision boundary closely matching the expert-drawn curves while also capturing subtle effects that the empirical charts ignore.

Why Margin Matters for Safety

In liquefaction prediction, the margin of the SVM has a direct engineering interpretation. A site that falls deep inside the "no liquefy" region (far from the boundary) is confidently safe. A site near the decision boundary — inside the margin — is uncertain and warrants further investigation (e.g., additional boreholes, dynamic analysis). The SVM's decision function value provides a continuous measure of this confidence, analogous to a factor of safety.

Understanding Kernels: A Visual Walkthrough

The kernel trick can feel abstract until you see it in action. Let us walk through a concrete example. Consider two features describing a soil sample: moisture content (x-axis) and density (y-axis). The "liquefiable" samples cluster in a ring-shaped region in the centre, while the "non-liquefiable" samples surround them. No straight line can separate the two classes.

Linear Kernel: When a Line Suffices

The linear kernel K(x_i, x_j) = x_i . x_j is equivalent to the standard SVM without any transformation. It works well when the classes are approximately separable by a hyperplane — for example, when distinguishing between two grades of steel based on yield strength and elongation percentage. It is fast, interpretable, and should always be the first kernel you try.

Polynomial Kernel: Capturing Feature Interactions

The polynomial kernel K(x_i, x_j) = (gamma * x_i . x_j + r)^d maps data into a space that includes all polynomial combinations of the original features up to degree d. A degree-2 polynomial kernel on two features (x1, x2) implicitly creates features like x1^2, x2^2, and x1*x2 — enabling the SVM to learn elliptical and parabolic boundaries.

This is useful when you suspect that the interaction between features matters. For example, the combined effect of water-cement ratio and curing temperature on concrete strength is not simply additive; there is a synergistic (polynomial) interaction that a linear model would miss.

RBF Kernel: Infinite Flexibility

The RBF kernel K(x_i, x_j) = exp(-gamma * ||x_i - x_j||^2) measures the similarity between two points based on their Euclidean distance. When two points are close, the kernel value is near 1; when they are far apart, it decays toward 0. The parameter gamma controls how quickly this decay happens.

The RBF kernel implicitly maps to an infinite-dimensional space, which means it can learn arbitrarily complex boundaries. It is the default choice in scikit-learn and works well for most problems. The two hyperparameters (C and gamma) must be tuned together via grid search or random search with cross-validation.

scikit-learn SVM Example 
from sklearn.svm import SVC
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
from sklearn.model_selection import GridSearchCV
from sklearn.pipeline import Pipeline

# Build a pipeline: standardise, then SVM
pipe = Pipeline([
    ('scaler', StandardScaler()),
    ('svm', SVC(kernel='rbf'))
])

# Grid search over C and gamma
param_grid = {
    'svm__C':     [0.1, 1, 10, 100, 1000],
    'svm__gamma': [0.001, 0.01, 0.1, 1, 10]
}

grid = GridSearchCV(pipe, param_grid, cv=5, scoring='accuracy')
grid.fit(X_train, y_train)

print(f"Best C:     {grid.best_params_['svm__C']}")
print(f"Best gamma: {grid.best_params_['svm__gamma']}")
print(f"Best CV accuracy: {grid.best_score_:.3f}")

When to Choose SVM

SVMs shine when you have a small-to-medium dataset (up to ~10,000 samples), high-dimensional features, and you need a non-linear boundary with good generalisation. They are particularly popular in geotechnical engineering where datasets come from field tests (expensive, limited samples) and the relationships are complex. For very large datasets (>100,000 samples), tree-based ensembles or neural networks are usually more practical due to SVM's cubic training complexity.

Practical Considerations

Training an SVM involves several practical decisions beyond choosing the kernel. Here are the most important ones for civil engineering practitioners:

Feature Scaling

SVMs are not scale-invariant. Features with large numerical ranges will dominate the distance calculations in the kernel function. Always standardise (zero mean, unit variance) or normalise (min-max to [0, 1]) your features. In civil engineering, this is especially important because features like compressive strength (20-80 MPa), modulus of elasticity (20,000-40,000 MPa), and Poisson's ratio (0.15-0.25) span vastly different ranges.

Hyperparameter Tuning

The performance of an SVM depends heavily on the choice of C and gamma (for the RBF kernel). A systematic approach:

1

Coarse Grid Search

Start with a wide logarithmic grid: C = [10^-3, 10^-2, ..., 10^3] and gamma = [10^-3, 10^-2, ..., 10^3]. Use 5-fold cross-validation to evaluate each combination.

2

Fine Grid Search

Narrow the search around the best region found in step 1. For example, if the best coarse result was C=10, gamma=0.1, search C = [5, 7, 10, 15, 20] and gamma = [0.05, 0.08, 0.1, 0.15, 0.2].

3

Final Evaluation

Train the final model with the best hyperparameters on the full training set and evaluate on the held-out test set. Report the test accuracy, not the cross-validation accuracy.

Multi-Class Classification

SVMs are inherently binary classifiers. For multi-class problems (like damage grades D0-D5), two strategies are common:

  • One-vs-One (OvO): Train K*(K-1)/2 binary classifiers, one for each pair of classes. Classify by majority vote. Default in scikit-learn's SVC.
  • One-vs-Rest (OvR): Train K binary classifiers, each distinguishing one class from all others. Classify by highest decision function value. Default in scikit-learn's LinearSVC.

Probability Estimation

Unlike logistic regression, SVMs do not naturally output probabilities. The raw output is a decision function value — the signed distance from the hyperplane. To convert this to a probability, Platt scaling fits a sigmoid function to the decision values using cross-validation. In scikit-learn, set probability=True, but be aware this adds training time and the probabilities are approximate.

Advantages & Limitations

Advantages

  • Excellent generalisation on small datasets due to the maximum-margin principle and structural risk minimisation theory.
  • Effective in high dimensions — SVMs work well even when the number of features exceeds the number of samples, a situation common in sensor-rich structural health monitoring.
  • Kernel flexibility — The same algorithm handles linear and non-linear problems by simply changing the kernel.
  • Sparse solution — Only support vectors are needed at prediction time, reducing memory requirements.
  • Robust to overfitting when C and gamma are properly tuned, thanks to the regularisation built into the margin.

Limitations

  • Slow on large datasets — Training complexity is O(n^2) to O(n^3) in the number of samples. Datasets with more than 50,000 samples become impractical without approximations.
  • Sensitive to feature scaling — Forgetting to standardise features is the most common SVM mistake.
  • Limited interpretability with non-linear kernels — You cannot easily extract "rules" from an RBF SVM the way you can from a decision tree.
  • No native probability output — Platt scaling is an approximation and can be unreliable for small datasets.
  • Hyperparameter sensitivity — Performance depends strongly on the choice of C and gamma. Grid search is computationally expensive for large parameter spaces.

CE Applications: SVM in Water Resources

Beyond geotechnical problems, SVMs have proven highly effective in water resources engineering where complex, non-linear decision boundaries are common.

Water Resources SVM Applications

  • Flood vs. non-flood classification — RBF kernel SVM separates flood events from normal flows using antecedent rainfall, river stage, soil moisture, and upstream discharge. The non-linear boundary captures threshold effects that logistic regression misses.
  • Streamflow forecasting (SVR) — Support Vector Regression predicts daily discharge from lagged rainfall, temperature, and snowmelt variables. SVR handles the non-linear rainfall-runoff transformation well.
  • Water quality anomaly detection — One-class SVM trained on normal water quality data detects contamination events (turbidity spikes, pH anomalies) in real-time sensor data.
  • Drought classification — Multi-class SVM classifies drought severity (D0–D4) from SPI, PDSI, soil moisture, and vegetation index. Kernel trick captures non-linear climate-drought relationships.

Quick Check 1

An SVM is trained on 500 data points and the optimal solution has 12 support vectors. If you remove a training point that is NOT a support vector and retrain, what happens to the decision boundary?

Correct! The SVM solution depends only on the support vectors. Removing any non-support-vector point has zero effect on the decision boundary or margin. This is a key property of SVMs and one reason they generalise well.
Not quite. The SVM decision boundary depends only on the support vectors. Removing a non-support-vector point has absolutely no effect on the boundary, margin, or number of support vectors.

Quick Check 2

You are training an SVM with an RBF kernel on a soil liquefaction dataset. With C=0.01, the model achieves 60% accuracy. With C=10000, it achieves 98% training accuracy but only 55% test accuracy. What is the most likely explanation?

Correct! C=0.01 gives a very wide margin that allows many misclassifications (underfitting), while C=10000 forces the model to classify every training point correctly at the cost of a very narrow, complex boundary (overfitting). The optimal C lies somewhere in between and should be found via cross-validation.
Not quite. This is a classic bias-variance trade-off scenario. Small C = underfitting (high bias), large C = overfitting (high variance). The solution is to find an intermediate C via cross-validation.

Quick Check 3

Why is feature scaling critical before training an SVM, but not necessary for decision trees?

Correct! SVM kernels compute dot products or Euclidean distances between feature vectors. A feature measured in thousands (e.g., modulus in MPa) will dominate one measured in fractions (e.g., Poisson's ratio). Decision trees, by contrast, split on one feature at a time using thresholds, so the absolute scale of each feature does not affect the split quality.
Not quite. The key insight is that SVMs use distance and dot-product calculations across all features simultaneously, so scale matters. Decision trees consider one feature at a time and only care about the ordering of values, not their magnitude.

Quick Check 4

In Support Vector Regression (SVR), what is the role of the epsilon parameter?

Correct! Epsilon defines the width of the insensitive tube around the regression function. Any prediction within epsilon of the true value is considered "good enough" and incurs zero loss. Only points outside this tube become support vectors and contribute to the loss. This makes SVR robust to small measurement noise in engineering test data.
Not quite. Epsilon defines the epsilon-insensitive tube around the regression function. Errors smaller than epsilon are ignored (zero loss). Only data points outside this tube are penalised and become support vectors.

Key Takeaways

  • SVMs find the maximum-margin hyperplane — the decision boundary with the largest buffer zone between classes, providing built-in generalisation guarantees.
  • Support vectors are the critical data points on the margin boundaries. The entire model is defined by these few points; all other data is irrelevant to the boundary.
  • The C parameter controls the trade-off between margin width and misclassification tolerance. Large C = tight fit (risk of overfitting); small C = wide margin (risk of underfitting).
  • The kernel trick enables SVMs to learn non-linear boundaries by implicitly mapping data to higher-dimensional spaces. The RBF kernel is the most flexible and commonly used.
  • SVR extends SVMs to regression with an epsilon-insensitive loss function that ignores small prediction errors, providing robustness to measurement noise.
  • In civil engineering, SVMs excel at earthquake damage classification (multi-class with class imbalance) and soil liquefaction prediction (non-linear boundary, small dataset, safety-critical).
