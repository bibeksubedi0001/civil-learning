Chapter 2 — Sub-chapter 2.2

Linear Regression

The simplest and most powerful predictive model in engineering — draw a straight line through data and use it to predict the future. From concrete strength to settlement estimation, linear regression is where every engineer's ML journey begins.

35 min read 2 interactive demos 4 quiz questions

The Line That Predicts Concrete Strength

Every civil engineer knows that increasing the water-cement (w/c) ratio weakens concrete. You learned this in your materials course, verified it in the lab, and apply it on every project. But have you ever asked: by exactly how much? If the w/c ratio goes from 0.45 to 0.50, what is the expected drop in 28-day compressive strength?

Linear regression answers that question with mathematical precision. It takes your laboratory data — dozens or hundreds of test results linking w/c ratio to compressive strength — and finds the single straight line that best describes the relationship. That line becomes a predictive tool: feed it any w/c ratio, and it returns the expected strength, along with a measure of how confident you should be in that prediction.

Linear regression is the foundation of predictive modelling. It is the simplest supervised learning algorithm, yet it remains one of the most widely used in engineering practice. Understanding it deeply — not just the formula, but the geometry, the optimisation, and the diagnostics — will prepare you for every model that follows, because more complex algorithms are built on the same principles.

Prerequisites

This sub-chapter assumes you have completed Sub-chapter 2.1 (Supervised Learning Fundamentals) and understand the concepts of features, labels, training data, and loss functions. Basic algebra and familiarity with coordinate geometry (plotting points on an x-y axis) are helpful.

The Equation: y = mx + b

Linear regression boils down to finding the best straight line through a set of data points. That line is described by the equation you first met in secondary school:

The Linear Equation 
y = m * x + b

where:
  y = predicted output (e.g., compressive strength in MPa)
  x = input feature  (e.g., water-cement ratio)
  m = slope          (how much y changes per unit change in x)
  b = y-intercept    (the value of y when x = 0)

In machine learning notation, you will often see this written as ŷ = w1x + w0, where w1 is the weight (slope) and w0 is the bias (intercept). The hat on ŷ indicates it is a predicted value, not the actual measured value. Do not let the notation change confuse you — it is the same equation.

Civil Engineering Analogy

The linear equation is like Abrams' Law for concrete. Duff Abrams showed in 1918 that concrete strength is inversely related to the water-cement ratio. His empirical formula is essentially a regression model fitted to laboratory data. Linear regression formalises and generalises this process: instead of deriving a formula by hand, the algorithm finds the optimal parameters automatically from your data.

Understanding Slope and Intercept

The slope (m) tells you the rate of change. In our concrete example, if m = -65, that means for every 0.01 increase in the w/c ratio, the predicted compressive strength drops by 0.65 MPa. A negative slope confirms what engineers already know: more water means weaker concrete.

The intercept (b) is the predicted value when the input is zero. For concrete strength vs. w/c ratio, the intercept has no physical meaning (you cannot have a w/c ratio of zero and still have concrete), but it is mathematically necessary to position the line correctly. This is common in engineering — extrapolation beyond the data range is always dangerous.

Extrapolation Warning

A regression model is only reliable within the range of the training data. If your data covers w/c ratios from 0.35 to 0.65, do not use the model to predict strength at w/c = 0.20 or 0.85. The relationship may be non-linear outside the observed range. This is a universal rule in engineering modelling, not unique to machine learning.

CE Context: Predicting Concrete Compressive Strength

Let us ground the theory in a real civil engineering problem. We have a dataset of concrete specimens, each tested at 28 days. For each specimen, we recorded the water-cement ratio (our feature, x) and the resulting compressive strength in MPa (our label, y). Our goal: fit a line that best describes this relationship, then use that line to predict the strength of future mixes.

Sample Dataset: Water-Cement Ratio vs. 28-Day Strength

Here is a simplified dataset that we will use throughout this sub-chapter:

Specimen w/c Ratio (x) Strength MPa (y)
C-01 0.35 48.2
C-02 0.40 43.5
C-03 0.45 39.1
C-04 0.50 34.8
C-05 0.55 31.0
C-06 0.60 27.5
C-07 0.65 23.8

Notice the clear trend: as the w/c ratio increases, compressive strength decreases. The relationship is approximately linear within this range.

If you plotted these seven points on a graph, you would see them falling roughly along a straight line — but not perfectly. Real data always has scatter caused by variations in aggregate quality, curing conditions, testing equipment, and human error. The goal of linear regression is to find the line that best represents the underlying trend despite this scatter.

The Least Squares Method

How do we define "best" when fitting a line? We need a mathematical criterion that measures how well a candidate line fits the data. The most widely used criterion is the method of least squares, developed by Legendre and Gauss over two centuries ago.

Residuals: The Distance Between Prediction and Reality

For each data point, the residual is the vertical distance between the actual value (yi) and the predicted value (ŷi) on the regression line:

Residual Formula 
residual_i = y_i - y_hat_i
           = y_i - (m * x_i + b)

A positive residual means the data point is above the line (the model underestimates). A negative residual means the point is below the line (the model overestimates). The best line minimises these residuals — but we cannot just sum them, because positive and negative values would cancel out.

1

Compute each residual

For every data point, subtract the predicted ŷ from the actual y. This gives you a list of errors — some positive, some negative.

2

Square each residual

Squaring eliminates negative signs and penalises large errors more heavily than small ones. A point that is off by 10 MPa contributes 100 to the sum, while a point off by 2 MPa contributes only 4.

3

Sum the squared residuals

The total is the Sum of Squared Errors (SSE). The line that produces the smallest SSE is the best-fit line. This is the "least squares" criterion.

Civil Engineering Analogy

Least squares is like surveying with a total station. When you measure elevations along a proposed road alignment, no single measurement is perfectly accurate. To design the road profile, you fit a smooth curve that minimises the overall deviation from all measured points. You do not force the curve through every point (that would follow the noise), but you find the profile that best represents the true ground level. Least squares does the same thing mathematically.

The Cost Function: Mean Squared Error

In machine learning, the quantity we minimise is called the cost function (also called the loss function or objective function). For linear regression, the standard cost function is the Mean Squared Error (MSE):

Mean Squared Error 
MSE = (1/n) * SUM[ (y_i - y_hat_i)^2 ]

where:
  n      = number of data points
  y_i    = actual measured value for point i
  y_hat_i = predicted value (m * x_i + b) for point i

MSE is simply the average of the squared residuals. It has the same units as y2 (MPa2 in our concrete example), which can make interpretation tricky. That is why you will sometimes see the Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) — the square root of MSE — which returns the error to the original units (MPa).

The Cost Surface: A Bowl-Shaped Landscape

Imagine plotting MSE as a function of the two parameters m (slope) and b (intercept). For every pair (m, b), you get a single MSE value. If you plot all possible MSE values in 3D, you get a bowl-shaped surface (technically a paraboloid). The bottom of the bowl represents the optimal m and b — the values that minimise the error.

Finding the minimum of this bowl is the entire goal of training a linear regression model. There are two ways to do it: the closed-form solution (normal equations) and the iterative approach (gradient descent). Both reach the same answer, but gradient descent scales to problems with millions of features where closed-form solutions are computationally impractical.

Why "Cost" Function?

The name comes from optimisation theory. Think of the MSE as the "cost" of being wrong. High MSE = high cost = bad model. Low MSE = low cost = good model. Training is the process of reducing this cost. Civil engineers already think in terms of cost optimisation — minimising material cost subject to strength constraints. ML optimisation is conceptually identical: minimise prediction error subject to the model structure.

Gradient Descent: Walking Downhill

Gradient descent is the workhorse algorithm for training machine learning models. It finds the minimum of the cost function by taking iterative steps in the direction of steepest descent — like a ball rolling to the bottom of a bowl.

1

Initialise parameters

Start with random (or zero) values for the slope m and intercept b. At this point, the model's predictions are essentially random, and the MSE is high. You are at a random position on the bowl-shaped cost surface.

2

Compute the gradient

The gradient is the vector of partial derivatives of the cost function with respect to each parameter. It points in the direction of steepest ascent. We want to go the opposite direction (downhill), so we subtract the gradient.

3

Update parameters

Adjust m and b by subtracting a fraction of the gradient. The fraction is controlled by the learning rate (alpha) — a small positive number (e.g., 0.01) that determines the step size.

4

Repeat until convergence

Keep computing gradients and updating parameters. With each iteration, the MSE decreases and the model improves. Stop when the improvement becomes negligibly small (convergence) or after a set number of iterations.

Gradient Descent Update Rules 
// For each iteration:
gradient_m = (2/n) * SUM[ x_i * (y_hat_i - y_i) ]
gradient_b = (2/n) * SUM[ (y_hat_i - y_i) ]

m = m - learning_rate * gradient_m
b = b - learning_rate * gradient_b
Civil Engineering Analogy

Gradient descent is like grading a site to find the lowest point. Imagine you are standing on hilly terrain in dense fog and need to find the lowest elevation. You cannot see the whole landscape, but you can feel the slope under your feet. At each step, you walk in the direction where the ground slopes downward most steeply. Eventually, you reach the valley floor — the minimum. The learning rate is your step length: too large and you overshoot the valley; too small and you take forever to get there.

The Learning Rate: A Critical Hyperparameter

The learning rate controls how aggressively the model updates its parameters. It is a hyperparameter — a value you set before training, not learned from the data.

  • Too large — The model overshoots the minimum, bouncing back and forth across the valley or even diverging to infinity. Like a bulldozer that takes steps too big for the terrain.
  • Too small — The model creeps toward the minimum agonisingly slowly, wasting computation. Like surveying millimetre by millimetre when metre-level steps would suffice.
  • Just right — The model converges efficiently to the minimum in a reasonable number of iterations. Typical values range from 0.001 to 0.1.

R-Squared: Measuring Model Quality

Once we have fitted a line, we need to know: how good is it? The MSE tells us the average squared error, but its magnitude depends on the scale of the data. A model predicting concrete strength in MPa might have an MSE of 4.0, while a model predicting settlement in millimetres might have an MSE of 0.5 — but which model is better relative to its problem?

Enter R-squared (R2), also called the coefficient of determination. It measures the proportion of variance in the label that is explained by the model, on a scale from 0 to 1.

R-Squared Formula 
R^2 = 1 - (SS_res / SS_tot)

where:
  SS_res = SUM[ (y_i - y_hat_i)^2 ]   (residual sum of squares)
  SS_tot = SUM[ (y_i - y_mean)^2 ]    (total sum of squares)

  y_mean = average of all actual y values

Interpreting R-Squared

  • R2 = 1.0 — The model explains 100% of the variance. Every prediction matches the actual value exactly. This almost never happens with real data.
  • R2 = 0.85 — The model explains 85% of the variance in the data. This is excellent for most engineering applications.
  • R2 = 0.50 — The model explains only half the variance. Significant patterns remain uncaptured. You might need more features or a non-linear model.
  • R2 = 0.0 — The model is no better than simply predicting the mean for every data point. The features have no linear relationship with the label.
R-Squared Limitations

R2 always increases (or stays the same) when you add more features, even if those features are irrelevant. This is why the Adjusted R2 exists — it penalises unnecessary features. Also, a high R2 does not guarantee the model is correct. The relationship might be non-linear, and a straight line might still achieve a decent R2 while missing important curvature. Always examine the residuals.

Residual Analysis: Diagnosing Your Model

Fitting a line is only half the job. A responsible engineer always checks the residuals — the differences between actual and predicted values — to verify that the model's assumptions hold. Residual analysis is the engineering equivalent of quality control.

What Good Residuals Look Like

  • Randomly scattered around zero — No systematic pattern. Points above and below the line are evenly distributed. This indicates the linear model captures the true relationship.
  • Constant spread (homoscedasticity) — The residuals have roughly the same magnitude across all values of x. If they fan out (larger errors at higher x values), the model may need transformation.
  • Approximately normal distribution — A histogram of residuals should look roughly bell-shaped. This validates confidence intervals and hypothesis tests.

Warning Signs in Residuals

  • Curved pattern — If residuals form a U-shape or inverted U, the true relationship is non-linear. A polynomial regression (covered in Sub-chapter 2.3) may be more appropriate.
  • Funnel shape — Increasing spread suggests heteroscedasticity. Consider a log transformation or weighted regression.
  • Outliers — Points with extremely large residuals deserve investigation. They may be data entry errors, unusual specimens, or genuine anomalies that reveal important engineering phenomena.
Civil Engineering Analogy

Residual analysis is like checking theodolite closing errors. After completing a traverse survey, you compute the closing error to assess accuracy. If the error is small and random, the survey is acceptable. If the error shows a systematic pattern (always drifting in one direction), something is wrong with the instrument or technique. Residuals serve the same diagnostic purpose for regression models.

Putting It All Together: The Complete Workflow

Let us trace the entire linear regression workflow from start to finish, using our concrete strength prediction problem:

1

Collect and prepare data

Gather laboratory test results: w/c ratio (feature) and 28-day compressive strength (label) for each specimen. Clean the data — remove obviously erroneous entries, handle missing values, and verify units.

2

Visualise the data

Plot a scatter diagram of x vs y. Does the relationship look linear? Are there obvious outliers? This visual check is essential before fitting a model — just as you would inspect a soil sample before testing it.

3

Fit the model (find m and b)

Use least squares (closed-form) or gradient descent (iterative) to find the slope and intercept that minimise the MSE. Record the equation: e.g., Strength = -81.3 * (w/c) + 76.8.

4

Evaluate the model

Compute R2, RMSE, and examine the residuals. Is the R2 high enough for your application? Are residuals random? If not, revisit your assumptions.

5

Make predictions

Use the fitted equation to predict strength for new mix designs. For example, at w/c = 0.48: Strength = -81.3 * 0.48 + 76.8 = 37.8 MPa. Always report the prediction alongside the R2 and RMSE so stakeholders understand the uncertainty.

Complete Workflow in Pseudocode 
// 1. Data
x = [0.35, 0.40, 0.45, 0.50, 0.55, 0.60, 0.65]
y = [48.2, 43.5, 39.1, 34.8, 31.0, 27.5, 23.8]

// 2. Compute means
x_mean = average(x)    // 0.50
y_mean = average(y)    // 35.41

// 3. Compute slope (m) and intercept (b)
numerator   = SUM[ (x_i - x_mean) * (y_i - y_mean) ]
denominator = SUM[ (x_i - x_mean)^2 ]
m = numerator / denominator   // approx -81.3
b = y_mean - m * x_mean       // approx 76.1

// 4. Predict
for w_c_ratio = 0.48:
    predicted_strength = m * 0.48 + b  // approx 37.1 MPa

// 5. Evaluate
R_squared = 1 - SS_res / SS_tot   // approx 0.998

Beyond One Feature: Multiple Linear Regression

So far, we have used a single feature (w/c ratio) to predict concrete strength. But concrete strength depends on many factors: cement content, aggregate type, superplasticiser dosage, curing temperature, and more. Multiple linear regression extends the single-feature model to handle multiple inputs simultaneously:

Multiple Linear Regression 
y_hat = w_0 + w_1 * x_1 + w_2 * x_2 + ... + w_p * x_p

Example for concrete strength:
  Strength = w_0
           + w_1 * (cement content)
           + w_2 * (w/c ratio)
           + w_3 * (fine aggregate)
           + w_4 * (coarse aggregate)
           + w_5 * (superplasticiser)
           + w_6 * (age in days)

The principles are identical — we still minimise MSE, still use gradient descent (now with more parameters to update), and still evaluate with R2. The only difference is that instead of a 2D line, we are fitting a hyperplane in multi-dimensional space. You cannot visualise it beyond three dimensions, but the mathematics works exactly the same way.

Feature Scaling Matters

When features have vastly different scales (e.g., cement content in hundreds of kg/m3 vs. w/c ratio between 0.3 and 0.7), gradient descent can struggle. The cost surface becomes elongated (like an ellipse rather than a circle), causing the algorithm to zigzag inefficiently.

The solution is feature scaling: normalise all features to a similar range (e.g., 0 to 1) before training. This is analogous to converting all measurements to consistent units before performing structural analysis — you would not mix metres and millimetres in the same stiffness matrix.

Practical Tips for Civil Engineers

Tip 1: Start Simple

Always start with a simple linear regression before trying complex models. If a straight line achieves R2 = 0.92, you may not need a neural network. Simple models are easier to interpret, validate, and explain to non-technical stakeholders — a critical advantage when presenting to project managers or regulatory bodies.

Tip 2: Check Your Assumptions

Linear regression assumes a linear relationship, independent errors, constant variance, and normally distributed residuals. If these assumptions are violated, the model's predictions and confidence intervals may be unreliable. Always plot residuals versus predicted values and check for patterns.

Tip 3: Domain Knowledge is Your Superpower

As a civil engineer, you have something most data scientists lack: deep domain expertise. You know that concrete strength cannot be negative, that settlement increases with load, and that certain features are physically correlated. Use this knowledge to sanity-check your model. If the regression equation predicts negative strength at reasonable w/c ratios, something is wrong — do not blindly trust the mathematics.

Tip 4: Report Uncertainty

Never present a single predicted value without context. Always report the RMSE, R2, and ideally a confidence interval. Saying "predicted strength = 37 MPa" is incomplete. Saying "predicted strength = 37 +/- 3 MPa (R2 = 0.95)" tells the engineer how much to trust the prediction. This is no different from reporting survey coordinates with their estimated accuracy.

Test Your Understanding

Answer the following questions to check your grasp of linear regression concepts. Each question relates to civil engineering applications covered in this sub-chapter.

Quick Check 1

In the linear regression equation y = mx + b for predicting concrete strength from water-cement ratio, what does the slope (m) represent?

Correct! The slope (m) represents the rate of change — how many MPa of strength change for each unit change in the w/c ratio. A negative slope confirms that higher w/c ratios lead to lower strength.
Not quite. The slope (m) represents the rate of change — the expected change in strength per unit change in w/c ratio. Option A describes the mean, option C describes the intercept (b), and option D is a separate metric.

Quick Check 2

Why does the least squares method square the residuals instead of simply summing them?

Correct! Squaring serves two purposes: it eliminates negative signs (so positive and negative errors do not cancel) and it penalises larger errors disproportionately (an error of 10 contributes 100, not 10, to the total).
Not quite. Squaring the residuals prevents positive and negative residuals from cancelling out and penalises large errors more heavily. Without squaring, a point 10 MPa above the line and another 10 MPa below would sum to zero — suggesting a perfect fit when it is clearly not.

Quick Check 3

In gradient descent, what happens if the learning rate is set too high?

Correct! A learning rate that is too high causes the algorithm to take steps that are too large, overshooting the minimum of the cost surface and potentially bouncing back and forth or diverging to infinity. Think of it like a bulldozer taking 10-metre steps on a narrow valley floor.
Not quite. A high learning rate causes overshooting and potential divergence. The steps are too large for the curvature of the cost surface, causing the algorithm to bounce past the minimum rather than settling into it.

Quick Check 4

A linear regression model predicting concrete compressive strength from w/c ratio yields R2 = 0.03. What does this tell you?

Correct! An R2 of 0.03 means the model explains only 3% of the variance in the data. The w/c ratio alone is barely better than predicting the mean for every specimen. This could indicate the data is noisy, the relationship is non-linear, or other factors dominate strength variation in this particular dataset.
Not quite. R2 = 0.03 means the model explains only 3% of the variance. It is essentially useless as a predictor. R2 is not a percentage error — it is the proportion of variance explained. A value this low suggests the linear model does not capture the relationship in this data.

Key Takeaways

What You Should Remember

  • Linear regression fits a straight line (y = mx + b) to data by minimising the Mean Squared Error between predictions and actual values.
  • The slope tells you the rate of change; the intercept positions the line on the y-axis.
  • The least squares method finds the optimal parameters by minimising the sum of squared residuals.
  • Gradient descent is an iterative optimisation algorithm that walks downhill on the cost surface. The learning rate controls step size.
  • R-squared measures the proportion of variance explained by the model, from 0 (useless) to 1 (perfect).
  • Always check residuals for patterns — random scatter is good; systematic patterns indicate model mis-specification.
  • Do not extrapolate beyond the range of training data. The model is only reliable within the observed input range.
  • In civil engineering, linear regression powers applications from concrete strength prediction to settlement estimation and traffic volume forecasting.
