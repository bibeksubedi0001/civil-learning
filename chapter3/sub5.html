Dimensionality Reduction — PCA

When your bridge has 200 sensors recording 50 measurements each, you drown in 10,000 dimensions. Principal Component Analysis finds the handful of axes that capture almost all the information — compressing the data while preserving its essential structure.

55 min read 2 interactive demos 4 quiz questions

The Curse of Dimensionality

Picture a geotechnical engineer collecting soil samples across a construction site. For each sample, the lab returns grain size distribution, moisture content, liquid limit, plastic limit, specific gravity, cohesion, angle of internal friction, dry density, permeability, organic content, and several more parameters. A single borehole log easily generates 15–25 numeric features. Scale this to 200 boreholes, and you have a dataset with thousands of rows and dozens of columns.

Now imagine a structural health monitoring system on a cable-stayed bridge. Each of the 300 sensors records acceleration, velocity, displacement, temperature, and humidity at 100 Hz. A one-hour recording produces hundreds of millions of data points across hundreds of dimensions. Trying to visualise, cluster, or model this raw high-dimensional data is practically impossible.

This is the curse of dimensionality — a term coined by Richard Bellman in 1961. As the number of features (dimensions) grows, several problems compound:

Volume Explodes Exponentially

In one dimension, 10 evenly spaced sample points cover an interval well. In two dimensions, you need 102 = 100 points to achieve the same density in a square. In 20 dimensions, you need 1020 points — more than the number of grains of sand on Earth. Real datasets cannot possibly fill high-dimensional spaces uniformly, so every data point becomes effectively isolated.

Distances Become Meaningless

In high-dimensional spaces, the ratio of the distance to the nearest neighbour and the distance to the farthest neighbour converges to 1. All points appear roughly equidistant. Algorithms that rely on distance — K-Means, DBSCAN, k-NN — lose their discriminative power. A clustering algorithm cannot distinguish "close" from "far" when every pair is effectively the same distance apart.

Models Overfit

With many features and limited samples, models can memorise the training data rather than learning genuine patterns. A regression model predicting concrete strength from 50 mix-design parameters might achieve perfect training accuracy but fail completely on new mixes. Reducing dimensionality before modelling is often the most effective regularisation strategy.

Storage and Computation Costs

Structural health monitoring systems generate terabytes of high-dimensional time-series data every month. Transmitting, storing, and processing all raw dimensions is expensive. Compressing the data to a lower-dimensional representation — while retaining the information needed for damage detection — drastically cuts cost and latency.

Civil Engineering Analogy

Think of a structural drawing set for a high-rise building: plan view, elevation, section, detail — each is a different "projection" of the same 3D structure onto 2D paper. No single drawing captures everything, but together two or three well-chosen views convey nearly all the geometric information. PCA is the mathematical equivalent: it finds the best "views" (projections) of your high-dimensional data so that a few projections capture most of the variability.

Prerequisites

This sub-chapter assumes familiarity with basic linear algebra (vectors, matrices, matrix multiplication) and the unsupervised learning fundamentals from Sub-chapter 3.1. You do not need prior experience with eigenvalues or eigenvectors — we will build that intuition from scratch using civil engineering examples.

What Is Dimensionality Reduction?

Dimensionality reduction transforms a dataset from a high-dimensional feature space to a lower-dimensional one, aiming to preserve the most important relationships among the data points. If your original dataset has d features, a dimensionality reduction algorithm produces a new dataset with k features (where k < d), constructed so that the essential structure of the data is retained.

Feature Selection vs. Feature Extraction

There are two broad strategies for reducing dimensionality:

  • Feature selection keeps a subset of the original features and discards the rest. For example, dropping highly correlated soil parameters or removing sensor channels with constant readings. The surviving features retain their physical meaning.
  • Feature extraction creates new features as mathematical combinations of the originals. PCA belongs to this category. The new features (principal components) are linear combinations of all original features and generally do not correspond to a single physical measurement.

Goals of Dimensionality Reduction

Depending on the engineering context, dimensionality reduction serves one or more of these objectives:

  • Visualisation: Projecting high-dimensional sensor data to 2D or 3D for human interpretation.
  • Noise reduction: Discarding minor components that carry mostly noise improves downstream model accuracy.
  • Computational efficiency: Fewer features means faster training and lower memory usage.
  • De-correlation: PCA produces uncorrelated features, removing multicollinearity that destabilises regression models.
  • Data compression: Transmitting a handful of principal components instead of hundreds of raw readings saves bandwidth in remote SHM systems.

Among all dimensionality reduction techniques, Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is the most widely used, the most mathematically transparent, and the first method every engineer should learn. It is a linear technique: the new features are straight-line (linear) combinations of the originals. Non-linear methods such as t-SNE and UMAP (covered in Sub-chapter 3.6) capture more complex structures but are harder to interpret and more computationally expensive.

PCA Intuition: Finding the Axes of Maximum Variance

The central idea behind PCA is breathtakingly simple: find the direction in which the data varies the most, then find the next direction (perpendicular to the first) with the most remaining variance, and so on. These directions are called the principal components.

Step-by-Step Intuition

Imagine you have a 2D scatter plot of bridge sensor data: the x-axis is mid-span deflection and the y-axis is cable tension force. The data forms an elongated elliptical cloud oriented at roughly 45 degrees. PCA performs the following:

  1. Centre the data: Subtract the mean of each feature so the cloud is centred at the origin.
  2. Find the first principal component (PC1): This is the direction (a unit vector) along which the projected data has the largest variance. For our tilted ellipse, PC1 points along the long axis of the ellipse.
  3. Find the second principal component (PC2): This must be perpendicular (orthogonal) to PC1 and captures the maximum remaining variance. For a 2D dataset, PC2 is automatically determined — it points along the short axis of the ellipse.
  4. Project: Express every data point in the new coordinate system defined by PC1 and PC2. The coordinates in this rotated system are the principal component scores.

If PC1 captures 95% of the total variance, you can discard PC2 and represent each data point with a single number (its PC1 score) instead of two. You have reduced from 2D to 1D with only 5% information loss. In a 200-dimensional SHM dataset, the first 5 principal components might capture 98% of the variance, compressing 200 features to 5 with negligible loss.

Why Maximum Variance?

Variance is a proxy for information content. A feature that is constant across all samples carries zero information — it cannot help distinguish one sample from another. A feature with high variance separates samples effectively. By maximising variance, PCA ensures that the retained components carry as much discriminative power as possible.

An equivalent perspective: PCA minimises the reconstruction error — the squared distance between the original high-dimensional points and their low-dimensional projections. Maximising variance in the projection and minimising reconstruction error are two sides of the same coin.

Civil Engineering Analogy

Imagine shining a flashlight at a 3D structural model and looking at the shadow it casts on a wall. The shadow is a 2D projection of the 3D object. By rotating the model, you can find the angle that produces the most "informative" shadow — the one where the shadow has the largest area and shows the most structural detail. PCA automates this search: it rotates the coordinate axes until the first axis captures the largest "shadow" (variance), the second axis captures the next largest, and so on.

Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors — Simplified

PCA is computed through the eigenvalue decomposition of the data's covariance matrix. If "eigenvalue" sounds intimidating, let us demystify it with concrete intuition.

The Covariance Matrix

Given a dataset with d features, the covariance matrix C is a d × d symmetric matrix where the entry Cij measures how features i and j vary together. Diagonal entries Cii are the variances of individual features. Off-diagonal entries are covariances.

For a bridge with 4 sensor types (deflection, strain, temperature, wind speed), C is a 4×4 matrix. If deflection and strain are highly correlated (they both increase under load), their covariance will be large and positive. If temperature and strain are uncorrelated, their covariance will be near zero.

What Is an Eigenvector?

An eigenvector of a matrix A is a non-zero vector v that, when multiplied by A, only gets scaled (stretched or compressed) but not rotated:

A · v = λ · v

The scalar λ (lambda) is the corresponding eigenvalue. It tells you the scaling factor.

For the covariance matrix, each eigenvector defines a direction in feature space, and its eigenvalue equals the variance of the data when projected onto that direction. The eigenvector with the largest eigenvalue points in the direction of maximum variance — that is PC1.

What Is an Eigenvalue?

The eigenvalue associated with each eigenvector tells you how much variance that principal component captures. Large eigenvalue = important direction. Small eigenvalue = direction with little spread (mostly noise). By sorting eigenvalues from largest to smallest, you obtain the principal components in order of importance.

Civil Engineering Analogy

Think of a reinforced concrete beam under flexural loading. The beam deforms into a deflected shape. The mode shape of vibration (the pattern of deformation) is an eigenvector of the stiffness-mass system: it defines a direction in the deformation space. The corresponding natural frequency (related to the eigenvalue) tells you how "important" that mode is. The first mode (lowest frequency, largest deformation) captures most of the dynamic response, just as PC1 (largest eigenvalue) captures most of the variance. Higher modes contribute progressively less, just like higher PCs.

The PCA Algorithm Step by Step

Here is the complete PCA procedure for a dataset with n samples and d features.

Step 1: Standardise the Data

Subtract the mean of each feature and (optionally) divide by the standard deviation. Standardisation is critical when features have different units or scales. In civil engineering data, deflection might be in millimetres while temperature is in degrees Celsius. Without standardisation, the feature with the largest absolute values would dominate the variance and the first principal component.

Step 2: Compute the Covariance Matrix

Calculate the d × d covariance matrix C of the standardised data. Each entry Cij = (1 / (n−1)) ∑ (xi − meani)(xj − meanj). This matrix encodes all pairwise relationships between features.

Step 3: Compute Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

Find the eigenvalues λ1 ≥ λ2 ≥ ... ≥ λd and corresponding unit eigenvectors v1, v2, ..., vd of C. Because C is symmetric and positive semi-definite, all eigenvalues are non-negative, and eigenvectors are mutually orthogonal.

Step 4: Choose the Number of Components (k)

Rank the eigenvectors by their eigenvalues (largest first) and select the top k. The choice of k is guided by the explained variance ratio and the scree plot (see next sections). Form the d × k projection matrix W whose columns are the top k eigenvectors.

Step 5: Project the Data

Multiply the standardised data matrix X (n × d) by the projection matrix W (d × k) to obtain the reduced dataset Z = X · W (n × k). Each row of Z contains the k principal component scores for one sample.

Complexity

Computing the full eigendecomposition of a d × d matrix takes O(d3). For very high-dimensional data (d > 1000), randomised SVD or incremental PCA algorithms bring the cost down significantly. Scikit-learn's PCA(svd_solver='randomized') handles millions of features efficiently.

Explained Variance Ratio

The explained variance ratio of the i-th principal component is the fraction of total variance captured by that component:

EVRi = λi / ∑j=1..d λj

Interpreting EVR

If λ1 = 4.5, λ2 = 2.1, λ3 = 0.3, and λ4 = 0.1, the total variance is 7.0. PC1 explains 4.5/7.0 = 64.3%, PC2 explains 30.0%, PC3 explains 4.3%, and PC4 explains 1.4%. Together, PC1 and PC2 explain 94.3% of the variance — reducing 4 dimensions to 2 while retaining over 94% of the information.

Cumulative Explained Variance

The cumulative explained variance after k components is the sum of the first k EVRs. This tells you the total fraction of information preserved when you keep k components. Common thresholds are 90%, 95%, or 99%.

A civil engineer monitoring a dam with 50 sensor channels might find that the first 3 principal components capture 97% of the cumulative variance. This means the dam's behaviour can be described almost entirely by 3 latent factors — perhaps hydrostatic pressure, thermal expansion, and seasonal reservoir level — even though 50 sensors are recording.

The Scree Plot and Choosing k

A scree plot is a bar chart (or line chart) of eigenvalues plotted in descending order. The name comes from geology: "scree" is the rubble at the base of a cliff. In a scree plot, the eigenvalues typically drop steeply at first (the cliff) and then level off (the scree). The point where the curve bends — the elbow — suggests the optimal number of components.

The Elbow Method for PCA

Eigenvalues to the left of the elbow represent components that capture genuine data structure. Eigenvalues to the right are small and roughly equal, suggesting they model noise rather than signal. Retaining only the "cliff" components and discarding the "scree" achieves dimensionality reduction with minimal information loss.

Kaiser's Rule

A simpler heuristic: keep all components with eigenvalue greater than 1 (when working with the correlation matrix). The rationale is that a component should explain at least as much variance as a single original standardised variable. This rule is easy to apply but can be too aggressive or too conservative depending on the dataset.

Variance Threshold

Choose the smallest k such that the cumulative explained variance exceeds a threshold (e.g., 95%). This is the most common approach in engineering practice because it directly controls the amount of information retained.

Civil Engineering Analogy

The scree plot is like a Pareto chart for structural vibration modes. In modal analysis, the first few natural modes capture most of a building's dynamic response to wind or seismic loading. Higher modes contribute increasingly less. Engineers routinely truncate the modal expansion after 5–10 modes because the remaining modes have negligible participation factors. The scree plot applies the same principle to data dimensions rather than vibration modes.

CE Application: Reducing Multi-Sensor Bridge Monitoring Data

Structural health monitoring of a modern bridge generates staggering volumes of high-dimensional data. Consider a typical instrumented highway bridge with:

  • 40 accelerometers (3-axis each = 120 channels)
  • 30 strain gauges
  • 15 displacement transducers
  • 20 temperature sensors
  • 10 wind speed/direction sensors

That is 195 sensor channels. Each hourly snapshot produces a 195-dimensional feature vector. Across a year of continuous monitoring, you accumulate 8,760 samples in 195 dimensions — and that is after aggressive time-domain summarisation.

How PCA Helps

Applying PCA to this dataset typically reveals that the first 3–5 principal components capture 90–98% of the variance. Why? Because many sensors measure aspects of the same underlying physical phenomena:

  • PC1 often captures traffic loading — deflection, strain, and acceleration all increase when heavy trucks cross the bridge. Dozens of sensor channels are correlated because they respond to the same load.
  • PC2 often captures thermal effects — temperature changes cause expansion of the deck, shifts in cable forces, and drift in strain readings.
  • PC3 might capture wind loading — lateral accelerations and wind speed sensors load heavily on this component.

Instead of storing and transmitting 195 numbers per hour, you transmit 5 principal component scores. For damage detection, you monitor these scores over time: a sudden shift in PC1 that is not explained by traffic load may indicate structural degradation.

Novelty Detection via PCA Residuals

The variance not captured by the top k components — the reconstruction error or residual — is also valuable. Under normal conditions, the residual is small and stable. If a new data point produces an unusually large residual, it means the point cannot be explained by the k dominant patterns learned from historical data. This flags the data as anomalous — a principle used in PCA-based structural damage detection systems worldwide.

Practical Tip

Always standardise sensor data before applying PCA. Accelerometers record values in m/s2 (order of magnitude 0.01–10), while strain gauges record microstrain (order of magnitude 10–1000). Without standardisation, the strain channels would dominate the covariance matrix, and PC1 would simply reflect "which features have the biggest numbers" rather than "which direction has the most information."

CE Application: Compressing Soil Test Results

Geotechnical site investigations generate datasets where each borehole sample is described by 10–25 laboratory parameters. Many of these parameters are correlated by the underlying mineralogy and depositional history of the soil.

Typical Soil Parameters

A comprehensive soil lab report might include: natural moisture content (w), liquid limit (LL), plastic limit (PL), plasticity index (PI), specific gravity (Gs), grain size fractions (gravel, sand, silt, clay percentages), dry density (γd), void ratio (e), degree of saturation (S), unconfined compressive strength (UCS), cohesion (c), friction angle (φ), permeability (k), compression index (Cc), and organic content.

Many of these are physically linked. Plasticity index is the difference of liquid and plastic limits. Void ratio and dry density are inversely related. Clay content correlates strongly with plasticity. These correlations mean the "intrinsic" dimensionality of the data is much lower than the number of measured parameters.

PCA on Soil Data

Research studies on alluvial soil datasets from major projects have shown that PCA typically reduces 15 soil parameters to 3–4 principal components explaining over 85% of the variance:

  • PC1: A "plasticity" factor — LL, PL, PI, clay content, and cohesion load heavily on this component.
  • PC2: A "density/compaction" factor — dry density, void ratio, moisture content, and degree of saturation.
  • PC3: A "gradation" factor — sand and gravel percentages, permeability.

These compressed representations can then be used for soil classification, spatial interpolation (kriging in PC-space), or input to machine learning models predicting bearing capacity.

Civil Engineering Analogy

Soil classification systems like USCS already perform a crude form of dimensionality reduction: they compress many lab parameters into a single letter code (CL, SM, GP, etc.). PCA does the same thing more rigorously and quantitatively, capturing the continuous spectrum of soil variation rather than forcing samples into discrete categories. Think of PCA as a "continuous Casagrande chart" that uses all available lab data simultaneously.

Limitations and Practical Considerations

PCA is powerful but not a silver bullet. Understanding its limitations helps you decide when to use it and when to choose alternatives.

Linearity Assumption

PCA finds linear combinations of features. If the true structure of the data lies on a curved manifold (e.g., a spiral or a Swiss roll), PCA will not capture it efficiently. Non-linear methods like kernel PCA, t-SNE, or UMAP (Sub-chapter 3.6) handle curved structures better.

Loss of Interpretability

Principal components are abstract linear combinations. PC1 might be "0.35 × deflection + 0.28 × strain − 0.41 × cable_force + ...". This lacks the physical transparency of the original features. Engineers must examine the loadings (the eigenvector coefficients) to interpret what each PC represents. In some regulated domains, stakeholders may prefer interpretable features.

Sensitivity to Scaling

If you forget to standardise, features measured in large units dominate PCA. A temperature range of 0–50 °C and a microstrain range of 0–2000 will make PCA think strain is 40 times more important than temperature, even if both carry equal information. Always standardise unless all features share the same units and similar scales.

Sensitivity to Outliers

PCA is based on variance, and variance is sensitive to outliers. A single faulty sensor reading with an extreme value can pull PC1 toward the outlier direction. Robust PCA variants or preprocessing with outlier removal mitigate this issue.

Gaussian Assumption

PCA is optimal for Gaussian-distributed data. For heavily skewed or multi-modal data, the principal components may not align with the most meaningful directions. In practice, PCA often works well even for non-Gaussian data, but Independent Component Analysis (ICA) may be preferred when the goal is to separate independent non-Gaussian sources (e.g., separating traffic-induced vibrations from wind-induced vibrations in SHM data).

Practical Implementation Notes

When implementing PCA on real civil engineering data, keep these practical considerations in mind.

Scikit-learn Workflow

In Python, the standard PCA workflow uses scikit-learn: 

from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
from sklearn.decomposition import PCA
import numpy as np

# X is your n x d data matrix
scaler = StandardScaler()
X_scaled = scaler.fit_transform(X)

pca = PCA(n_components=0.95)  # retain 95% variance
X_reduced = pca.fit_transform(X_scaled)

print(f"Reduced from {X.shape[1]} to {X_reduced.shape[1]} dims")
print(f"Explained variance: {pca.explained_variance_ratio_}")

Key Parameters

  • n_components: An integer (exact number of PCs to keep) or a float between 0 and 1 (minimum cumulative variance to retain). Using a float like 0.95 is often the most practical choice.
  • svd_solver: 'auto', 'full', 'arpack', or 'randomized'. For large datasets, use 'randomized' for speed.
  • whiten: If True, each component is scaled to unit variance. Useful as preprocessing for some downstream algorithms.

Examining Loadings

The loadings (eigenvector components) tell you which original features contribute to each PC. Access them via pca.components_ in scikit-learn. A loading matrix heatmap is an invaluable diagnostic tool: it reveals which sensors or soil parameters "load" on which principal component, enabling physical interpretation.

Reconstruction

PCA is invertible (approximately). You can reconstruct the original data from the reduced representation using pca.inverse_transform(X_reduced). The difference between the original and reconstructed data is the reconstruction error, which quantifies the information lost during compression. In SHM applications, monitoring reconstruction error over time serves as a damage indicator.

Knowledge Check

Quick Check 1

What does PCA maximise when choosing the direction of the first principal component?

Correct! PCA selects the direction (unit vector) that maximises the variance of the projected data. This is equivalent to minimising the total reconstruction error. The eigenvalue of the covariance matrix in that direction equals the projected variance.
Not quite. PCA finds the direction that maximises the variance of the data when projected onto that axis. Variance measures the spread of projected values and serves as a proxy for information content.

Quick Check 2

A bridge monitoring dataset has 200 sensor channels. After PCA, the first 4 components explain 96% of the total variance. What does this imply?

Correct! When a few PCs capture nearly all the variance, it means the original features are highly correlated and driven by a small number of underlying factors (e.g., traffic load, temperature, wind). The remaining 196 dimensions contain mostly noise or redundant information.
Not quite. The high explained variance by 4 components indicates strong correlations among the 200 sensor channels. A handful of latent factors (physical phenomena like load, temperature, wind) drive the majority of the observed variation.

Quick Check 3

Why is it critical to standardise features before applying PCA to soil test data with parameters in different units?

Correct! PCA maximises variance. If permeability is measured in mm/s (range 0.001–10) and UCS in kPa (range 10–500), UCS will have far larger variance simply due to its scale, and PC1 will just reflect UCS. Standardisation gives each feature equal weight so that PCA captures genuine data structure, not unit artifacts.
Not quite. The key issue is that PCA maximises variance, and variance depends on scale. Features with larger numeric ranges have larger variances and would dominate PCA if not standardised. Standardisation ensures equal weighting across features.

Quick Check 4

In a scree plot, the "elbow" point indicates:

Correct! The elbow in a scree plot marks the transition from components with large eigenvalues (capturing genuine data structure) to components with small, roughly equal eigenvalues (capturing noise). Retaining components before the elbow and discarding those after gives you the optimal dimensionality.
Not quite. The elbow in a scree plot is where eigenvalues transition from large (signal) to small and flat (noise). Components before the elbow capture meaningful data structure; those after mostly capture random fluctuations.

Key Takeaways

Summary

  • The curse of dimensionality causes distances to become meaningless, models to overfit, and computation to explode as the number of features grows.
  • PCA finds orthogonal axes (principal components) that maximise the variance of the projected data.
  • Principal components are eigenvectors of the covariance matrix; the corresponding eigenvalues equal the variance captured by each component.
  • The explained variance ratio tells you how much information each component retains. The scree plot helps you choose how many components to keep.
  • In bridge SHM, PCA compresses hundreds of sensor channels into a handful of components representing load, temperature, and wind — enabling efficient transmission and anomaly detection.
  • In geotechnical engineering, PCA reduces 15+ soil parameters to 3–4 latent factors (plasticity, density, gradation).
  • Always standardise features before PCA when they have different units or scales.
  • PCA is linear; for non-linear structure, consider kernel PCA, t-SNE, or UMAP (next sub-chapter).
