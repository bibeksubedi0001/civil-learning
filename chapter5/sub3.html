Chapter 5 — Sub-chapter 5.3

Transformer Architecture

In 2017 a single paper declared "Attention Is All You Need" and dismantled the dominance of recurrence. The Transformer processes entire sequences in parallel, stacks self-attention into deep towers, and powers every modern large language model — from GPT to BERT and beyond.

40 min read 2 interactive demos 4 quiz questions

"Attention Is All You Need" (2017)

In June 2017 Vaswani et al. published a paper at Google Brain that would reshape the entire field of deep learning. The title was deliberately provocative: Attention Is All You Need. The core claim was that a model built entirely from attention mechanisms — with no recurrence and no convolutions — could outperform every existing sequence-to-sequence architecture on machine translation benchmarks.

They were right. The architecture they introduced, the Transformer, not only matched the best RNN and CNN-based translation systems but surpassed them while training significantly faster. Within two years, the Transformer became the foundation of BERT, GPT, T5, and virtually every breakthrough in natural language processing. Today it has expanded into computer vision, protein folding, weather forecasting, and — increasingly — civil engineering applications.

The key innovations were not individual components but their composition. Self-attention (which we explored in Sub-chapter 5.2) was already known. Residual connections and layer normalisation had been used in deep CNNs. The breakthrough was assembling these pieces into a coherent, elegant architecture that eliminated the sequential bottleneck of RNNs entirely.

Prerequisites

This sub-chapter assumes you understand the attention mechanism from Sub-chapter 5.2: Query, Key, and Value vectors, scaled dot-product attention, and self-attention. We build directly on those foundations to construct the full Transformer.

Why the Transformer Matters for Civil Engineers

The Transformer is relevant to civil engineering not because you will build one from scratch, but because it is the engine inside the AI tools you will increasingly use: automated specification analysis, generative design assistants, sensor anomaly detection systems, and natural-language interfaces to BIM models. Understanding the architecture lets you evaluate these tools critically — knowing their strengths, limitations, and failure modes.

The Design Review Committee Analogy

Civil Engineering Analogy

The Transformer is a multi-disciplinary design review committee. Imagine a major infrastructure project — a cable-stayed bridge — undergoing its final design review. The review committee has eight specialist engineers (the attention heads), each focusing on a different aspect:

  • Head 1 (Structural): focuses on load paths, member forces, and connection details
  • Head 2 (Geotechnical): focuses on foundation bearing capacity and settlement
  • Head 3 (Hydraulic): focuses on scour depth and flood levels
  • Head 4 (Seismic): focuses on dynamic response and ductility
  • Head 5 (Durability): focuses on concrete cover, coatings, and corrosion protection
  • Head 6 (Construction): focuses on build sequence, crane access, and temporary works
  • Head 7 (Cost): focuses on material quantities, labour hours, and budget
  • Head 8 (Compliance): focuses on code references, design standards, and regulatory requirements

Each head reads the same set of design documents (the input sequence) but attends to different sections. After the review, their individual findings are concatenated into a combined report and passed through a synthesis step (the linear projection) that merges all perspectives into a unified assessment.

This happens six times (the encoder layers). At each round, the committee refines its understanding, building on the combined insights from the previous round. Early rounds catch obvious issues; later rounds identify subtle interactions between disciplines — like how the geotechnical settlement affects the structural load distribution which in turn affects the seismic response.

This analogy maps onto every major component of the Transformer: multi-head attention is the committee of specialists, the feed-forward layers are where each specialist processes their findings independently, residual connections ensure that original information is never lost, and layer normalisation keeps the review process stable across rounds.

Multi-Head Attention

In Sub-chapter 5.2 we learned that a single attention head computes Query, Key, and Value projections and produces a weighted combination of Values. A single head, however, can only focus on one type of relationship at a time. Multi-head attention runs h parallel attention heads, each with its own learned Q, K, V projection matrices, so the model can simultaneously attend to information from different representation subspaces.

Multi-Head Attention Formula

MultiHead(Q, K, V) = Concat(head1, ..., headh) · WO
where headi = Attention(Q · WiQ, K · WiK, V · WiV)

Each head operates on a subspace of the full model dimension. If dmodel = 512 and h = 8 heads, each head works with dk = dv = 512/8 = 64 dimensions. The total computation cost is similar to a single head with full dimensionality, but the model gains the ability to attend to multiple relationships simultaneously.

What Different Heads Learn

Research has shown that different attention heads in trained Transformers specialise in recognising different types of relationships:

Observed Head Specialisations

  • Positional heads: attend to adjacent tokens (local context)
  • Syntactic heads: attend to grammatically related words (subject-verb pairs)
  • Semantic heads: attend to contextually related words regardless of distance
  • Rare-token heads: disproportionately attend to unusual or important tokens

No one programs these specialisations. They emerge naturally during training as the model discovers that different types of attention patterns are useful for the task.

Original Configuration

The original Transformer used dmodel = 512 with h = 8 attention heads. Modern large language models scale both parameters dramatically: GPT-3 uses dmodel = 12288 with 96 heads. The principle, however, remains identical.

Positional Encoding: Why Position Matters

There is a critical problem with self-attention: it is permutation-invariant. If you shuffle the order of input tokens, the attention mechanism produces the same output (just shuffled the same way). Self-attention has no built-in notion of position or order — every token is treated as if it exists in a position-less set.

This is a serious limitation. In an RNN, position is implicit: the hidden state at step 5 has been shaped by steps 1 through 4. But the Transformer processes all positions in parallel with no recurrence, so it has no idea whether a token appears at position 1 or position 100 unless we explicitly tell it.

Civil Engineering Analogy

Positional encoding is like chainage markers on a highway alignment. A set of horizontal curve parameters (radius, deflection angle, superelevation) is meaningless without knowing where along the route each curve occurs. Chainage markers (Km 0+000, Km 1+250, etc.) provide absolute position. Without them, you have a bag of curves with no spatial order — you could not reconstruct the road alignment.

Similarly, positional encodings stamp each token with a unique position signature so the Transformer knows that "beam" at position 3 is different from "beam" at position 47, even though the word embedding is the same.

Sinusoidal Positional Encoding

The original Transformer uses sinusoidal functions to generate positional encodings. For each position pos and each dimension i of the encoding vector:

Sinusoidal Encoding Formulae

PE(pos, 2i)    = sin(pos / 100002i/dmodel)
PE(pos, 2i+1) = cos(pos / 100002i/dmodel)

Even dimensions use sine; odd dimensions use cosine. The wavelength increases geometrically from 2π (for i = 0) to 10000 · 2π (for i = dmodel/2). This creates a unique pattern for every position.

Why Sinusoids?

The choice of sinusoidal functions is not arbitrary. The authors chose them for two key properties:

Unique Encoding for Every Position

The combination of sine and cosine waves at different frequencies produces a unique vector for every position. No two positions share the same encoding, so the model can always distinguish between them.

Relative Positions Through Linear Transformations

For any fixed offset k, PE(pos + k) can be expressed as a linear function of PE(pos). This means the model can learn to attend to relative positions (e.g., "two tokens to the left") without needing a separate mechanism. The sinusoidal structure encodes relative distance naturally.

Adding Encodings to Embeddings

The positional encoding vector has the same dimension dmodel as the word embedding. It is added element-wise to the input embedding, not concatenated. This means the model receives a single vector per token that combines both what the token is (the embedding) and where it appears (the positional encoding).

Learned vs. Fixed Encodings

The original paper found that learned positional embeddings performed nearly identically to sinusoidal encodings. Modern models like BERT use learned positional embeddings, while others use more advanced schemes like Rotary Position Embedding (RoPE). The sinusoidal approach remains important conceptually because it generalises to unseen sequence lengths without retraining.

The Encoder Stack

The encoder's job is to process the input sequence and produce a rich, contextualised representation of every token. It does this by stacking N identical layers (N = 6 in the original paper). Each layer contains two sub-layers:

Sub-layer 1: Multi-Head Self-Attention

Every token attends to every other token in the input sequence (including itself). This is the self-attention mechanism from Sub-chapter 5.2, extended to multiple heads. After this sub-layer, each token's representation has been enriched with contextual information from the entire sequence.

Sub-layer 2: Position-wise Feed-Forward Network

After attention has mixed information across positions, each position passes through a two-layer feed-forward network independently. The same feed-forward network is applied to every position, but there is no information exchange between positions in this sub-layer.

FFN(x) = max(0, x · W1 + b1) · W2 + b2

The inner dimension dff is typically 4 × dmodel (2048 for dmodel = 512). The ReLU activation introduces non-linearity, allowing the model to learn complex transformations of the attended representations.

Residual Connections

Each sub-layer is wrapped in a residual connection (also called a skip connection). The output of each sub-layer is:

Residual Connection Formula

output = LayerNorm(x + SubLayer(x))

The input x is added directly to the sub-layer's output before normalisation. This means the sub-layer only needs to learn the residual — the difference between the desired output and the input — rather than the full transformation. This makes deep networks dramatically easier to train.

Civil Engineering Analogy

Residual connections are like redline markups on design drawings. When a reviewer marks up a structural drawing, they do not redraw the entire sheet. They add annotations, circles, and corrections on top of the original drawing. The original information is fully preserved (the base drawing), and the markup adds incremental changes (the residual). If a reviewer adds nothing useful, the original drawing passes through unchanged — no information is lost.

Layer Normalisation

Layer normalisation standardises the activations across the feature dimension at each position. For a vector x = [x1, x2, ..., xd], it computes the mean and variance across all d dimensions, then normalises:

Layer Normalisation

LayerNorm(x) = γ · (x − μ) / √(σ2 + ε) + β

μ and σ2 are the mean and variance of x. γ (scale) and β (shift) are learned parameters. ε is a small constant for numerical stability. This keeps activations in a well-behaved range throughout the deep network, preventing training instability.

Stacking Depth

The original Transformer stacks 6 encoder layers. Each layer refines the representations further. Early layers capture local syntactic patterns; deeper layers capture increasingly abstract, long-range semantic relationships. The residual connections ensure that gradients can flow through all 6 layers without vanishing.

The Decoder Stack

The decoder generates the output sequence one token at a time (during inference). Like the encoder, it consists of N = 6 identical layers, but each decoder layer has three sub-layers instead of two:

Sub-layer 1: Masked Multi-Head Self-Attention

The decoder self-attention is masked so that position i can only attend to positions ≤ i. This prevents the decoder from "cheating" by looking at future tokens during training. The mask sets attention scores for future positions to −∞ before the softmax, ensuring they receive zero weight.

Sub-layer 2: Cross-Attention (Encoder-Decoder Attention)

This is where the decoder "reads" the encoder's output. The Queries come from the decoder (what information does the decoder currently need?), while the Keys and Values come from the encoder's final output (what information is available in the input?). This allows each decoder position to attend to all positions in the input sequence.

Sub-layer 3: Position-wise Feed-Forward Network

Identical in structure to the encoder's feed-forward network. Each position is processed independently through a two-layer network with a ReLU activation.

As in the encoder, every sub-layer is wrapped with a residual connection and layer normalisation: output = LayerNorm(x + SubLayer(x)).

Civil Engineering Analogy

Cross-attention is like the construction team consulting the design documents. The encoder has produced a fully reviewed set of design documents (the encoder output). The decoder is the construction team, generating the build sequence one step at a time. At each construction step, the team consults the design documents (cross-attention) to determine what to build next. They can look at any part of the design — foundations, superstructure, finishes — but they can only see what they have already built (masked self-attention prevents looking at future steps).

The Final Output Layer

After the stack of N decoder layers, the output passes through a linear layer followed by a softmax to produce a probability distribution over the vocabulary. The token with the highest probability is selected as the output for that position. This process repeats autoregressively — each predicted token is fed back as input for the next position.

Encoder-Only vs. Decoder-Only

The original Transformer uses both encoder and decoder. However, many modern models use only one half: BERT uses only the encoder (for understanding tasks), while GPT uses only the decoder (for generation tasks). We will explore these variants in Sub-chapters 5.4 and 5.5.

The Full Architecture

Bringing all the components together, the Transformer architecture follows this data flow:

Encoder Side

  1. Input Embedding: convert tokens to dmodel-dimensional vectors
  2. Positional Encoding: add sinusoidal position signals
  3. ×N Encoder Layers: each containing:
    • Multi-Head Self-Attention + Add & Norm
    • Feed-Forward Network + Add & Norm
  4. Output: contextualised representations for all input positions

Decoder Side

  1. Output Embedding: convert previously generated tokens to vectors
  2. Positional Encoding: add sinusoidal position signals
  3. ×N Decoder Layers: each containing:
    • Masked Multi-Head Self-Attention + Add & Norm
    • Multi-Head Cross-Attention (Q from decoder, K/V from encoder) + Add & Norm
    • Feed-Forward Network + Add & Norm
  4. Linear + Softmax: produce next-token probability distribution

Parameter Counts

The original Transformer base model had approximately 65 million parameters. Most parameters reside in the embedding layers and the feed-forward networks (which have the large inner dimension dff = 2048). The attention mechanisms themselves are relatively parameter- efficient — their power comes from the dynamic computation of attention weights, not from static parameters.

Model Variants

The paper presented two configurations: Base (dmodel=512, N=6, h=8, dff=2048, 65M params) and Big (dmodel=1024, N=6, h=16, dff=4096, 213M params). The Big model achieved the best translation results. Modern models have scaled to hundreds of billions of parameters using the same fundamental architecture.

Residual Connections & Layer Normalisation

These two techniques are not glamorous, but they are essential. Without them, stacking 6 (or 96) layers of attention and feed-forward networks would be practically impossible to train. Let us understand why.

The Vanishing Gradient Problem in Deep Networks

As a network grows deeper, gradients during backpropagation must travel through more and more layers. At each layer, the gradient is multiplied by the layer's Jacobian matrix. If these multiplications consistently shrink the gradient (eigenvalues < 1), the gradient effectively vanishes by the time it reaches the early layers — those layers stop learning.

How Residual Connections Help

With the residual formulation y = x + F(x), the gradient of the loss with respect to x includes a direct path:

dL/dx = dL/dy · (1 + dF/dx)

The "1" in the expression ensures that the gradient is at least as large as dL/dy, regardless of what dF/dx is. This creates a gradient highway through the network — gradients can flow from the loss all the way to the first layer without attenuation.

Why Layer Normalisation Instead of Batch Normalisation?

Batch normalisation, which normalises across the batch dimension, was the standard for CNNs. But for sequence models, batch normalisation has a critical flaw: sequences in a batch may have different lengths, making the batch statistics unreliable for positions near the end of shorter sequences. Layer normalisation sidesteps this entirely by normalising across the feature dimension within each individual example.

Pre-Norm vs. Post-Norm

The original Transformer applies layer norm after the residual addition (Post-Norm): LayerNorm(x + SubLayer(x)). Many modern implementations apply it before the sub-layer (Pre-Norm): x + SubLayer(LayerNorm(x)). Pre-Norm tends to be more stable for very deep models and often allows larger learning rates.

The sinusoidal encoding is reminiscent of binary number representations. In binary, the least significant bit alternates every number (0, 1, 0, 1, ...), the next bit every two numbers (0, 0, 1, 1, ...), and so on. Similarly, the lowest PE dimensions oscillate rapidly while higher dimensions oscillate more slowly. The encoding acts like a continuous, smooth version of binary counting.

Putting It All Together

Let us trace a complete forward pass through the Transformer to solidify the architecture. We will follow a translation task: English to French.

Step 1: Encode the Input

The English sentence "The bridge design is adequate" is tokenised into tokens: ["The", "bridge", "design", "is", "adequate"]. Each token is converted to a 512-dimensional embedding vector. Positional encodings are added, giving each token a unique position signature.

Step 2: Pass Through Encoder Stack

The 5 × 512 matrix passes through 6 encoder layers. In each layer, multi-head self-attention allows "bridge" to attend to "design" and "adequate" (learning that "adequate" modifies the "design" of the "bridge"). The feed-forward network then processes each enriched representation. After 6 layers, each token's vector captures rich contextual information about the entire sentence.

Step 3: Decoder Generates Output Autoregressively

The decoder begins with a start token. It uses masked self-attention (can only see previous outputs), cross-attention (reads the encoder output to find relevant source words), and the feed-forward network to predict the first French token: "Le". Then "Le" is fed back, and the decoder predicts "pont". This continues: "Le pont est adequat" until an end token is generated.

Step 4: Output Probability Distribution

At each decoder step, the final layer produces a vector that is projected to vocabulary size (e.g., 37,000 tokens) and passed through softmax. The model might output: P("Le") = 0.92, P("Un") = 0.04, P("La") = 0.03, etc. The highest-probability token is selected (or sampled from the distribution for more diverse outputs).

Training the Transformer

The original Transformer was trained on machine translation using a specific set of techniques that have since become standard practice for large models.

Learning Rate Schedule

The paper introduced a custom learning rate schedule with a warmup phase. The learning rate increases linearly for the first warmup_steps (4000 steps), then decreases proportionally to the inverse square root of the step number. This prevents early training instability while allowing aggressive learning in later stages.

lr = dmodel−0.5 · min(step−0.5, step · warmup−1.5)

Regularisation: Dropout and Label Smoothing

Dropout (rate 0.1) is applied after each sub-layer, to the attention weights, and to the positional encodings. This prevents overfitting by randomly zeroing activations during training. Label smoothing (ε = 0.1) replaces the hard 0/1 target distribution with a smoothed version, preventing the model from becoming overconfident in its predictions.

Training Speed

The original Transformer base model was trained on 8 NVIDIA P100 GPUs for 3.5 days (approximately 100,000 steps). The Big model took 3.5 days on 8 GPUs. This was dramatically faster than comparable RNN-based models because the Transformer's attention computation is fully parallelisable — no sequential dependency between time steps.

Key Takeaways

Summary

  • The Transformer eliminates recurrence — it processes all positions in parallel using self-attention, dramatically increasing training speed on modern hardware.
  • Multi-head attention runs h parallel attention heads, each learning to focus on different types of relationships (syntactic, semantic, positional, etc.).
  • Positional encoding injects position information via sinusoidal functions, since self-attention is inherently position-agnostic.
  • Encoder = N layers of [Self-Attention + FFN], each with residual connections and layer normalisation.
  • Decoder = N layers of [Masked Self-Attention + Cross-Attention + FFN], each with residual connections and layer normalisation.
  • Cross-attention connects the decoder to the encoder, allowing the output generator to consult the input representations at every layer.
  • Residual connections create gradient highways; layer normalisation keeps activations stable.

Knowledge Check

Test your understanding of the Transformer architecture with these four questions. Select the best answer for each.

Question 1 of 4

Why does the Transformer need positional encoding?

Correct! Self-attention computes the same output regardless of input ordering. Positional encoding explicitly injects position information so the model can distinguish between tokens at different positions.
Not quite. The core issue is that self-attention treats input as an unordered set. Without positional encoding, "The beam supports the slab" and "The slab supports the beam" would produce identical representations.
Question 2 of 4

In the decoder, what is the purpose of masking in the self-attention layer?

Correct! The decoder must generate tokens one at a time. During training, masking ensures position i can only attend to positions ≤ i, preventing information leakage from future tokens.
Not quite. Masking preserves the autoregressive property: each position can only see itself and previous positions. Future positions are set to −∞ before softmax so they receive zero attention weight.
Question 3 of 4

In the cross-attention layer of the decoder, where do the Queries, Keys, and Values come from?

Correct! Cross-attention allows the decoder to "ask questions" (Queries from decoder) about the input sequence (Keys and Values from encoder). This is how the decoder accesses the encoded input representation.
Not quite. In cross-attention, the decoder provides the Queries (what it needs to know), while the encoder provides Keys and Values (what information is available in the source input).
Question 4 of 4

What is the primary purpose of residual connections in the Transformer architecture?

Correct! Residual connections add the input directly to the sub-layer output (y = x + F(x)), ensuring the gradient always has a direct path back through the network. This is essential for training deep stacks of 6+ layers.
Not quite. Residual connections solve the vanishing gradient problem. By adding the input directly to the output (y = x + F(x)), they guarantee that gradients can flow through the network unattenuated, enabling the training of very deep models.
