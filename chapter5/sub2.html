Chapter 5 — Sub-chapter 5.2

The Attention Mechanism

Not all information deserves equal focus. Just as a structural engineer's eye snaps to the critical load path in a hundred-page report, the attention mechanism teaches neural networks to concentrate on the parts that matter most.

35 min read 2 interactive demos 4 quiz questions

The Problem of Equal Treatment

In the previous sub-chapter we built RNNs that compress an entire sequence into a single hidden state vector. This works, but there is a fundamental limitation: by the time the network reaches the end of a long sequence, the information from early time steps has been repeatedly transformed and diluted. The hidden state becomes a lossy bottleneck.

Imagine reading a 500-page structural analysis report and being asked to answer a question about the foundation design discussed on page 12. If your only memory is a single paragraph summarising the entire report (the final hidden state), you would struggle. What you really need is the ability to look back at page 12 directly.

This is exactly what the attention mechanism provides. Instead of forcing the model to compress everything into a fixed-size vector, attention allows the network to dynamically focus on different parts of the input sequence depending on what it currently needs. Some positions receive high attention (they are highly relevant), while others receive near-zero attention (they can be safely ignored for the current task).

The concept was introduced by Bahdanau et al. in 2014 for machine translation and has since become the single most important building block in modern deep learning. It is the foundation upon which the Transformer architecture (sub-chapter 5.3) is built.

Prerequisites

This sub-chapter assumes you are comfortable with the RNN concepts from Sub-chapter 5.1, including hidden states and sequence processing. You should also be familiar with basic matrix multiplication and the softmax function from earlier chapters.

Why Attention Matters

Consider three concrete advantages that attention brings over the bottleneck approach of vanilla RNNs:

1. Selective Focus

Not every part of the input is equally relevant to every output. When translating a sentence, the word "bridge" in the source might map strongly to "pont" in French but have zero relevance to the translation of "today." Attention lets the model assign different weights to different input positions at each output step.

2. Direct Information Pathways

In a vanilla RNN, information from time step 1 must pass through every intermediate hidden state to reach time step 100. Each hop risks losing information. With attention, the model creates a shortcut — a direct connection from any input position to the current processing step, regardless of distance.

3. Interpretability

The attention weights themselves form a distribution over input positions that can be visualised. This reveals what the model is looking at when producing each output — a rare window into the otherwise opaque decision-making of neural networks.

The Structural Engineer's Report Analogy

Civil Engineering Analogy

Attention is how a structural engineer reads a report. Picture a senior structural engineer reviewing a 200-page design report for a multi-storey building. The report covers foundations, columns, beams, slabs, lateral systems, connections, material specifications, load combinations, and construction sequences.

The engineer's current task is to verify the adequacy of the column-beam connections on Floor 7. They do not read every page with equal intensity. Instead, they:

  • Quickly scan page headers and section titles (computing relevance scores)
  • Assign high attention to the section on connection design, the load table for Floor 7, and the steel grade specification
  • Assign moderate attention to adjacent floors and general lateral load assumptions
  • Assign near-zero attention to the foundation design chapter and the construction schedule
  • Extract and synthesise the high-attention information to form their conclusion

This is precisely what the attention mechanism does: it computes a relevance score between the current question (Query) and every section of information (Keys), then uses those scores to create a weighted combination of the content (Values).

This analogy maps directly onto the mathematical formulation we are about to explore. The engineer's question is the Query. The section headers are the Keys. The section contents are the Values. The attention weights are how much focus each section receives.

Query, Key, and Value Vectors

The attention mechanism operates on three types of vectors, each derived from the input through learned linear transformations (matrix multiplications). Understanding what each represents is the key to understanding attention.

Query (Q) — "What am I looking for?"

The Query vector represents the current position's information need. It encodes what the model is searching for in the rest of the sequence. In our analogy, it is the engineer's specific question: "What is the connection capacity at Floor 7?"

Q = X · WQ

Key (K) — "What do I contain?"

The Key vector represents the identity or label of each position's information. It is designed so that when a Key matches a Query well (high dot product), the corresponding position is relevant. In our analogy, Keys are the section headers and keywords that help the engineer decide where to focus.

K = X · WK

Value (V) — "What information do I provide?"

The Value vector represents the actual content at each position. Once the model knows which positions are relevant (via the Query-Key comparison), it extracts information by taking a weighted sum of the Value vectors. In our analogy, Values are the detailed technical content within each section.

V = X · WV

Why Separate Q, K, and V?

A natural question is: why not just use the original input vectors directly? The answer is that the three learned projections (WQ, WK, WV) give the model the flexibility to represent the same input differently depending on its role. The same word or sensor reading might contain different information when acting as a "question" versus when acting as an "answer."

Dimensions

If the input X has dimension dmodel per position, then WQ, WK, and WV are matrices of size dmodel × dk (for Q and K) and dmodel × dv (for V). In the original Transformer, dk = dv = dmodel / h, where h is the number of attention heads. We will explore multi-head attention in Sub-chapter 5.3.

From Inputs to Q, K, V — A Concrete Example

Let us trace through a small example with a sequence of 4 tokens. Suppose each token is represented by a 3-dimensional embedding vector:

Example: 4-Token Sequence

x1 = [0.2,  0.8, −0.1]   ("The")
x2 = [0.9,  0.3,  0.5]   ("beam")
x3 = [0.1, −0.6,  0.7]   ("deflects")
x4 = [0.4,  0.2,  0.9]   ("excessively")

Each xi is multiplied by WQ, WK, and WV to produce qi, ki, and vi. These weight matrices are learned during training so that the resulting Q, K, V vectors capture the right relationships for the task at hand.

Scaled Dot-Product Attention

With Q, K, and V vectors in hand, we can now compute attention. The procedure follows four clear steps:

Step 1: Compute Raw Scores

For each Query, compute the dot product with every Key. The dot product measures the similarity between the Query and each Key — high values mean the Key is relevant to the Query.

Score Computation

score(Q, K) = Q · KT

This produces a matrix of scores where entry (i, j) represents how much position i should attend to position j. For our 4-token example, this is a 4 × 4 matrix.

Step 2: Scale

Divide the raw scores by √dk, the square root of the Key dimension. This scaling is crucial for numerical stability.

Why Scale?

Without scaling, when dk is large, the dot products can grow very large in magnitude. Large values pushed through softmax produce distributions that are extremely peaked — almost all the weight goes to one position. This creates vanishing gradients because the softmax derivatives become tiny.

Dividing by √dk keeps the variance of the scores at approximately 1, ensuring the softmax operates in a region where its gradients are well-behaved.

Civil Engineering Analogy

Scaling is like normalising load values before comparison. When comparing loads on different structural members, you normalise them (e.g., by dividing by member capacity) to get utilisation ratios between 0 and 1. Without normalisation, a 10,000 kN column load and a 50 kN beam load are hard to compare meaningfully. Similarly, the √dk scaling puts attention scores in a comparable range regardless of the dimensionality chosen.

Step 3: Apply Softmax

The scaled scores are passed through the softmax function along each row. Softmax converts each row of raw scores into a probability distribution — all values between 0 and 1, summing to 1. The highest scores become the largest weights, and low scores are pushed toward zero.

Softmax Conversion

weights = softmax(Q · KT / √dk)

Each row of the resulting weight matrix is a probability distribution telling position i how much to attend to every other position. For instance, if position 2 has weights [0.05, 0.10, 0.70, 0.15], it attends primarily to position 3.

Step 4: Weighted Sum of Values

Finally, multiply the attention weights by the Value matrix. Each output position becomes a weighted average of all Value vectors, where the weights come from the attention distribution computed above.

The Complete Attention Formula

Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(Q · KT / √dk) · V

This single equation captures the entire mechanism: compute similarity scores between Queries and Keys, normalise them into a probability distribution, and use that distribution to create a weighted combination of Values.

Matrix Shape Summary

For a sequence of length n with key dimension dk and value dimension dv:
Q: (n × dk),   K: (n × dk),   V: (n × dv),   QKT: (n × n),   Output: (n × dv)

Self-Attention

In the original sequence-to-sequence attention, the Queries come from the decoder (the part generating the output) and the Keys and Values come from the encoder (the part processing the input). The decoder "asks" questions about the encoded input.

Self-attention is a special case where Q, K, and V all come from the same sequence. Each position attends to every other position within the same sequence, including itself. This allows the model to capture internal relationships and dependencies.

What Self-Attention Captures

Consider the sentence: "The beam in Span 3 deflects because the load on Span 3 exceeds the design limit." Self-attention allows the word "deflects" to attend strongly to "beam" and "load" — the words that explain the cause and subject of the deflection. Without self-attention, these connections must be inferred indirectly through positional proximity.

Self-Attention vs. RNN Memory

The critical difference from RNNs is the path length. In an RNN, information from position 1 must pass through every intermediate hidden state to reach position 100 — a path of length 99. In self-attention, the path length between any two positions is always 1. Every position can directly attend to every other position in a single step.

Civil Engineering Analogy

Self-attention is like a round-table design review. Imagine all the engineers on a project sitting in a room together: the structural engineer, the geotechnical engineer, the MEP designer, the architect, the construction manager, and the cost estimator. In an RNN-like process, information passes sequentially around the table — the architect whispers to the structural engineer, who whispers to the geotechnical engineer, and so on. By the time the message reaches the cost estimator, it is garbled.

With self-attention, every person can directly hear and address every other person simultaneously. The cost estimator can ask the architect a direct question without going through four intermediaries. This parallel communication is far more efficient and loses no information along the way.

Computational Cost

The price of this powerful direct access is computational. Self-attention computes an n × n attention matrix, where n is the sequence length. This means O(n2) time and memory complexity. For short sequences (up to a few thousand tokens), this is manageable. For very long sequences, this quadratic cost becomes a bottleneck — a topic addressed by efficient attention variants in later chapters.

For Civil Engineers

In structural health monitoring, sensor sequences of tens of thousands of time steps are common. The O(n2) cost of self-attention means that processing a 10,000-step sequence requires 100 million attention score computations. Techniques like windowed attention or sparse attention (covered later) reduce this to linear cost while preserving most of the benefit.

Step-by-Step Numerical Walkthrough

Let us trace through the complete attention computation with concrete numbers. We will use a tiny example: 3 input positions, each with dk = dv = 2 for simplicity.

Given: Q, K, V Matrices

Q = [[1.0, 0.0],    K = [[1.0, 1.0],    V = [[0.5, 0.2],
     [0.0, 1.0],        [0.0, 1.0],        [0.8, 0.1],
     [1.0, 1.0]]        [1.0, 0.0]]        [0.3, 0.9]]

Step 1: QKT (Raw Scores)

QKT = [[1.0, 0.0, 1.0],
         [1.0, 1.0, 0.0],
         [2.0, 1.0, 1.0]]

Entry (1,1) = q1 · k1 = (1)(1)+(0)(1) = 1.0
Entry (1,3) = q1 · k3 = (1)(1)+(0)(0) = 1.0

Step 2: Scale by √dk = √2 ≈ 1.414

Scaled = [[0.707, 0.000, 0.707],
          [0.707, 0.707, 0.000],
          [1.414, 0.707, 0.707]]

Step 3: Softmax (per row)

Weights = [[0.389, 0.221, 0.389],
           [0.389, 0.389, 0.221],
           [0.506, 0.247, 0.247]]

Each row sums to 1.0 — a valid probability distribution.

Step 4: Multiply Weights × V

Output = [[0.523, 0.428],
          [0.572, 0.316],
          [0.524, 0.348]]

Row 1: 0.389×[0.5,0.2] + 0.221×[0.8,0.1] + 0.389×[0.3,0.9] = [0.523, 0.428]

Position 1 attends equally to positions 1 and 3 (weight 0.389 each), and less to position 2 (0.221). Its output blends the Value vectors accordingly.

Attention in Practice for Civil Engineering

The attention mechanism has direct applications across multiple domains within civil engineering. Here are concrete scenarios where attention provides a measurable advantage:

Structural Health Monitoring

A bridge instrumented with 50 accelerometers generates continuous time-series data. When detecting an anomaly, attention allows the model to focus on the specific time window and specific sensors most relevant to the event. For example, if a truck overload causes excessive vibration in Span 3, the attention weights will peak on the sensors in that span during the overload interval — automatically identifying the location and timing.

Specification Document Analysis

When extracting requirements from a 500-page project specification, attention-based models can learn to focus on the relevant clauses. Given a question like "What is the minimum concrete cover for foundations?", the model attends to the foundation design section and the durability requirements section while ignoring the architectural finishes and project timeline sections.

Construction Progress Tracking

Using attention over sequences of construction site images or daily reports, models can identify which activities are on the critical path and flag delays. The attention mechanism naturally learns to weigh critical-path activities more heavily than non-critical activities when predicting schedule outcomes.

Multi-Sensor Fusion

In geotechnical monitoring, piezometers, inclinometers, settlement markers, and weather stations all provide different types of data about the same site. Attention can learn which sensor types are most informative for predicting a specific outcome (e.g., slope stability) and automatically adjust the weighting as conditions change — attending more to piezometers after heavy rainfall and more to temperature sensors during freeze-thaw cycles.

Key Takeaways

Summary

  • Attention solves the bottleneck problem — instead of compressing an entire sequence into a single vector, it allows the model to directly access any position.
  • Three vectors: Query (what am I looking for?), Key (what do I contain?), Value (what information do I provide?).
  • Scaled dot-product attention: softmax(QKT/√dk) · V — the √dk scaling prevents softmax saturation.
  • Self-attention lets every position attend to every other position within the same sequence, capturing long- range dependencies with O(1) path length.
  • Parallelisable — unlike RNNs, attention can process all positions simultaneously, making it ideal for modern GPU hardware.
  • Interpretable — attention weights reveal what the model focuses on, providing transparency for engineering applications where explainability matters.

Load Path Prioritization

Attention weights in structural analysis models learn to focus on critical load paths — similar to how an engineer identifies the most stressed members in a truss during analysis.
