Chapter 5 — Sub-chapter 5.10

CE Case Study: AI-Assisted Design Review

A structural engineering firm deploys NLP and LLM technologies to transform its design review process. This end-to-end case study traces the full pipeline — from OCR-based document ingestion through NER-driven information extraction, RAG-grounded code compliance checking, and automated report generation — culminating in human review and sign-off. We quantify time savings, accuracy improvements, error detection rates, and cost-benefit outcomes, while documenting every implementation challenge and lesson learned along the way.

55 min read 2 interactive demos 4 quiz questions

The Scenario

Meridian Structural Consultants is a mid-size structural engineering firm with 45 engineers across three offices. They handle 80–120 design review projects per year, ranging from residential multi-storey buildings to industrial warehouses and infrastructure projects. Each review involves checking structural drawings, design calculations, and specifications against applicable building codes — primarily IS 456 (reinforced concrete), IS 800 (structural steel), IS 1893 (seismic design), ACI 318, and Eurocode 2.

The Problem

Traditional design review at Meridian is a manually intensive process. A senior engineer spends 8–14 working days on a typical medium-complexity project review. The process involves reading through hundreds of pages of drawings and calculations, cross-referencing against multiple code documents, checking dimensional consistency, verifying load paths, and producing a comprehensive review report with annotated comments. The firm faces a growing backlog: demand for reviews outstrips the capacity of their experienced engineers, and junior engineers lack the expertise to conduct reviews independently.

The Objective

Meridian's leadership decides to pilot an AI-assisted design review system. The goal is not to replace human reviewers but to augment them — automating the repetitive, rule-based checks so that human experts can focus on judgment-intensive aspects: design philosophy, load path adequacy, detailing quality, and constructability. The target is to reduce review time by 60–70% while maintaining or improving review accuracy.

Civil Engineering Analogy

Think of the AI-assisted review system like an advanced concrete testing laboratory. The lab automates routine compressive strength tests, slump tests, and gradation analyses — producing reliable, standardised results far faster than manual testing. But the materials engineer still interprets the results, makes mix design decisions, and signs off on the concrete's fitness for purpose. The lab does not replace the engineer; it gives the engineer better data, faster.

Traditional Design Review Workflow

Before examining the AI-assisted pipeline, we must understand the baseline. Meridian's traditional design review follows a well-established sequential process that has evolved over decades of practice.

Phase 1: Document Receipt & Organisation (Days 1–2)

The design documents arrive as a mix of PDF drawings, calculation sheets (often scanned), specification documents, and geotechnical reports. A junior engineer spends 1–2 days organising the documents, creating a tracking spreadsheet, and assembling the applicable code documents. Missing documents are identified and requested from the design team, often introducing delays.

Phase 2: Preliminary Review (Days 2–4)

A senior engineer conducts a preliminary scan of all drawings and calculations. This involves reading through the general arrangement drawings, understanding the structural system, identifying the key members, and noting the design assumptions (material grades, load combinations, seismic zone, exposure conditions). The engineer manually extracts key parameters and records them for systematic checking.

Phase 3: Detailed Checking (Days 4–10)

The core of the review: the engineer checks every structural member against the applicable code. For a reinforced concrete building, this means verifying beam flexure and shear capacities, column interaction checks, slab design, foundation bearing pressures, development lengths, detailing provisions (minimum reinforcement, maximum spacing, cover requirements), and deflection limits. Each check requires locating the relevant code clause, extracting the applicable formula and limits, and verifying the designer's calculations.

Phase 4: Report Preparation (Days 10–12)

The reviewer prepares a formal review report, documenting each comment with the drawing reference, element identification, the specific code clause, the required value, the provided value, and the disposition (acceptable / revise / clarify). A typical medium-project review generates 80–200 comments. Formatting, cross-referencing, and quality-checking the report itself consumes 2–3 days.

Phase 5: Internal QA & Sign-off (Days 12–14)

A second senior engineer reviews the review report for completeness and accuracy. Discrepancies are resolved, the report is finalised, and the principal engineer signs off. The complete cycle from receipt to issuance typically takes 10–14 working days for a medium-complexity project.

Pain Points in the Traditional Process

  • Time-intensive: 80% of review time is spent on repetitive code-lookup and parameter-checking tasks
  • Error-prone: Manual transcription of values from drawings to spreadsheets introduces human errors
  • Inconsistent: Different reviewers apply different levels of scrutiny; junior reviewers miss subtle issues
  • Bottlenecked: Only 6 of the firm's 45 engineers have sufficient experience to lead reviews
  • Documentation overhead: Report writing consumes 20–25% of total review time
  • Code navigation: Engineers spend significant time searching through code documents for the relevant clause

AI-Assisted Pipeline Overview

Meridian's AI system is built as a five-stage pipeline. Each stage has specific AI/ML technologies, well-defined inputs and outputs, and clear boundaries where human oversight is required. The architecture follows the RAG paradigm studied in Sub-chapter 5.8, with NER and OCR technologies from earlier chapters integrated into the front end.

System Architecture

The pipeline operates in five sequential stages, each feeding into the next. The system is designed with explicit checkpoints where a human reviewer can inspect, override, or halt the process. No output reaches the client without human sign-off.

  1. Document Ingestion — OCR + PDF parsing of structural drawings and specifications
  2. Information Extraction — NER for materials, dimensions, loads, member sizes
  3. Code Compliance Checking — RAG-based lookup against IS 456, ACI 318, Eurocode 2
  4. Automated Report Generation — LLM generates structured review comments
  5. Human Review & Sign-off — Engineer validates, edits, and approves the report

Stage 1: Document Ingestion

The first challenge is converting heterogeneous engineering documents — scanned drawings, native PDFs, handwritten calculation sheets, and specification documents — into machine-readable text. This stage combines multiple technologies.

OCR Pipeline

Structural drawings present unique OCR challenges: text appears at arbitrary angles, mixed with graphical elements (dimension lines, section marks, reinforcement schedules), and uses engineering-specific notation (diameter symbols, reinforcement bar designations like "4-20T + 2-16T"). The system uses a two-pass OCR approach:

  • Pass 1 — Layout analysis: A CNN-based document layout model segments the drawing into regions: title block, beam schedule, column schedule, general notes, section details, and reinforcement tables
  • Pass 2 — Region-specific OCR: Each region is processed with specialised OCR models. Table regions use a table-extraction model that preserves row/column structure. Text regions use a standard OCR engine fine-tuned on engineering notation. Dimension text uses a separate model trained on rotated and scaled engineering fonts

Native PDF Parsing

For digitally-created PDFs (from AutoCAD, Revit, ETABS output), the system bypasses OCR entirely and extracts text directly from the PDF structure. This is significantly more accurate. The parser handles multi-column layouts, embedded tables, equation blocks, and cross-references. Structural analysis software outputs (ETABS, STAAD, SAP2000) are parsed using format-specific templates that map output fields to a standardised data schema.

Output: Structured Document Store

The ingestion stage produces a structured document store: a JSON-based representation where every text element is tagged with its source document, page number, region type, and spatial coordinates. Reinforcement schedules are extracted into structured tables. Design parameters are tagged with confidence scores. Elements with OCR confidence below 85% are flagged for manual verification. The ingestion stage achieves an overall text accuracy of 94.2% on engineering documents, compared to 99.5% for native PDFs.

Stage 2: Information Extraction (NER)

Raw text is insufficient for automated checking. The system must identify and classify specific engineering entities: material grades, member dimensions, reinforcement details, applied loads, and code references. This is the domain of Named Entity Recognition (NER), adapted for civil engineering.

Custom NER Model

Meridian trained a custom NER model on 2,400 annotated engineering documents. The model recognises 18 entity types specific to structural engineering:

  • MATERIAL_GRADE: M25, M30, Fe415, Fe500, E250, S355
  • MEMBER_TYPE: beam, column, slab, footing, wall, lintel
  • DIMENSION: 300x600 mm, 450 mm dia, 150 mm thick
  • REINFORCEMENT: 4-20T, 6-25T + 2-20T, 8mm @ 150 c/c
  • LOAD_VALUE: 25 kN/m, 150 kN, 3.5 kN/m²
  • LOAD_TYPE: dead load, live load, seismic, wind
  • CODE_REF: IS 456 Cl. 26.5.1.1, ACI 318 §9.6.1.2
  • COVER: 40 mm clear cover, 25 mm nominal cover
  • EXPOSURE: mild, moderate, severe, very severe
  • SPAN: 6.0 m clear span, 7.5 m effective span

Relation Extraction

NER alone identifies entities; relation extraction links them. The system identifies relationships such as "Beam B1 has dimensions 300x600 mm with 4-20T bottom reinforcement and M30 concrete." These relations are critical for downstream compliance checking: the system must know which reinforcement belongs to which member at which location. Relation extraction achieves 91.7% F1 score on the test set.

Knowledge Graph Construction

Extracted entities and relations are assembled into a project knowledge graph: a structured representation where every structural member is a node, connected to its properties (dimensions, materials, reinforcement, loads) and to other members (load paths, connections, support conditions). This graph becomes the queryable backbone for compliance checking. An engineer can ask "Show me all columns with concrete grade below M30 in seismic zone IV" and get an instant, accurate answer.

Stage 3: Code Compliance Checking

This is the intellectual core of the system. Given a structured knowledge graph of the project's structural elements and their properties, the system checks each element against the applicable design code provisions. The approach uses RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) to ground every check in the actual code text, as discussed in Sub-chapter 5.8.

Code Knowledge Base

The RAG system's retrieval corpus contains the full text of the following standards, chunked into semantically coherent segments with overlap:

  • IS 456:2000 — Plain and Reinforced Concrete (with amendments)
  • IS 800:2007 — General Construction in Steel
  • IS 1893 (Part 1):2016 — Earthquake Resistant Design
  • IS 13920:2016 — Ductile Design and Detailing of RC Structures
  • ACI 318-19 — Building Code Requirements for Structural Concrete
  • Eurocode 2 (EN 1992-1-1) — Design of Concrete Structures
  • SP 16:1980 — Design Aids for Reinforced Concrete to IS 456

Each code chunk is embedded using a domain-fine-tuned embedding model (based on E5-large, fine-tuned on 15,000 engineering Q&A pairs). The embeddings are stored in a vector database with metadata tags for code name, edition, clause number, and topic category.

Rule-Based Pre-Checks

Before invoking the LLM, the system performs deterministic rule-based checks for provisions that can be expressed as simple numerical comparisons. These checks are hard-coded and do not depend on AI inference:

  • Minimum concrete cover per exposure class (IS 456 Table 16)
  • Minimum and maximum reinforcement ratios (IS 456 Cl. 26.5.1)
  • Maximum spacing of shear reinforcement (IS 456 Cl. 26.5.1.5)
  • Minimum member dimensions for seismic detailing (IS 13920)
  • Width-to-depth ratios for beams and columns
  • Development length requirements (IS 456 Cl. 26.2)

These rule-based checks are 100% accurate by construction (they implement the code provisions directly) and process thousands of checks per second. They catch approximately 40% of all code violations without any LLM involvement.

RAG-Based Complex Checks

For provisions that require interpretation, contextual understanding, or multi-clause reasoning, the system uses RAG. The query is formulated from the knowledge graph (e.g., "For a beam with b=300mm, d=550mm, fck=30 MPa, fy=500 MPa, clear span=6.0m, carrying UDL of 35 kN/m: check if 4-20T bottom bars satisfy the flexural capacity requirement per IS 456 Cl. 38.1"). The retriever fetches the relevant code clauses, and the LLM performs the calculation, providing a step-by-step derivation with clause references at each step.

Confidence Scoring & Verification

Every RAG-based check receives a confidence score based on:

  • Retrieval confidence: Cosine similarity between query and retrieved chunks (threshold: 0.82)
  • Self-consistency: The check is run 3 times with temperature=0.1; if all 3 agree, confidence is "high"
  • Citation verification: Every clause number cited in the response is verified against the code index
  • Numerical validation: Calculated values are cross-checked against the rule-based engine where possible

Checks with confidence below "medium" are flagged for mandatory human review. In practice, approximately 12% of RAG-based checks require manual verification.

Stage 4: Automated Report Generation

The compliance checking stage produces a structured dataset of check results. The report generation stage transforms this data into a professional design review document that matches Meridian's existing report format and tone.

LLM-Generated Review Comments

For each compliance check result, the LLM generates a review comment in Meridian's standard format:

Comment #: RC-042
Drawing Ref: S-12, Grid C3-C5
Element: Beam B14 (300x600mm)
Check: Minimum shear reinforcement
Code Ref: IS 456:2000, Cl. 26.5.1.6
Required: 8mm dia @ 300 c/c (A_sv,min = 0.4b*s/0.87*f_y)
Provided: 8mm dia @ 350 c/c
Status: REVISE
Remark: Shear stirrup spacing exceeds maximum permitted. Reduce spacing to 300mm c/c or less.

Report Structure

The generated report includes:

  • Executive summary: Total checks performed, pass/fail/review counts, critical findings
  • Design basis verification: Material grades, load combinations, code editions confirmed
  • Member-by-member results: Organised by structural system (beams, columns, slabs, foundations)
  • Code compliance matrix: Tabular summary of all code provisions checked and their status
  • Critical findings: High-priority items requiring immediate attention, with severity ranking
  • Appendix: Detailed calculations for all REVISE dispositions with step-by-step derivations
Key Design Decision

The system generates the report in a structured intermediate format (JSON), not directly as a Word document. This allows the human reviewer to modify, reorder, add, or remove comments before the final document is rendered. The LLM produces content; the formatting is deterministic. This separation ensures that layout changes never require re-running the AI pipeline.

Stage 5: Human Review & Sign-off

The final stage is non-negotiable: a qualified professional engineer reviews every AI-generated comment, validates the compliance checks, exercises engineering judgment on ambiguous cases, and signs the report. The AI system is a tool; the engineer is the professional.

Review Interface

Meridian built a custom review interface that presents each AI-generated comment alongside the source drawing region and the retrieved code clause. The engineer can:

  • Accept: Approve the comment as-is
  • Edit: Modify the wording, add context, or change the disposition
  • Reject: Remove a false positive or incorrect comment
  • Escalate: Flag for senior review when the AI's assessment is uncertain
  • Add: Insert new comments that the AI missed

Human Reviewer Metrics

After six months of operation, Meridian tracked the human reviewer's actions on AI-generated comments:

  • Accepted as-is: 72% of comments
  • Edited (minor wording): 16% of comments
  • Rejected (false positive): 8% of comments
  • Escalated: 3% of comments
  • Added (AI missed): ~1% additional comments added by human reviewer

The 8% false positive rate and 1% miss rate (comments the AI failed to generate) are key quality metrics. The false positive rate is manageable — rejecting an incorrect comment takes seconds. The 1% miss rate confirms that the AI catches the vast majority of code violations.

Key Findings

The AI system excels at rule-based, quantitative checks where the code provision can be expressed as a numerical comparison. It consistently outperforms human reviewers in catching minimum reinforcement, cover, and spacing violations — errors that humans frequently overlook due to fatigue, particularly in large projects with hundreds of members.

Conversely, humans dramatically outperform the AI on judgment-intensive, holistic assessments: load path adequacy, constructability, and design philosophy. These require spatial reasoning, experience-based intuition, and an understanding of construction practice that current AI systems cannot replicate. This finding validates the "AI as augmentation" approach rather than replacement.

Engineering Insight

The AI is like a meticulous quantity surveyor who checks every dimension on every drawing against the bill of quantities — tireless, consistent, and thorough. But the experienced project manager still walks the site, looks at the soil conditions, talks to the contractor, and decides whether the proposed construction sequence will actually work. Both roles are essential; neither can fully replace the other.

Cost-Benefit Analysis

Meridian conducted a rigorous cost-benefit analysis after the six-month pilot. The numbers below reflect actual costs, not projections.

Implementation Costs (One-Time)

  • NER model training & annotation: 3,200 person-hours (12 months, 2 ML engineers + 1 domain expert)
  • RAG system development: 2,100 person-hours (code chunking, embedding, retrieval pipeline)
  • OCR pipeline & document parser: 1,800 person-hours
  • Review interface development: 1,200 person-hours
  • Infrastructure (GPU servers, vector DB): Initial setup cost
  • Total one-time investment: Approximately 8,300 person-hours plus infrastructure

Ongoing Costs (Monthly)

  • LLM API costs: Approximately 1,500–2,500 USD/month (GPT-4 class model for complex checks)
  • Cloud infrastructure: 800–1,200 USD/month (vector DB, compute, storage)
  • ML engineer maintenance: 0.25 FTE for model monitoring, updates, and fine-tuning
  • Code updates: 40–60 hours per year to update the knowledge base when codes are revised

Benefits Realised

  • Time savings: 11 days per project × 100 projects/year = 1,100 senior-engineer-days saved
  • Throughput increase: Review capacity increased from 100 to 180 projects/year without new hires
  • Quality improvement: 23% increase in code violations detected per review
  • Consistency: Standard deviation in review thoroughness reduced by 67%
  • Junior engineer enablement: Junior engineers can now lead reviews with AI assistance, with senior oversight reduced to the human review phase only

Break-even point: The system paid for its development costs within 8 months of deployment (month 14 from project start). By month 18, cumulative savings exceeded development costs by 2.3×. The projected 3-year ROI is 5.8× the total investment.

Implementation Challenges

The path to deployment was not smooth. Meridian encountered significant technical, organisational, and cultural challenges that offer valuable lessons for firms considering similar systems.

OCR Quality on Legacy Drawings

Older scanned drawings (pre-2005) with low resolution, faded ink, or hand-drawn annotations produced OCR accuracy as low as 72%. The system required a separate processing path for legacy documents, including image enhancement (contrast adjustment, de-skewing, noise removal) and manual verification of extracted parameters. This added 0.5–1.0 days to the review timeline for legacy projects.

Engineering Notation Ambiguity

Engineering notation is rife with ambiguity: "4-20T" could be 4 bars of 20mm diameter (correct) or a range from 4 to 20 (incorrect parse). "150 c/c" means centre-to-centre spacing of 150mm, but the NER model initially confused this with dimension values. Resolving these ambiguities required extensive domain-specific training data and context-aware parsing rules. The model needed to understand that "8mm @ 150 c/c" is a stirrup specification, not two separate dimensions.

Code Interpretation Edge Cases

Building codes contain provisions that require interpretation, not just lookup. IS 456 Clause 23.2.1 specifies deflection limits but notes that these may be exceeded "under certain conditions" with proper justification. The AI system initially treated all such provisions as hard limits, generating false positives. Handling interpretive provisions required adding a classification layer that distinguishes between mandatory provisions ("shall"), recommended provisions ("should"), and conditional provisions ("may, subject to...").

Cultural Resistance

Several senior engineers initially resisted the system, viewing it as a threat to their expertise. "I've been doing design reviews for 25 years — I don't need a machine to tell me what to check." Overcoming this required demonstrating that the system enhanced rather than replaced their role, involved them in the system design, and showed them concrete examples of errors the system caught that they had missed. After three months, the same engineers became the system's strongest advocates.

Data Confidentiality

Client drawings and design documents are confidential. Sending them to cloud-based LLM APIs raised data privacy concerns. Meridian addressed this by deploying an on-premise LLM (Llama 2 70B, later upgraded to Llama 3) for the compliance checking stage, using cloud APIs only for the less-sensitive report formatting stage with anonymised data. This hybrid approach maintained confidentiality while leveraging the superior language quality of frontier models for report generation.

Lessons Learned

Start with Rule-Based, Add AI Incrementally

The most impactful early wins came from deterministic rule-based checks, not AI. Minimum cover, maximum spacing, and reinforcement ratio checks are simple conditionals that can be coded in a weekend and deliver immediate value. Adding RAG-based complex reasoning on top of this rule-based foundation was far more effective than building an entirely AI-driven system from scratch.

Domain Expert Involvement is Non-Negotiable

Every stage of development required deep involvement from practising structural engineers. The ML engineers built the infrastructure; the domain experts defined what to check, how to check it, and what constitutes a correct result. Projects that minimise domain expert involvement produce technically impressive but practically useless systems.

False Positives Are More Damaging Than False Negatives

A system that generates too many incorrect review comments will be ignored by engineers. Meridian learned that tuning for precision (fewer false positives) was more important than tuning for recall (catching every possible issue). Engineers trust a system that is right 95% of the time far more than one that catches everything but is wrong 30% of the time. The target was set at less than 10% false positive rate.

The Human Review Phase Cannot Be Shortened

Despite pressure to reduce the human review time (the remaining bottleneck), Meridian's leadership decided that the 1.5-day human review phase was the minimum acceptable for professional liability. The AI saves time in preparation and checking; it does not save time in professional judgment. Attempting to compress the human review phase would undermine the system's fundamental value proposition of augmented (not autonomous) design review.

Continuous Feedback Loop is Essential

Every human correction (edit, reject, add) is fed back into the system as training data. After six months, the false positive rate dropped from 14% to 8%, and the miss rate from 3.5% to 1%. Without this feedback loop, the system would stagnate. Firms must budget for ongoing model improvement, not just initial deployment.

Future Directions

Meridian's roadmap extends the AI-assisted review system in several directions, driven by emerging capabilities in multimodal AI and foundation models.

Multimodal Models Reading Drawings Directly

The current pipeline relies on OCR to convert drawings to text before analysis. Emerging multimodal models (GPT-4V, Gemini Pro Vision, Claude 3) can interpret images directly. The vision is to feed a structural drawing sheet directly to a multimodal model and have it identify structural elements, read dimensions and reinforcement details, understand connection details from section drawings, and perform compliance checks — all from the visual representation, eliminating the OCR stage entirely.

Early experiments show promising results for simple drawings (85% accuracy on beam schedules) but significant challenges remain for complex detail drawings with overlapping annotations, multiple scales, and non-standard layouts. Meridian estimates this capability will be production-ready within 2–3 years.

BIM Integration

Integrating the review system with BIM (Building Information Modelling) software would enable direct access to the 3D model data, eliminating both the OCR stage and the information extraction stage. The system could query the BIM model directly for member dimensions, material properties, and reinforcement details, then run compliance checks against the code knowledge base. Meridian is developing an IFC parser that maps BIM entities to their compliance checking schema.

Multi-Code, Multi-Jurisdiction Support

The current system supports IS, ACI, and Eurocode standards. Future development will add support for BS EN (British), AS (Australian), and NZS (New Zealand) codes, with automatic code selection based on project location. The challenge is not just adding code text to the RAG corpus but understanding the philosophical differences between code systems (limit state vs. working stress, partial safety factors vs. load and resistance factor design).

AI-Assisted Design (Not Just Review)

The ultimate vision extends beyond review to design assistance: given a set of design requirements (spans, loads, material constraints, code requirements), the AI system could propose preliminary member sizes, reinforcement layouts, and connection details. The engineer would review and refine the AI's proposals, rather than starting from scratch. This reverses the workflow: instead of AI checking human designs, humans would refine AI-generated designs.

Summary

Key Takeaways

  • Five-stage pipeline: Document ingestion (OCR/PDF), information extraction (NER), code compliance checking (rule-based + RAG), report generation (LLM), and human review & sign-off
  • 79% time reduction: Average design review compressed from 14 working days to 3 working days
  • AI excels at quantitative checks: 95–99% detection rates for reinforcement, cover, and spacing violations, consistently outperforming human reviewers
  • Humans excel at judgment: Load path assessment, constructability, and design philosophy remain firmly in the human domain (85–91% human vs 8–42% AI)
  • Rule-based foundation: 40% of violations caught by simple deterministic checks with zero AI overhead
  • False positive management: Maintaining less than 10% false positive rate is critical for engineer trust and system adoption
  • ROI of 5.8x: System paid for itself within 8 months of deployment; 3-year projected return is 5.8 times total investment
  • Human sign-off is non-negotiable: Professional liability rests with the signing engineer, not the AI system
  • Continuous improvement: Feedback from human corrections reduces false positive rate over time (14% to 8% in six months)
  • Future: multimodal models will eliminate OCR by reading drawings directly; BIM integration will eliminate information extraction

This case study demonstrates that AI-assisted design review is not a futuristic concept but a practical, deployable reality for structural engineering firms today. The key to success is treating AI as an augmentation tool — not a replacement — for professional engineering judgment. The engineer's stamp, experience, and accountability remain at the centre of the process. AI simply gives the engineer better tools to work with.

Final Reflection

The transition from manual to AI-assisted design review mirrors the transition from hand calculations to computer analysis in the 1980s and 1990s. Engineers who mastered STAAD and ETABS did not become less important — they became more productive. The same principle applies here: engineers who learn to work with AI will not be replaced by AI. They will be replaced by engineers who use AI.
