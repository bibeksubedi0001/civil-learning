Chapter 5 — Sub-chapter 5.4

BERT & Encoder Models

In 2018, Google introduced BERT — Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers — and shattered every NLP benchmark simultaneously. By reading text in both directions and learning deep contextual representations, BERT demonstrated that a single pre-trained model could be fine-tuned for virtually any language-understanding task, from document classification to named entity recognition.

35 min read 2 interactive demos 4 quiz questions

The Paper That Redefined NLP (2018)

In October 2018, Devlin et al. at Google AI published "BERT: Pre-training of Deep Bidirectional Transformers for Language Understanding." The central insight was deceptively simple: take the Transformer encoder (which we explored in Sub-chapter 5.3), train it on massive unlabelled text with two clever self-supervised tasks, and then fine-tune the same model for dozens of downstream tasks with minimal task-specific architecture changes.

BERT achieved state-of-the-art results on 11 NLP benchmarks simultaneously, some by enormous margins. It was the beginning of the "pre-train, then fine-tune" paradigm that now dominates modern AI.

Before BERT, NLP models were typically trained from scratch for each task. A sentiment classifier was a different model from a named entity recogniser, which was different from a question-answering system. BERT changed this: one general-purpose language understanding model could be adapted to all of these tasks, much as a well-educated civil engineer can apply their fundamental knowledge to structural design, geotechnical analysis, or project management.

Prerequisites

This sub-chapter assumes familiarity with the Transformer encoder architecture from Sub-chapter 5.3: multi-head self-attention, feed-forward layers, residual connections, and layer normalisation. BERT is built entirely from Transformer encoder layers — it discards the decoder.

Why BERT Matters for Civil Engineers

BERT and its variants power the NLP tools that are increasingly entering the construction industry. Automated classification of construction documents (specifications, RFIs, submittals, daily reports), extraction of named entities from engineering texts (material names, structural members, code references), and sentiment analysis of safety reports all rely on encoder-based models. Understanding how BERT works allows you to evaluate these tools, anticipate their limitations, and communicate effectively with the data science teams deploying them on your projects.

The Pre-Trained Inspector Analogy

Civil Engineering Analogy

BERT is like a highly experienced construction inspector who has spent years reading every type of engineering document. During their career (pre-training), this inspector has read hundreds of thousands of specifications, inspection reports, RFIs, change orders, safety plans, and design drawings. They have never been told what to look for specifically — they have simply developed a deep understanding of engineering language, technical vocabulary, document structure, and how different concepts relate.

When you assign this inspector to a new project (fine-tuning), you do not need to teach them engineering from scratch. You simply say: "On this project, I need you to flag safety violations" (a classification task) or "I need you to identify every concrete mix reference" (a named entity recognition task). With just a brief orientation and a few examples, the inspector can apply their deep general knowledge to the specific task.

The bidirectional nature of BERT means the inspector reads each sentence considering both the words that come before and after the current word. When they encounter the word "column" in a sentence, they look both left and right to determine whether it refers to a structural column, a column in a spreadsheet, or a column of text.

This analogy captures the two-phase nature of BERT: an expensive, one-time pre-training phase (the inspector's years of experience), and a cheap, fast fine-tuning phase (the project-specific orientation). The pre-training captures general language understanding; fine-tuning directs it towards a specific task.

The Encoder-Only Architecture

In Sub-chapter 5.3 we saw that the original Transformer has both an encoder and a decoder. BERT makes a decisive architectural choice: it uses only the encoder stack. The decoder, with its masked self-attention and autoregressive generation, is discarded entirely.

BERT Architecture Summary

  • BERT-Base: 12 encoder layers, 768 hidden dimensions, 12 attention heads, 110M parameters
  • BERT-Large: 24 encoder layers, 1024 hidden dimensions, 16 attention heads, 340M parameters
  • Maximum sequence length: 512 tokens
  • Vocabulary size: 30,522 tokens (WordPiece)
  • Positional embeddings: Learned (not sinusoidal)

The lack of a decoder means BERT cannot generate text autoregressively (that is GPT's domain, covered in Sub-chapter 5.5). Instead, BERT excels at understanding tasks: reading a piece of text and producing a rich representation of its meaning. Every token in the input receives a contextualised embedding that captures its meaning within the context of the entire sequence.

Why Encoder-Only?

The critical advantage is bidirectionality. The encoder's self-attention has no causal mask — every token can attend to every other token in the sequence, both left and right. This means BERT can use the full context when representing any word. Compare this to a left-to-right decoder (like GPT), where each token can only see the tokens that came before it.

Civil Engineering Analogy

Bidirectional attention is like reading a construction specification with the full document open in front of you. When you encounter a reference to "the reinforcement described in Section 3.2," you can flip forward to Section 3.2 to understand it. A unidirectional model would be like reading the spec one page at a time, with all future pages hidden — you would have to guess what Section 3.2 says until you reached it.

Input Representation & Special Tokens

BERT's input format is carefully designed to handle both single sentences and sentence pairs. Every input sequence begins with a special [CLS] (classification) token and sentence boundaries are marked with [SEP] (separator) tokens.

The [CLS] Token

The [CLS] token is prepended to every input sequence. After passing through all encoder layers, the final hidden state of [CLS] serves as the aggregate sequence representation for classification tasks. Because self-attention allows [CLS] to attend to every other token in the sequence, its final representation captures information from the entire input.

Input Embeddings

BERT's input representation is the sum of three embeddings:

Three-Part Embedding

  1. Token Embedding: A learned vector for each token in the vocabulary (30,522 tokens via WordPiece tokenization)
  2. Segment Embedding: Indicates whether the token belongs to Sentence A or Sentence B (for sentence-pair tasks). All tokens in the first sentence get embedding EA, all tokens in the second get EB
  3. Position Embedding: A learned embedding for each position (0 to 511), unlike the sinusoidal encoding in the original Transformer

Input = TokenEmbed + SegmentEmbed + PositionEmbed

Example Input Format

Single Sentence

[CLS] The concrete compressive strength is 40 MPa [SEP]

Sentence Pair

[CLS] The beam span is 12 metres [SEP] The deflection limit is L/250 [SEP]

WordPiece Tokenization

BERT does not operate on words directly. Instead, it uses WordPiece tokenization, a subword tokenization algorithm that breaks text into a mix of whole words and word fragments. This gives BERT a fixed vocabulary of 30,522 tokens while still being able to represent any word — even rare technical terms it has never seen.

How WordPiece Works

Common words remain as single tokens. Rare or compound words are split into subword pieces, with continuation pieces prefixed by ##.

  • "concrete" → concrete (common word, single token)
  • "prestressed" → pre ##stressed (split into subwords)
  • "geotechnical" → ge ##ote ##ch ##nic ##al (rare term, multiple pieces)
  • "MPa" → mp ##a (engineering unit)

The ## prefix signals that this piece should be attached to the previous token when reconstructing the original word. This approach balances vocabulary size with coverage: BERT can represent any text using a manageable vocabulary.

Civil Engineering Analogy

WordPiece is like a standardised parts catalogue for prefabricated construction. Common components (beams, columns, slabs) are stocked as single units. Unusual components are assembled from standard sub-parts. You do not need to stock every possible beam size — you keep a fixed set of standard sections and combine them as needed. Similarly, WordPiece keeps a fixed vocabulary but can represent any word by combining subword pieces.

Tokenization Trade-off

When BERT splits a rare technical term into many subword pieces, each piece consumes one of the 512 available positions. A heavily jargon-laden construction document may exhaust the sequence length faster than general text. This is one reason why domain-specific BERT variants (like SciBERT or construction-domain models) use vocabularies better suited to technical text.

Pre-Training Task 1: Masked Language Modelling (MLM)

The first and most important pre-training task is Masked Language Modelling (MLM). During pre-training, BERT randomly selects 15% of the input tokens and replaces them with a special [MASK] token. The model must then predict the original token at each masked position, using the surrounding context in both directions.

MLM Procedure

  1. Randomly select 15% of tokens in the input
  2. Of those selected:
    • 80% are replaced with [MASK]
    • 10% are replaced with a random token
    • 10% are left unchanged
  3. The model predicts the original token at each selected position
  4. Loss is computed only on the selected positions, not the entire sequence

Why the 80/10/10 Split?

If BERT only ever saw [MASK] tokens during pre-training, it would learn representations optimised for masked inputs. But during fine-tuning and inference, there are no [MASK] tokens. The 10% random replacement and 10% unchanged tokens force BERT to maintain good representations for all tokens, not just masked ones, reducing the mismatch between pre-training and fine-tuning.

MLM Example — Construction Context

Original: The steel reinforcement shall have a minimum yield strength of 500 MPa

Masked: The steel [MASK] shall have a minimum [MASK] strength of 500 MPa

BERT must predict "reinforcement" and "yield" from the surrounding context. To do this correctly, it must understand construction vocabulary, typical specification phrasing, and the relationship between steel, reinforcement, yield strength, and units.

Civil Engineering Analogy

MLM is like a cloze test for engineering students. Imagine covering random words in a structural design calculation and asking a student to fill in the blanks. A student who has studied extensively can fill in "moment" when they see "___ = wL²/8" or "deflection" when they see "the maximum ___ shall not exceed L/250." The ability to fill these blanks demonstrates deep understanding of the subject matter, not just memorisation.

Pre-Training Task 2: Next Sentence Prediction (NSP)

The second pre-training task is Next Sentence Prediction (NSP). Many NLP tasks (question answering, natural language inference) require understanding the relationship between two sentences. MLM alone captures token-level context but not inter-sentence relationships.

NSP Procedure

  1. Take a pair of sentences (A, B) from the training corpus
  2. 50% of the time, B is the actual next sentence after A (label: IsNext)
  3. 50% of the time, B is a random sentence from the corpus (label: NotNext)
  4. The model uses the [CLS] token representation to predict whether B follows A

NSP Example — Construction Context

IsNext (positive):
A: "The foundation shall be designed for a bearing capacity of 200 kPa."
B: "Plate load tests shall be conducted to verify the design assumptions."

NotNext (negative):
A: "The foundation shall be designed for a bearing capacity of 200 kPa."
B: "Traffic signal timing shall comply with local authority requirements."

NSP Controversy

Later research (notably RoBERTa) showed that NSP may actually hurt performance on some tasks. The issue is that the "NotNext" examples are too easy — randomly sampled sentences are usually from completely different topics, so the model learns to detect topic shifts rather than logical coherence. RoBERTa removes NSP entirely and achieves better results. We discuss this in the Variants section below.

Pre-Training Data & Procedure

BERT was pre-trained on two large text corpora:

Training Corpora

  • BooksCorpus: 800M words from unpublished books (diverse writing styles and long-form text)
  • English Wikipedia: 2,500M words (factual, structured, encyclopaedic text)

Combined: approximately 3.3 billion words of English text. Pre-training BERT-Large took 4 days on 64 TPUs — a computational expense that few organisations could afford in 2018. This is why the pre-train/fine-tune paradigm is so powerful: Google bears the pre-training cost once, and everyone benefits by fine-tuning.

Pre-Training Configuration

BERT was trained for 1,000,000 steps with a batch size of 256 sequences, each up to 512 tokens. The learning rate was warmed up over the first 10,000 steps, then linearly decayed. The Adam optimiser was used with weight decay regularisation. The total pre-training loss is the sum of the MLM loss and the NSP loss.

Civil Engineering Analogy

Pre-training is like a civil engineering degree programme. The university (Google) invests enormous resources to teach fundamental principles: structural mechanics, soil mechanics, fluid dynamics, materials science. No specific project is in mind — the goal is to produce a broadly competent engineer. Fine-tuning is the first six months on a real project, where the engineer quickly adapts their general knowledge to the specific project's context, codes, and requirements.

Fine-Tuning for Downstream Tasks

Fine-tuning is remarkably simple. You take the pre-trained BERT model, add a small task-specific output layer on top, and train the entire model (BERT + output layer) on your labelled data for a few epochs. The pre-trained weights provide excellent initialisation, so fine-tuning is fast and requires relatively little data.

Common Fine-Tuning Tasks

  • Sequence Classification (e.g., document type): Use [CLS] hidden state → linear layer → softmax over classes
  • Token Classification / NER (e.g., entity extraction): Use each token's hidden state → linear layer → label per token
  • Sentence Pair Classification (e.g., textual entailment): Input both sentences with [SEP] → [CLS] hidden state → classify relationship
  • Question Answering (extractive): Input question + passage → predict start and end positions of the answer span

Fine-Tuning Hyperparameters

The BERT paper recommends these hyperparameter ranges for fine-tuning:

Recommended Ranges

  • Learning rate: 2e-5, 3e-5, or 5e-5
  • Batch size: 16 or 32
  • Epochs: 2, 3, or 4
  • Warm-up: 10% of total steps

Fine-tuning typically takes minutes to hours on a single GPU, compared to the days on 64 TPUs required for pre-training. This dramatic asymmetry is the key practical benefit of the transfer learning approach.

Civil Engineering Applications

BERT-based models are already being applied to construction and civil engineering tasks. Here are three practical applications that demonstrate the power of encoder models in the built environment.

Application 1: Document Classification

Construction projects generate thousands of documents: specifications, shop drawings, RFIs (Requests for Information), submittals, daily reports, change orders, safety reports, and meeting minutes. A BERT-based classifier can automatically categorise incoming documents by type, routing them to the correct team member.

How it works: Fine-tune BERT for sequence classification. The [CLS] token's final hidden state is fed through a linear layer with as many outputs as document types. A softmax produces a probability distribution over categories. With just a few hundred labelled examples per category, the model achieves high accuracy because the pre-trained representations already understand document structure and engineering vocabulary.

Application 2: Named Entity Recognition (NER)

Extracting structured information from unstructured construction text is a major pain point. BERT-based NER can identify and classify entities such as:

  • Materials: "Grade 60 rebar", "C40/50 concrete", "S355 steel"
  • Structural members: "Column C-14", "Beam B-2A", "Pile P-07"
  • Dimensions: "12.5 metres", "300 mm diameter", "25 kN/m"
  • Code references: "ACI 318-19", "Eurocode 2", "IS 456:2000"
  • Locations: "Level 3", "Grid Line F", "North abutment"

Each token in the input receives a label (e.g., B-Material, I-Material, O for outside). The BIO tagging scheme is used: B = beginning of entity, I = inside entity, O = outside any entity.

Application 3: Sentiment / Safety Analysis

Safety reports, incident logs, and toolbox talk records contain valuable signals about site safety culture. A BERT model fine-tuned for sentiment analysis can classify the tone and urgency of safety reports:

  • Critical: "Worker fell from scaffolding without harness"
  • Warning: "Multiple workers observed without hard hats in zone B"
  • Positive: "Excellent compliance with fall protection protocols this week"
  • Neutral: "Weekly safety inspection completed on schedule"

By processing hundreds of daily reports, the model can track safety sentiment trends over time, flagging periods of declining safety culture before incidents occur.

BERT Variants

After BERT's release, researchers quickly developed improved variants that addressed its limitations. Understanding these variants helps you choose the right model for your application.

RoBERTa (Robustly Optimised BERT)

Facebook AI's RoBERTa (2019) showed that BERT was significantly undertrained. By making four key changes, RoBERTa outperformed BERT-Large on every benchmark:

  • Remove NSP: The next sentence prediction task was found to hurt performance
  • Dynamic masking: Generate a new random mask for each training example each epoch, instead of using a fixed mask
  • More data: Train on 160GB of text (10x BERT's data)
  • Longer training: Train for more steps with larger batches

The lesson: the Transformer architecture was not the bottleneck — BERT's original training recipe was simply not aggressive enough.

ALBERT (A Lite BERT)

Google's ALBERT (2019) tackled the parameter efficiency problem. BERT-Large has 340M parameters, making it expensive to deploy. ALBERT introduces two innovations:

  • Factorised embedding parameters: Instead of projecting directly from vocabulary size (30K) to hidden size (768/1024), ALBERT uses a smaller embedding dimension (128) and then projects up. This reduces embedding parameters from 30K × 1024 to 30K × 128 + 128 × 1024
  • Cross-layer parameter sharing: All encoder layers share the same parameters. The model has the depth of 12 (or 24) layers but the parameter count of a single layer

ALBERT-xxlarge achieves better results than BERT-Large with 70% fewer parameters. It also replaces NSP with a harder Sentence Order Prediction (SOP) task: predict whether two consecutive sentences are in the correct order or swapped.

DistilBERT (Distilled BERT)

Hugging Face's DistilBERT (2019) uses knowledge distillation to create a smaller, faster model that retains most of BERT's performance:

  • 6 layers instead of 12 (50% of the architecture)
  • 66M parameters (60% of BERT-Base's 110M)
  • 60% faster inference
  • Retains 97% of BERT's performance on GLUE benchmarks

Knowledge distillation works by training the smaller "student" model to mimic the probability distributions produced by the larger "teacher" model (BERT), rather than learning from hard labels alone. The teacher's soft probability distributions contain richer information (dark knowledge) than simple one-hot labels.

Limitations of BERT

Despite its transformative impact, BERT has important limitations that you should understand as a practitioner.

Key Limitations

  • 512-token limit: BERT cannot process sequences longer than 512 tokens. A typical construction specification section can easily exceed this. Longer documents must be chunked and processed in pieces, losing cross-chunk context.
  • No text generation: BERT is an encoder — it produces representations, not text. It cannot write specifications, generate reports, or answer questions in natural language. For generation, you need decoder models like GPT (Sub-chapter 5.5).
  • Pre-training/fine-tuning mismatch: The [MASK] token appears during pre-training but never during fine-tuning or inference. The 80/10/10 strategy mitigates this but does not eliminate it.
  • Independence assumption in MLM: BERT predicts each masked token independently. If two masked tokens are "New" and "York," BERT might predict "New Delhi" and "New York" for the two positions independently, because it does not model the joint probability.
  • Domain gap: BERT was pre-trained on Wikipedia and books — not on construction documents, engineering standards, or technical specifications. Its vocabulary and learned representations may not capture domain-specific nuances without additional pre-training or fine-tuning on in-domain data.
Domain-Specific Models

To address the domain gap, researchers have pre-trained BERT variants on domain-specific corpora: SciBERT (scientific text), BioBERT (biomedical), LegalBERT (legal documents), and ConstructBERT-style models for the construction industry. These models learn domain-specific vocabulary and relationships that general BERT misses.

Knowledge Check

Test your understanding of BERT and encoder models with these four questions.

Question 1 of 4

What percentage of input tokens does BERT randomly select for masking during the Masked Language Modelling (MLM) pre-training task?

Correct: B. BERT randomly selects 15% of the input tokens. Of those, 80% are replaced with [MASK], 10% with a random token, and 10% are left unchanged. This 80/10/10 strategy helps reduce the mismatch between pre-training and fine-tuning.

Question 2 of 4

Which BERT variant demonstrated that removing the Next Sentence Prediction (NSP) task and training longer with more data leads to better performance?

Correct: C. RoBERTa (Robustly Optimised BERT Pretraining Approach) by Facebook AI showed that BERT was undertrained. By removing NSP, using dynamic masking, training on 10x more data, and training longer, RoBERTa outperformed BERT-Large on every benchmark.

Question 3 of 4

In a BERT-based document classification system for construction, which token's final hidden state is typically used as the input to the classification head?

Correct: A. The [CLS] token is prepended to every input and its final hidden state serves as the aggregate sequence representation. Because self-attention allows [CLS] to attend to every other token, it captures information from the entire input, making it suitable for sequence-level classification tasks.

Question 4 of 4

DistilBERT retains approximately what percentage of BERT-Base's performance while being 60% faster?

Correct: C. DistilBERT retains 97% of BERT-Base's performance on GLUE benchmarks while using only 6 layers (vs. 12), 66M parameters (vs. 110M), and achieving 60% faster inference. This makes it practical for deployment on resource-constrained systems common in construction site environments.

Summary

Key Takeaways

  • BERT is an encoder-only Transformer that reads text bidirectionally, producing deep contextualised representations for every token.
  • Pre-training uses two self-supervised tasks: Masked Language Modelling (MLM) and Next Sentence Prediction (NSP), training on billions of words of unlabelled text.
  • Fine-tuning adds a thin task-specific layer on top and trains the entire model on labelled data, achieving strong results with small datasets and minimal computation.
  • The [CLS] token provides an aggregate sequence representation for classification, while individual token representations are used for token-level tasks like NER.
  • WordPiece tokenization breaks rare words into subword pieces, allowing a fixed vocabulary to represent any text.
  • Variants improve on BERT: RoBERTa (better training), ALBERT (fewer parameters), DistilBERT (faster inference via distillation).
  • Civil engineering applications include document classification, named entity recognition in construction documents, and safety report analysis.
