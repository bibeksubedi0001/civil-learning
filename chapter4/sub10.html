CE Case Study: Crack Detection

An end-to-end deep learning project — from raw concrete images captured by inspection drones to a deployed CNN model that automatically detects cracks in infrastructure, validated with Grad-CAM and evaluated for safety-critical reliability.

55 min read 2 interactive demos 4 quiz questions

The Problem: Manual Inspection Does Not Scale

Every year, tens of thousands of bridges, retaining walls, tunnel linings, and building facades must be inspected for surface defects. The most common defect — and often the first visible sign of structural distress — is cracking. Hairline cracks in concrete may indicate shrinkage, thermal cycling, or the early stages of reinforcement corrosion. Wider cracks may signal overloading, settlement, or seismic damage.

Traditional inspection relies on trained engineers physically examining every surface, photographing defects, and manually logging their location, width, and severity. This process is slow, expensive, subjective, and dangerous when it involves rope access, scaffolding, or under-bridge inspection vehicles.

The goal of this case study is to build an automated crack detection system powered by a convolutional neural network. Given a photograph of a concrete surface, the model will classify it as either Crack or No Crack with high confidence. This is a binary image classification task — the simplest and most well-understood form of computer vision, yet one with enormous practical impact.

Why Automate Crack Detection?

  • Scale — A single drone survey of a highway bridge can produce thousands of high-resolution images. No inspector can manually review every patch of every image at pixel-level detail. A CNN can process hundreds of images per second.
  • Consistency — Human inspectors disagree on crack classification approximately 15–30% of the time, depending on crack width and lighting conditions. A trained CNN applies the same criteria to every image, eliminating inter-observer variability.
  • Speed — What takes a team of engineers days can be processed by a CNN in minutes, enabling rapid post-disaster assessment when time is critical.
  • Safety — Reducing the need for inspectors to access dangerous locations (bridge undersides, dam faces, high-rise facades) by processing drone-captured imagery remotely.
  • Record keeping — Automated systems generate structured, time-stamped defect databases that enable longitudinal tracking of crack propagation across inspection cycles.
Civil Engineering Analogy

Automated crack detection is to structural inspection what GPS total stations are to surveying. Both technologies replace a slow, manual, skill-dependent process with a faster, more consistent, and more scalable digital workflow — not eliminating the engineer, but freeing them from tedious repetitive tasks so they can focus on interpretation and decision-making.

The Dataset: Concrete Crack Images

The foundation of any supervised learning project is a high-quality labelled dataset. For crack detection, two publicly available benchmarks are widely used in the civil engineering AI literature:

SDNET2018 — Structural Defects Network Dataset

Created by Utah State University, SDNET2018 contains over 56,000 images (256 × 256 pixels) of cracked and uncracked concrete surfaces from bridges, pavements, and walls. Each image is labelled as either Crack or No Crack. The dataset is challenging because it includes images with stains, rough textures, joints, and surface variations that can confuse a model into false positive detections.

Concrete Crack Images for Classification (CCIC)

This dataset comprises 40,000 images (227 × 227 pixels) equally split into 20,000 cracked and 20,000 uncracked samples. The images were collected from various concrete structures and manually verified. Its balanced class distribution makes it an excellent starting point for binary classification.

For this case study, we will use the Concrete Crack Images for Classification (CCIC) dataset due to its clean labelling, perfect class balance, and widespread adoption in benchmarking studies. The dataset requires no additional cleaning — a luxury rarely found in real-world projects.

40,000 Total Images
20,000 Crack Class
20,000 No-Crack Class

Sample Images: What the Model Sees

The Crack class contains images with visible surface cracks of varying widths — from hairline cracks barely visible to the naked eye to wide structural cracks several millimetres across. Cracks appear as dark, roughly linear features against the lighter concrete background.

The No Crack class includes images of intact concrete surfaces. Critically, many of these contain challenging features that could be confused with cracks: expansion joints, surface scratches, staining patterns, form lines from construction, aggregate shadows, and water marks. These “hard negatives” force the model to learn the true visual signature of structural cracking rather than simply detecting any dark linear feature.

Civil Engineering Analogy

A balanced dataset is like a well-designed experiment. In geotechnical testing, you would not run 100 tests on clay and only 2 on sand and then claim your model applies to all soils. Similarly, a crack detector trained on 95% uncracked images and 5% cracked images would learn to always predict “no crack” and still achieve 95% accuracy — a useless model. Balanced datasets ensure the model learns both classes equally.

Class Balance Matters

The CCIC dataset is perfectly balanced (50/50). In real-world deployments, however, the vast majority of concrete surface patches will not contain cracks — typical ratios might be 98% no-crack to 2% crack. This imbalance requires careful handling during training:

  • Weighted loss function — assign higher penalty to misclassifying the minority (crack) class.
  • Oversampling — duplicate or augment crack images to balance the training batches.
  • Undersampling — reduce the no-crack images to match the crack count (risks losing useful variation).
  • Focal loss — a modified loss function that down-weights easy examples and focuses training on hard, misclassified samples.

Data Preparation

Raw images rarely go directly into a neural network. A series of preprocessing steps transforms the data into a form suitable for training. Each decision affects model performance, training speed, and generalisation.

Step 1: Resizing

The original images are 227 × 227 pixels. For transfer learning with ResNet-18, the standard input size is 224 × 224. We resize all images to this dimension. The 3-pixel difference is negligible and avoids any architecture mismatch.

Step 2: Data Augmentation

Data augmentation artificially expands the training set by applying random transformations to each image during training. This forces the model to learn features that are invariant to orientation, lighting, and scale — all of which vary enormously in real inspection imagery.

Augmentation Pipeline

  • Random horizontal flip — cracks have no preferred left-right orientation. Probability: 50%.
  • Random vertical flip — drone images can be captured from any angle. Probability: 50%.
  • Random rotation (0–30 degrees) — cracks can run at any angle across the surface.
  • Colour jitter — randomly adjust brightness (±20%), contrast (±20%), and saturation (±10%) to simulate varying lighting conditions.
  • Random resized crop — crop a random 80–100% region and resize back to 224 × 224, simulating scale variation.
# PyTorch augmentation pipeline from torchvision import transforms train_transform = transforms.Compose([ transforms.Resize((256, 256)), transforms.RandomResizedCrop(224, scale=(0.8, 1.0)), transforms.RandomHorizontalFlip(p=0.5), transforms.RandomVerticalFlip(p=0.5), transforms.RandomRotation(30), transforms.ColorJitter( brightness=0.2, contrast=0.2, saturation=0.1 ), transforms.ToTensor(), transforms.Normalize( mean=[0.485, 0.456, 0.406], std=[0.229, 0.224, 0.225] ), ]) val_transform = transforms.Compose([ transforms.Resize((224, 224)), transforms.ToTensor(), transforms.Normalize( mean=[0.485, 0.456, 0.406], std=[0.229, 0.224, 0.225] ), ])

Notice that the validation transform does not include random augmentations. We only resize and normalise, because we want evaluation to be deterministic and reproducible.

Step 3: Train / Validation / Test Split

We partition the 40,000 images into three disjoint subsets:

Subset Size Percentage Purpose
Training 28,000 70% Learn weights via backpropagation
Validation 6,000 15% Tune hyperparameters, monitor overfitting
Test 6,000 15% Final, unbiased performance estimate

The split is stratified — each subset maintains the 50/50 class ratio. The test set is never used during training or hyperparameter tuning. It is opened only once, at the very end, to report final performance. This discipline prevents optimistic bias.

Data Leakage Warning

If the same concrete surface appears in both training and test sets (e.g., overlapping drone image crops from the same wall), the model may memorise specific textures rather than learning general crack features. Always split by structure or inspection session, not by individual image, when working with real-world data.

Architecture: Transfer Learning vs Custom CNN

The most critical design decision is whether to train a CNN from scratch or leverage transfer learning — using a model pre-trained on millions of images from ImageNet and fine-tuning it on our crack dataset. Both approaches have trade-offs.

ResNet-18 (Transfer Learning)

  • Pre-trained on 1.2M ImageNet images
  • Already knows edges, textures, shapes
  • Only need to retrain the final FC layer
  • Fast convergence (5–15 epochs)
  • 11.7M parameters total, 512 retrained
  • Strong performance even with small datasets
Recommended for this project

Custom Lightweight CNN

  • Designed from scratch for this task
  • Smaller model (fewer parameters)
  • Needs more data and longer training
  • Full control over architecture
  • May underperform without sufficient data
  • Better for highly specialised domains
Viable with larger datasets

We choose ResNet-18 with transfer learning. The reasoning is straightforward: ImageNet pre-training teaches the early convolutional layers to detect edges, gradients, and textures — exactly the low-level features that distinguish cracks from intact concrete. We freeze these early layers and only retrain the final fully connected layer to output two classes (crack / no crack).

ResNet-18 Architecture Summary

ResNet-18 consists of an initial 7×7 convolution, followed by 4 residual blocks (each containing two 3×3 convolutions with skip connections), a global average pooling layer, and a final fully connected layer. The skip connections solve the vanishing gradient problem, enabling deeper networks to train effectively.

For our binary classification task, we replace the original 1000-class output layer with a single fully connected layer mapping 512 features to 2 output neurons (or 1 neuron with sigmoid activation).

# Fine-tuning ResNet-18 for crack detection import torchvision.models as models import torch.nn as nn # Load pre-trained ResNet-18 model = models.resnet18(weights=models.ResNet18_Weights.IMAGENET1K_V1) # Freeze all layers for param in model.parameters(): param.requires_grad = False # Replace the final FC layer num_features = model.fc.in_features # 512 model.fc = nn.Sequential( nn.Dropout(p=0.3), nn.Linear(num_features, 2) ) # Optionally unfreeze layer4 for fine-tuning for param in model.layer4.parameters(): param.requires_grad = True

By unfreezing layer4 (the deepest residual block), we allow the model to adapt its high-level feature representations to crack-specific patterns while keeping the lower layers (edge detectors, texture detectors) frozen at their ImageNet-learned weights.

Training the Model

Training a CNN involves feeding batches of images through the network, computing the loss (how wrong the predictions are), and updating the weights via backpropagation. The hyperparameters governing this process significantly affect final performance.

Hyperparameter Configuration

Hyperparameter Value Rationale
Optimiser Adam Adaptive learning rate, fast convergence
Initial learning rate 1e-4 Small LR for fine-tuning (avoid destroying pre-trained weights)
Batch size 32 Good balance of speed and gradient stability
Loss function CrossEntropyLoss Standard for multi-class (2-class) classification
Epochs 25 (max) With early stopping, typically converges in 8–12
Weight decay 1e-4 L2 regularisation to prevent overfitting
Dropout 0.3 Applied before the final FC layer

Learning Rate Scheduling

A constant learning rate rarely produces optimal results. We use ReduceLROnPlateau, which monitors the validation loss and reduces the learning rate by a factor of 0.1 when improvement stalls for 3 consecutive epochs. This allows the model to make large initial progress and then fine-tune with smaller updates as it approaches the optimum.

# Learning rate scheduler from torch.optim.lr_scheduler import ReduceLROnPlateau optimizer = torch.optim.Adam( model.parameters(), lr=1e-4, weight_decay=1e-4 ) scheduler = ReduceLROnPlateau( optimizer, mode='min', factor=0.1, patience=3, verbose=True ) # After each epoch: scheduler.step(val_loss)

Early Stopping

Early stopping prevents overfitting by monitoring the validation loss and halting training when it stops improving. We use a patience of 5 epochs — if validation loss does not decrease for 5 consecutive epochs, training stops and the model weights from the best epoch are restored.

Why Early Stopping Matters for Safety-Critical Systems

An overfit crack detector memorises specific crack patterns from the training set rather than learning generalised features. It may achieve 99.5% accuracy on training data but drop to 85% on new images from a different bridge. In safety-critical applications, this gap between training and real-world performance can lead to missed cracks — a potentially catastrophic failure mode. Early stopping is your primary defence against this.

Training Loop Summary

  • For each epoch, iterate over all training batches. For each batch: forward pass, compute loss, backward pass, update weights.
  • After each epoch, evaluate on the validation set. Record validation loss and accuracy. No gradient computation during validation (use torch.no_grad()).
  • Update the learning rate scheduler with the validation loss. If the loss has plateaued, the learning rate is reduced.
  • Check early stopping. If validation loss has improved, save the model checkpoint. If not improved for 5 epochs, stop training.
  • After training, load the best checkpoint and evaluate on the held-out test set exactly once.

Evaluation: Measuring What Matters

Accuracy alone is insufficient for evaluating a crack detection system. In safety-critical applications, we care deeply about which errors the model makes. Missing a crack (false negative) is far more dangerous than flagging intact concrete as cracked (false positive). This asymmetry demands a nuanced evaluation framework.

The Confusion Matrix

A confusion matrix breaks down the model's predictions into four categories:

Predicted: Crack Predicted: No Crack
Actual: Crack True Positive (TP) False Negative (FN) — DANGEROUS
Actual: No Crack False Positive (FP) — costly True Negative (TN)

In structural inspection, a false negative (missed crack) could lead to an undetected defect progressing to structural failure. A false positive (false alarm) wastes inspection time but poses no safety risk. Therefore, we prioritise recall (sensitivity) for the crack class.

Key Metrics

Precision (Crack Class)
Precision = TP / (TP + FP)

Of all images predicted as “crack,” what fraction truly contain cracks?

Recall (Crack Class) — CRITICAL
Recall = TP / (TP + FN)

Of all images that truly contain cracks, what fraction does the model detect? A recall of 0.99 means only 1 in 100 cracks is missed.

F1 Score
F1 = 2 × (Precision × Recall) / (Precision + Recall)

Harmonic mean of precision and recall. Balanced single-number summary.

ROC Curve and AUC

The Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve plots the True Positive Rate (recall) against the False Positive Rate at various classification thresholds. The Area Under the Curve (AUC) summarises performance across all thresholds in a single number between 0 and 1.

  • AUC = 1.0 — perfect classifier
  • AUC = 0.5 — random guessing (the diagonal line)
  • AUC > 0.95 — typical target for crack detection

Typical Results for ResNet-18 on CCIC

Published studies using ResNet-18 with transfer learning on the Concrete Crack Images dataset report the following approximate performance on held-out test sets:

  • Accuracy: 98.5–99.5%
  • Precision (crack): 98.0–99.2%
  • Recall (crack): 98.8–99.6%
  • F1 Score: 98.5–99.4%
  • AUC: 0.998–0.999

These numbers are achievable because the CCIC dataset is clean and well-separated. Real-world performance on novel structures will be lower, making robust evaluation even more important.

Threshold Tuning for Safety

The default classification threshold is 0.5: if the model outputs a crack probability above 0.5, it predicts “crack.” But for safety-critical applications, we can lower this threshold — say to 0.3 — to increase recall at the cost of more false positives. This is a deliberate engineering trade-off: better to inspect 10 extra patches of intact concrete than to miss one real crack.

Deployment: From Lab to Field

A model that achieves 99% accuracy on a test set is worthless if it cannot run in the field. Deployment is where engineering meets machine learning — the model must work within the constraints of real hardware, real power budgets, and real-time requirements.

Edge Deployment on Inspection Drones

The target deployment platform for this crack detector is an edge computing device mounted on an inspection drone. The device processes images from the drone's camera in real-time during flight, flagging cracked regions for the pilot to investigate or automatically geotagging defect locations for later review.

Drone-Mounted Processing

NVIDIA Jetson Nano or Xavier NX mounted on the drone payload. Processes frames at 10–30 FPS depending on model size. Results available during flight, enabling adaptive survey paths.

Edge Computing Box

Ruggedised edge device at the inspection site. Receives images via Wi-Fi from the drone. Runs inference on batches of images as they are captured. Results displayed on a field tablet.

Cloud Processing

Images uploaded post-flight to a cloud GPU server for batch inference. Highest throughput but requires internet connectivity and introduces latency. Best for non-time-critical inspections.

Mobile App

Lightweight model (MobileNetV2 or quantised ResNet) running directly on an engineer's smartphone. Point the camera at a surface and get instant crack classification. Useful for walkthrough inspections.

Model Optimisation for Edge

To achieve acceptable inference speed on edge devices, several optimisation techniques are applied:

  • ONNX Export — Convert the PyTorch model to ONNX (Open Neural Network Exchange) format for cross-platform compatibility and runtime optimisation.
  • TensorRT Optimisation — NVIDIA's TensorRT compiler fuses layers, optimises memory access, and applies kernel auto-tuning for the specific GPU architecture.
  • Quantisation — Reduce weight precision from FP32 (32-bit floating point) to FP16 or INT8. This halves or quarters the model size and speeds up computation with minimal accuracy loss (typically less than 0.5% for crack detection).
  • Pruning — Remove neurons and connections with near-zero weights. A pruned ResNet-18 can be 40–60% smaller with less than 1% accuracy loss.
# Export PyTorch model to ONNX import torch dummy_input = torch.randn(1, 3, 224, 224) torch.onnx.export( model, dummy_input, "crack_detector.onnx", input_names=["image"], output_names=["prediction"], dynamic_axes={"image": {0: "batch"}}, opset_version=13 )

Results Interpretation: Grad-CAM Validation

High accuracy numbers are necessary but not sufficient. We must verify that the model is making predictions for the right reasons. Grad-CAM (Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping) produces a heatmap showing which regions of the input image most influenced the model's prediction.

What Good Grad-CAM Results Look Like

For a correctly functioning crack detector, the Grad-CAM heatmap should show warm colours (red/orange) concentrated along the crack itself and cool colours (blue/transparent) on the surrounding intact concrete. This confirms that the model has learned to focus on the crack — not on irrelevant background features.

Red Flags in Grad-CAM Analysis

If Grad-CAM reveals any of the following patterns, the model has learned a spurious correlation rather than true crack features:

  • Heatmap on image borders — the model may have learned that cracked images tend to have different border artifacts (e.g., from inconsistent cropping).
  • Heatmap on shadows or stains — the model confuses dark shadow regions with cracks, a common failure when training data has correlated lighting conditions.
  • Uniform heatmap — the model is not localising features at all; it may be relying on global statistics like average brightness.
  • Heatmap on metadata text — some inspection images contain embedded timestamps or labels. The model may learn to read these instead of analysing the concrete surface.
Civil Engineering Analogy

Grad-CAM validation is like checking a structural model's deformed shape. A finite element analysis might report stress values that look reasonable, but if the deformed shape shows the building bending the wrong way, you know the boundary conditions are wrong. Similarly, a CNN might report 99% accuracy, but if Grad-CAM shows it looking at the wrong part of the image, you know the model has learned a shortcut rather than real structural features.

# Grad-CAM implementation (simplified) def grad_cam(model, image, target_layer): # Register hooks to capture gradients and activations activations = [] gradients = [] def forward_hook(module, inp, out): activations.append(out) def backward_hook(module, grad_in, grad_out): gradients.append(grad_out[0]) handle_f = target_layer.register_forward_hook(forward_hook) handle_b = target_layer.register_full_backward_hook(backward_hook) # Forward pass output = model(image) pred_class = output.argmax(dim=1) # Backward pass for predicted class model.zero_grad() output[0, pred_class].backward() # Compute weights (global average pooling of gradients) weights = gradients[0].mean(dim=[2, 3], keepdim=True) # Weighted combination of activations cam = (weights * activations[0]).sum(dim=1, keepdim=True) cam = torch.nn.functional.relu(cam) # Keep only positive # Normalise to [0, 1] cam = cam / cam.max() handle_f.remove() handle_b.remove() return cam

Chapter 4 Recap: Computer Vision for Civil Engineers

This case study brings together every concept from Chapter 4 into a single, cohesive project. Let us trace the connections:

How It All Connects

  • 4.1 Images as Data — Each concrete patch is a 224 × 224 × 3 tensor of normalised pixel values.
  • 4.2 Convolution Operation — The convolutional layers in ResNet-18 slide learned filters across the image to detect edges, textures, and patterns.
  • 4.3 Pooling & Stride — Max pooling in ResNet progressively reduces spatial dimensions while preserving dominant features like crack edges.
  • 4.4 CNN Architecture — ResNet-18's four residual blocks form the backbone, with skip connections enabling gradient flow through 18 layers.
  • 4.5 Transfer Learning — ImageNet pre-training gives us powerful feature extractors. We only retrain the classifier head for crack vs no-crack.
  • 4.6 Data Augmentation — Flips, rotations, and colour jitter expand our effective dataset and improve generalisation to unseen structures.
  • 4.7 Object Detection — Beyond classification, the next step would be localising cracks with bounding boxes using architectures like YOLO or Faster R-CNN.
  • 4.8 Semantic Segmentation — For precise crack delineation, pixel-wise segmentation with U-Net can trace crack paths and measure their width.
  • 4.9 Visualizing CNN Internals — Grad-CAM validates that our model focuses on the crack itself, not on spurious features.

Key Lessons from This Case Study

  • Transfer learning is powerful — a pre-trained ResNet-18, fine-tuned with 28,000 crack images, achieves over 99% accuracy in as few as 10 epochs. Building this from scratch would require far more data and compute.
  • Evaluation must match the application — for safety-critical crack detection, recall is more important than precision. A missed crack is more dangerous than a false alarm.
  • Interpretability builds trust — Grad-CAM heatmaps allow engineers to verify that the model's reasoning aligns with physical reality. This is essential for gaining acceptance in a profession built on rigorous verification.
  • Deployment is engineering, not just data science — converting a PyTorch model to ONNX, quantising weights, and optimising for edge hardware requires systems-level thinking that civil engineers are well-equipped to learn.
  • The pipeline is the product — no single component (data, model, evaluation, deployment) works in isolation. The value lies in the complete, validated, deployed system.

Key Takeaways

What You Should Remember

  1. Automated crack detection replaces slow, subjective manual inspection with a fast, consistent, scalable CNN-based system.
  2. A balanced, well-labelled dataset (like CCIC with 40,000 images) is the foundation. Data quality matters more than model complexity.
  3. Transfer learning with ResNet-18 achieves over 99% accuracy with minimal training time by leveraging ImageNet-learned features.
  4. Data augmentation (flips, rotations, colour jitter) is essential for generalisation to unseen structures and lighting conditions.
  5. In safety-critical systems, recall is king — missing a crack is far worse than a false alarm. Tune your threshold accordingly.
  6. Grad-CAM validation confirms the model focuses on actual cracks, not spurious features. Never deploy without interpretability checks.
  7. Edge deployment requires model optimisation (ONNX, TensorRT, quantisation) to achieve real-time inference on drone-mounted hardware.
  8. The complete pipeline — data, preparation, architecture, training, evaluation, interpretation, deployment — is the deliverable, not just the model.
