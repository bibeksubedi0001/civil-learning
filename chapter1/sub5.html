Chapter 1 — Sub-chapter 1.5

Types of Artificial Intelligence

Not all AI is created equal. From narrow tools that detect cracks to theoretical superintelligences that could redesign entire cities — understand the capability spectrum and memory classifications that define every AI system you will encounter.

28 min read 2 interactive demos 4 quiz questions

Why Classify AI at All?

When a structural engineer evaluates a building, they do not simply say "it is a structure." They classify it — steel frame, reinforced concrete, prestressed, timber — because the classification determines the analysis methods, design codes, and construction techniques that apply.

AI classification works the same way. Knowing what type of AI you are dealing with tells you what it can and cannot do, how much you should trust its outputs, and where it fits in your engineering workflow. Misclassifying an AI system is like applying a timber design code to a steel structure — dangerous and fundamentally wrong.

There are two primary frameworks for classifying AI systems. The first is capability-based classification, which asks: "How broadly can this system think?" The second is memory-based classification, which asks: "What can this system remember and reason about?" Together, these frameworks give you a complete picture of any AI system you encounter in civil engineering practice.

Key Insight

Every AI tool you currently use in civil engineering — crack detection software, BIM optimization, traffic prediction, cost estimation — falls into a specific category in both classification systems. Understanding these categories helps you set realistic expectations and avoid the dangerous trap of over-relying on a narrow tool for tasks it was never designed to handle.

Classification by Capability: ANI, AGI, ASI

The capability-based classification divides AI into three tiers based on how broadly intelligent the system is. Think of it as a pyramid: the base is wide and common (ANI), the middle is aspirational (AGI), and the peak is theoretical (ASI). Every AI system that exists today sits firmly at the base.

Currently Exists

Artificial Narrow Intelligence (ANI)

Also called Weak AI, ANI systems are designed and trained for a single specific task. They can match or exceed human performance in that task, but they cannot transfer their knowledge to other domains.

  • Crack detection in concrete surfaces
  • Traffic flow prediction models
  • Structural load optimization
  • Cost estimation from BIM models
  • GPT-based report generation
Does Not Yet Exist

Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)

AGI would match the full breadth of human cognitive ability — reasoning, learning new tasks without retraining, understanding context, and transferring knowledge across domains effortlessly.

  • Could design, estimate, and manage a project end-to-end
  • Would understand building codes and client intent
  • Could adapt to unforeseen site conditions
  • Would reason about safety like a human engineer
Theoretical / Speculative

Artificial Super Intelligence (ASI)

ASI would surpass the collective intelligence of all humans combined. It is purely theoretical — no credible timeline exists for its development, and it raises profound ethical and safety questions.

  • Could optimize entire city infrastructure systems simultaneously
  • Would discover novel materials and structural forms
  • Could solve climate-scale engineering challenges
  • Beyond current scientific understanding
Civil Engineering Analogy

Think of ANI as a concrete vibrator — a specialized tool that does one job exceptionally well. You would never ask a concrete vibrator to do rebar layout or formwork design. It vibrates concrete, period. Similarly, a crack-detection AI only detects cracks. Ask it to estimate project costs, and it will produce nonsense.

AGI would be like a skilled civil engineer — someone who can design structures, manage projects, read and interpret codes, negotiate with contractors, adapt to unexpected soil conditions, and explain decisions to clients. The engineer can transfer knowledge between domains: understanding loads from structural analysis helps them reason about geotechnical problems too.

ASI would be like an entire design firm thinking at light speed — every structural, geotechnical, environmental, transportation, and construction specialist working in perfect coordination, processing a century of engineering knowledge in seconds, and innovating beyond anything any individual engineer could conceive.

The ANI Trap: A Warning for Engineers

Modern AI systems like large language models (GPT, Claude) can seem like AGI because they handle many different tasks. But they are still ANI — incredibly sophisticated narrow systems trained on vast datasets. They do not truly understand engineering principles; they recognize patterns in text.

This distinction is critical. If you ask an LLM to check a structural calculation, it might produce something that looks correct but contains fundamental errors. It is a pattern matcher, not an engineer. Always verify AI outputs against established engineering principles and codes. The responsibility for structural safety remains with the licensed professional engineer, never with the AI.

1

Identify the Task Scope

Before adopting any AI tool, define exactly what task it needs to perform. "Detect cracks in concrete" is a narrow task (ANI). "Manage my construction project" requires general reasoning (AGI-level) and no current AI can truly do it. Matching the AI type to the task scope prevents disappointment and ensures safe, effective deployment.

2

Set Realistic Expectations

ANI excels when the problem is well-defined and the training data is representative. It fails when the real-world conditions deviate significantly from training data — for example, a crack detector trained on highway bridges may fail on historical masonry. Understanding ANI limitations prevents costly errors.

3

Plan for Human Oversight

Because all current AI is ANI, human oversight is not optional — it is essential. Design your workflows with AI as an assistant, not a decision-maker. The engineer reviews, validates, and takes responsibility. This is not a limitation; it is proper engineering practice with a powerful but specialized tool.

Classification by Memory & Learning

The second major classification framework looks at how AI systems use memory and past experience. This framework was proposed by Arend Hintze and divides AI into four types based on increasing sophistication of memory and self-awareness. For civil engineers, this classification helps you understand how an AI system processes information — and what its fundamental limitations are.

Type I: Reactive Machines

Currently Exists

The simplest form of AI. Reactive machines have no memory and cannot use past experiences to inform current decisions. They receive an input and produce an output — every single time as if it is the first time.

CE Example: A simple threshold-based sensor that triggers an alarm when vibration exceeds a fixed limit. It does not remember previous readings or learn from patterns. IBM's Deep Blue chess engine is the classic example — it evaluated positions but never "learned."

Type II: Limited Memory

Currently Exists

These systems can use historical data and past experiences stored during training to make decisions. Most modern AI falls here — deep learning models, self-driving cars, and all the CE tools you use daily.

CE Example: A crack-detection CNN trained on thousands of labelled images. It "remembers" crack patterns from training data. A traffic prediction model that uses historical flow data to forecast congestion. These systems learn from the past but do not update in real time (unless explicitly retrained).

Type III: Theory of Mind

Research Stage

Theory of Mind AI would understand that other entities have their own beliefs, desires, intentions, and emotions — and factor those into decisions. This is a frontier research area with no complete implementations.

Hypothetical CE Use: An AI project manager that understands when a subcontractor is overpromising on a schedule, reads the reluctance in a client's tone during a design review, and adjusts recommendations based on the team's emotional state during high-stress project phases.

Type IV: Self-Aware

Purely Theoretical

Self-aware AI would possess consciousness, self-awareness, and sentience. It would understand its own internal states and existence. This remains firmly in the realm of philosophy and science fiction.

Theoretical Concept: An AI that not only designs a bridge but understands why it finds certain structural forms elegant, feels satisfaction when a design passes all checks, and questions its own assumptions about load paths. No credible research pathway exists for this level.

Civil Engineering Analogy: The Site Team

Reactive Machine = A simple spirit level. It tells you if something is level right now, with no memory of previous readings and no ability to learn. Every measurement is independent.

Limited Memory = A total station with data logging. It records previous survey points and uses them to compute relative positions. It "remembers" past measurements and uses them for current calculations, but it follows fixed procedures.

Theory of Mind = An experienced site foreman who reads the crew's body language, anticipates the client's unspoken concerns, and adjusts communication style for different stakeholders. The foreman models other people's mental states.

Self-Aware = A conscious being that reflects on its own existence, questions its motivations, and has subjective experiences. No engineering tool or AI system has this capability.

Practical Takeaway

Every AI tool you will use in civil engineering for the foreseeable future is Type II: Limited Memory. This means it learned patterns from training data and applies those patterns to new inputs. The critical implication: if your real-world data differs significantly from the training data, the AI will fail — sometimes silently. Always validate AI outputs against domain knowledge and engineering judgement.

Where Current CE AI Tools Fall on the Spectrum

Let us map the AI tools commonly used in civil engineering onto both classification frameworks. This practical mapping helps you understand the capabilities and limitations of tools you may already be using.

Crack Detection & Structural Inspection

Capability: ANI — trained to detect specific defect types in specific materials. A crack detector for concrete will not work on steel weld inspection without complete retraining.

Memory: Type II (Limited Memory) — uses patterns learned from thousands of labelled training images. Does not learn from new inspections unless explicitly retrained.

Traffic Flow Prediction

Capability: ANI — predicts traffic volumes and speeds for specific road networks. Cannot generalize to a different city without retraining on local data.

Memory: Type II (Limited Memory) — learns temporal patterns from historical sensor data (peak hours, seasonal variation, event impacts). Some advanced models incorporate real-time data feeds for short-term predictions.

BIM-Based Design Optimization

Capability: ANI — optimizes specific parameters (member sizes, layouts, energy performance) within predefined constraints. Cannot "creatively" rethink the entire design approach.

Memory: Type II (Limited Memory) — uses training data from previous design iterations and optimization runs to guide search. Generative design tools explore the solution space but do not "understand" architecture.

Cost Estimation & Schedule Prediction

Capability: ANI — predicts cost and duration based on historical project data. Accuracy depends heavily on the similarity between new and historical projects.

Memory: Type II (Limited Memory) — trained on databases of completed projects with known costs and durations. Some systems incorporate real-time market data for material price adjustments.

Large Language Models (ChatGPT, Claude, etc.)

Capability: ANI — despite appearing general-purpose, LLMs are sophisticated pattern matchers trained on text. They can draft reports, summarize specs, and answer questions, but they do not truly understand engineering principles.

Memory: Type II (Limited Memory) — the model's "memory" is its training data. Context window serves as short-term memory within a conversation, but the model does not learn from your interactions (unless fine-tuned).

Key Takeaways

What You Should Remember

  1. AI is classified by capability (ANI, AGI, ASI) and by memory (Reactive, Limited Memory, Theory of Mind, Self-Aware). These are independent dimensions.
  2. All current AI is ANI + Type II (Limited Memory). Every AI tool you use in civil engineering today — without exception — falls into these categories.
  3. ANI is a specialized tool, not a thinking partner. Like a concrete vibrator, it does one job well but cannot generalize. Match the tool to the task scope.
  4. LLMs appear general but are still ANI. They are sophisticated pattern matchers. Never trust LLM output for safety-critical calculations without independent verification by a qualified engineer.
  5. AGI and ASI do not exist. No credible timeline exists for AGI, and ASI is purely theoretical. Do not let marketing hype distort your expectations of current AI capabilities.
  6. Understanding AI types prevents misapplication. Knowing that your crack detector is ANI + Limited Memory tells you it will fail on unfamiliar materials, does not learn from new inspections automatically, and should never be the sole basis for safety-critical decisions.
  7. Human oversight is essential. Because all current AI is narrow and memory-limited, the professional engineer's judgement remains the final authority on all engineering decisions.
