Chapter 1 — Sub-chapter 1.10

Types of Machine Learning

Supervised, Unsupervised, Reinforcement, and Semi-supervised Learning — the four paradigms that power every AI application in civil engineering.

30 min read 2 interactive demos 4 quiz questions

Four Ways a Machine Can Learn

Think about how knowledge is passed down on a construction site. Sometimes an experienced engineer sits beside a junior and says, "This is the correct rebar spacing for this load case." Other times, a surveyor walks an unknown site and discovers natural drainage patterns nobody documented. A project manager learns through years of trial and error which scheduling strategies minimise delays. And occasionally, a team combines a handful of verified lab results with thousands of unverified field measurements to make practical decisions.

Each of these scenarios mirrors a fundamental type of machine learning. Understanding these four paradigms — Supervised, Unsupervised, Reinforcement, and Semi-supervised — is the key to choosing the right approach for any engineering problem.

Construction Site Analogy

Machine learning paradigms are like training methods for construction crews. A master mason teaching an apprentice step-by-step is supervised learning. A surveyor mapping terrain without a prior map is unsupervised learning. A crane operator who improves through simulated practice runs is reinforcement learning. And a quality inspector who verifies a few samples, then applies those standards to thousands of unchecked units, is semi-supervised learning.

Before we dive into each type, let us see them at a glance. The four paradigms differ primarily in what kind of data they use and how they receive feedback during learning.

Supervised Learning

Learn from labelled examples. The algorithm sees both the input and the correct output, then learns to map between them.

Unsupervised Learning

Find hidden structure in unlabelled data. No correct answers are provided; the algorithm discovers patterns on its own.

Reinforcement Learning

Learn by interacting with an environment. The agent receives rewards or penalties and optimises its strategy over time.

Semi-supervised Learning

Combine a small amount of labelled data with a large pool of unlabelled data to achieve better results than either alone.

Supervised Learning: The Master-Apprentice Model

Supervised learning is the most widely used and most intuitive form of machine learning. The concept is straightforward: you give the algorithm a set of examples where each input is paired with its correct output, and the algorithm learns the relationship between them. Once trained, it can predict outputs for new, unseen inputs.

In mathematical terms, supervised learning finds a function f(x) = y that maps inputs x to outputs y, minimising the error between predicted and actual values across all training examples.

Civil Engineering Analogy

Supervised learning is like an apprentice learning from a master engineer. The master shows the apprentice dozens of concrete mix designs (inputs) and their resulting 28-day compressive strengths (outputs). "This mix with a 0.45 water-cement ratio gave 35 MPa. This one with 0.38 gave 42 MPa." After studying enough examples, the apprentice can predict the strength of a new mix design — without having to wait 28 days for the test result.

How It Works

1

Collect Labelled Data

Gather input-output pairs. For concrete strength prediction: the inputs are mix proportions (cement, water, aggregate, admixture amounts), and the output is the measured compressive strength. You need hundreds or thousands of such pairs.

2

Train the Model

Feed the data to a learning algorithm (linear regression, decision tree, neural network, etc.). The algorithm adjusts its internal parameters to minimise prediction error across all training examples. This is the "learning" phase.

3

Validate and Test

Evaluate the model on data it has never seen. If it predicts concrete strengths within an acceptable margin of error on new mix designs, the model has successfully generalised.

4

Deploy and Predict

Use the trained model in production. An engineer inputs a proposed mix design and instantly gets a predicted strength — enabling rapid iteration without waiting for laboratory results.

Two Sub-types

Regression (Continuous Output)

When the output is a continuous number. Predicting concrete compressive strength (35.2 MPa), estimating construction cost ($4.7 million), or forecasting settlement of a foundation (12.3 mm) are all regression problems. The model outputs a specific numerical value.

Classification (Discrete Categories)

When the output is a category. Classifying a crack as "structural" vs "cosmetic", determining if a bridge is "safe" vs "needs inspection", or categorising soil as "clay", "sand", or "silt" are classification problems. The model assigns one of several predefined labels.

CE Applications of Supervised Learning
  • Concrete strength prediction: Given mix proportions, curing conditions, and aggregate properties, predict the 28-day compressive strength.
  • Load capacity estimation: Predict the ultimate load capacity of a beam given its dimensions, reinforcement, and material properties.
  • Crack classification: Analyse images of concrete surfaces and classify detected cracks by severity level.
  • Cost estimation: Predict project costs based on historical data from similar projects.

Unsupervised Learning: Mapping Unknown Terrain

Unsupervised learning is fundamentally different from supervised learning. Here, the algorithm receives only inputs — no correct answers, no labels, no teacher. Its job is to find hidden structure, patterns, or groupings in the data entirely on its own.

This may sound abstract, but it is remarkably practical. Think about the enormous volume of sensor data collected from a bridge over a decade. No human has labelled every reading as "normal" or "anomalous." Unsupervised learning can sift through millions of readings and discover patterns that humans might never notice.

Civil Engineering Analogy

Unsupervised learning is like a surveyor mapping unknown terrain. When a surveyor enters an unmapped region, nobody hands them a finished map. They observe the landscape, measure elevations, note where rivers flow and ridges rise, and gradually discover the natural structure of the terrain. They might find that the land naturally divides into three distinct drainage basins or five geological zones — categories that existed but were never defined by anyone.

Common Techniques

Clustering

Group similar data points together without predefined categories. K-means, DBSCAN, and hierarchical clustering are popular algorithms. In civil engineering, clustering can group soil samples by their geotechnical properties, revealing natural soil type boundaries that do not follow textbook categories.

Dimensionality Reduction

Reduce the number of variables while preserving essential information. Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and t-SNE are widely used. When a structural health monitoring system records 500 sensor channels, PCA can identify the 10 most informative combinations — making analysis tractable without losing critical signals.

Anomaly Detection

Identify data points that deviate significantly from the norm. The algorithm learns what "normal" looks like and flags anything unusual. For structural damage pattern recognition, the model learns the typical vibration signature of a healthy bridge and alerts engineers when the pattern shifts — potentially indicating damage.

CE Applications of Unsupervised Learning
  • Soil type clustering: Group borehole samples by geotechnical properties (plasticity, grain size, moisture content) to identify natural soil strata without predefined categories.
  • Structural damage pattern recognition: Detect unusual vibration patterns in bridges or buildings that indicate developing structural issues.
  • Traffic flow segmentation: Discover natural groupings in traffic patterns to optimise road design and signalling.
  • Construction material grouping: Cluster suppliers or material batches by quality characteristics for procurement optimisation.

Reinforcement Learning: Trial, Error, and Optimisation

Reinforcement learning (RL) stands apart from both supervised and unsupervised approaches. Instead of learning from a fixed dataset, an RL agent learns by interacting with an environment. It takes actions, observes the consequences, and receives rewards or penalties. Over time, it discovers which strategies maximise cumulative reward.

The key elements are: an agent (the learner), an environment (the world it operates in), states (the current situation), actions (what the agent can do), and rewards (feedback from the environment).

Civil Engineering Analogy

Reinforcement learning is like optimising traffic signal timing through simulation. Imagine a traffic engineer who can run thousands of simulated traffic scenarios. She tries different signal timing patterns at an intersection. When a pattern reduces average wait time, she gets a "reward" (good performance metric). When it causes gridlock, she gets a "penalty." After thousands of trials, she discovers an optimal timing strategy that no amount of manual analysis would have found — because the problem has too many interacting variables for a human to optimise simultaneously.

The RL Loop

1

Observe State

The agent observes the current state of the environment. For a construction robot, this might be its position, the positions of obstacles, and the location of materials it needs to move.

2

Choose Action

Based on the state, the agent selects an action from its repertoire. The robot might choose to move forward, turn left, pick up an item, or wait.

3

Receive Reward

The environment provides a numerical reward. Moving closer to the goal earns a small positive reward. Colliding with an obstacle earns a negative reward. Reaching the destination earns a large positive reward.

4

Update Strategy

The agent updates its decision-making policy based on the reward. Over many episodes, it converges on the strategy that maximises long-term cumulative reward.

CE Applications of Reinforcement Learning
  • Construction robot path planning: Autonomous vehicles and robots on construction sites learn to navigate complex, changing environments while avoiding workers and obstacles.
  • HVAC optimisation: RL agents control heating, ventilation, and air conditioning systems in buildings to minimise energy use while maintaining occupant comfort.
  • Traffic signal control: Adaptive signal timing that responds to real-time traffic conditions, reducing congestion and emissions.
  • Structural design optimisation: An agent explores the vast design space of structural configurations to find optimal solutions that balance weight, strength, and cost.

Semi-supervised Learning: Best of Both Worlds

In the real world of civil engineering, fully labelled datasets are a luxury. Getting a structural engineer to label 50,000 crack images as "structural" or "cosmetic" costs months of expert time. But collecting 50,000 unlabelled crack images from routine inspections is trivial. Semi-supervised learning bridges this gap.

The idea is elegantly practical: use a small number of expensive labelled examples to guide the algorithm, and let it leverage a large pool of cheap unlabelled data to improve its understanding. Typically, even 5–10% labelled data combined with the remaining 90–95% unlabelled data can achieve performance close to a fully supervised model.

Civil Engineering Analogy

Semi-supervised learning is like combining verified lab results with unverified field measurements. A geotechnical engineer has 20 carefully controlled laboratory tests on soil samples from a site (labelled data). She also has 2,000 quick field measurements taken with a dynamic cone penetrometer (unlabelled data — correlated with properties but not directly giving classification). By using the lab results to calibrate her interpretation of the field measurements, she creates a far more detailed and accurate soil map than either data source alone could provide.

Why It Matters

Cost Efficiency

Labelling data in civil engineering often requires expensive expert analysis or destructive testing. A single concrete core test can cost hundreds of dollars. Semi-supervised learning reduces the number of labelled samples needed by 80–90%, dramatically cutting data preparation costs while maintaining model accuracy.

Leveraging Abundant Unlabelled Data

Modern infrastructure generates massive quantities of unlabelled sensor data every day. Structural health monitoring systems, drone surveys, and IoT devices produce terabytes of data that would be impractical to manually label. Semi-supervised learning puts this data to productive use.

CE Applications of Semi-supervised Learning
  • Crack detection with limited labels: Label a few hundred images with expert annotations, then use thousands of unlabelled inspection photos to train a robust detector.
  • Material quality assessment: Combine a few destructive test results with many non-destructive measurements (rebound hammer, ultrasonic pulse) to predict material properties.
  • Land use classification: Label a small number of satellite image patches, then classify large areas using the unlabelled imagery.

Practical Tip

In practice, many civil engineering projects use a combination of approaches. You might start with unsupervised learning to explore and clean your data, then use the discovered structure to create labels for supervised learning. Or you might use semi-supervised learning when labelling is expensive, and reinforcement learning when you need to optimise an ongoing process. The boundaries between paradigms are guidelines, not walls.

Test Your Understanding

Test your grasp of the four machine learning paradigms. Each question reinforces a key concept that you will apply in later chapters.

Quick Check 1

A civil engineer has 10,000 concrete cylinder test results, each paired with the exact mix design that produced it. She wants to predict the 28-day strength of a new mix. Which type of ML is most appropriate?

Correct! With 10,000 fully labelled input-output pairs (mix designs mapped to strengths), this is a classic supervised learning regression problem. The model learns the relationship between mix parameters and resulting strength.
Not quite. Since the engineer has fully labelled data (each mix design paired with its test result), this is a supervised learning problem. The model learns to predict outputs (strength) from inputs (mix design).

Quick Check 2

A transportation engineer wants to automatically group 50,000 traffic sensor readings into distinct traffic patterns, but has no predefined categories. Which approach should she use?

Correct! With no predefined categories and the goal of discovering natural groupings, this is a textbook unsupervised learning (clustering) problem. Algorithms like K-means or DBSCAN will reveal the inherent traffic patterns.
Not quite. Since there are no predefined categories and the goal is to discover natural groupings, this is an unsupervised learning problem using clustering algorithms.

Quick Check 3

An HVAC system in a large commercial building adjusts its settings every 15 minutes based on occupancy, weather, and energy prices. It receives a "score" based on energy cost and comfort level. Which ML type is this?

Correct! The HVAC system is an RL agent: it takes actions (adjusting settings), observes states (occupancy, weather), and receives rewards (the comfort/cost score). Over time it learns the optimal control policy.
Not quite. This is reinforcement learning. The system interacts with its environment in real time, takes actions (settings), and receives a reward signal (the comfort/cost score). It improves its strategy through continuous interaction.

Quick Check 4

A research team has 200 expertly-labelled bridge inspection images and 15,000 unlabelled images from drone surveys. They want to build a damage detection model. Which approach maximises their data?

Correct! Semi-supervised learning is the ideal approach here. The 200 labelled images provide the ground truth the model needs, and the 15,000 unlabelled images provide the volume needed to learn robust features. This combination far outperforms using either dataset alone.
Not quite. Semi-supervised learning is the best fit because it leverages both the small set of expensive labelled images and the large pool of cheap unlabelled images, getting far better results than 200 supervised examples alone.

Key Takeaways

What You Should Remember

  • Supervised learning requires labelled data (input-output pairs) and is the most common type. Use it when you have enough labelled examples and need to predict a specific output (strength, cost, category).
  • Unsupervised learning works with unlabelled data and discovers hidden structure. Use it when you want to find natural groupings, detect anomalies, or reduce the complexity of high-dimensional data.
  • Reinforcement learning learns through interaction with an environment via rewards and penalties. Use it for sequential decision-making and optimisation problems where an agent must adapt in real time.
  • Semi-supervised learning combines a small labelled set with a large unlabelled set. Use it when labelling is expensive but unlabelled data is abundant — a very common scenario in civil engineering.
  • The choice of ML type depends on your data availability, labelling budget, and problem structure. Use the decision flowchart as a practical starting point.
  • Real-world projects often combine multiple paradigms. Unsupervised exploration followed by supervised prediction is a particularly powerful pattern in engineering applications.
